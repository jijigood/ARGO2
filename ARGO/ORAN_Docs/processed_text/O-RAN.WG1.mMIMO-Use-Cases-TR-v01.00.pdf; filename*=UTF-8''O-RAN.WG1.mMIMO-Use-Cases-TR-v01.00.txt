3 GoB / L3 Mobility 1
3.1 Overview 2
Grid of Beam Optimization (GoB) provides an automated beam forming configuration tailored to the topology of the cell, 3
the physical environment, as well as the distribution of users and traffic in a cell (e.g. wide beams might cover low-density 4
areas while narrow beams might cover high-density areas). Beam-based Mobility Robustness Optimization (bMRO) is 5
an autonomous self-optimizing algorithm that improves beam -based inter-cell mobility performance by applying beam -6
specific Cell Individual O ffsets (CIO) on the handover triggers between neighbor cells, based on the analysis of beam - 7
AI/ML assisted network-wide (multi-gNB/TRP) optimizations framework proactively and autonomously i nfers optimal 8
configuration per gNB/TRP for SS Burst Set, DMRS and CSI-RS based on available measurements, observations, and 9
PIs at different nodes of the 3GPP NR and/or O-RAN access and core network elements.  10
 11
3.2 Solution 1: Grid-of-Beams Beamforming (GoB BF) Optimization 12
3.2.1 Problem Statement, Solution and Value Proposition 13
Massive MIMO (mMIMO) is among the key methods to increase performance and QoS in 5G networks. Capacity 14
enhancement is obtained by means of beamforming of the transmitted signals, and by spatially multiplexing data streams. 15
Beamforming can increase the received signal power and simultaneously decrease the interference generated for other 16
users, hence resulting in higher SINR and higher user throughputs. Grid -of-Beams (GoB) with the corresponding beam 17
sweeping has been introduced to allow beamforming of the control channels used during initial access as well as for data 18
transmission and reception, mainly for high frequency (but can be used also for the sub -6 GHz band) MIMO operation. 19
The physical properties of the antenna array and its possible configu rations characterize the span of the beams, namely 20
the horizontal and vertical aperture in which beamforming is supported, and therefore the coverage area and the shape of 21
the cell. mMIMO can be deployed in 5G macrocell clusters as well as in heterogeneous networks, where macro-cells and 22
small cells co-exist and complement each other for better aggregated capacity and coverage. In order to obtain optimal 23
beamforming and cell resources (Tx power, PRB) configuration, one will have to look at a multi-cell environment instead 24
of a single cell. Moreover, different vendors may have different implementations in terms of the number of beams, the 25
horizontal/vertical beam widths, azimuth and elevation range, to achieve the desired coverage. In a multi -node/multi-26
vendor scenario, centralized monitoring and control is required to offer optimal coverage, capacity and mobility 27
performance as well as control over electromagnetic emissions in order to comply with regulatory requirements.  28
The problem associated with traditional mMIMO BF is that its performance is highly dependent on the choice of the BF 29
pattern. Manual configuration is usually based on the empirical knowledge and manual test results of the domain expert(s) 30
and is performed in a semi -static way. That is, (near -)real time contextual, per -site information (such as cell geometry 31
change, user/traffic distribution, mobility patterns, seasonalities etc.) is taken into account in a suboptimal and non -real 32
time way. This may cause one or more of the following problems: 33
1. High inter-cell interference. 34
2. Unbalanced traffic between neighboring cells. 35
3. Low performance at the cell edges or throughout the cell. 36
4. Poor handover performance. 37
 38
This solution proposes a framework that allows the operator to flexibly configure the mMIMO BF parameters in a cell or 39
in a cluster of cells by means of policies and configuration assisted by machine learning (ML) techniques. The 40
configuration optimization relies on contextual information and patterns such as the user distribution, traffic demand 41
distribution, cell geometries, and mobility. 42

3.2.2 Architecture/Deployment Options 1
 Option 1: Non-RT RIC Deployment 2
The Non-RT RIC hosts an rApp application whose task is to determine the suitable GoB  configuration for a cell or a 3
cluster of cells as a function of input metrics and of an operator objective. The underlying ML model is trained on 4
historical data collected from a cell or a cluster of cells and its aim is to determine correlation patterns between mMIMO 5
GoB configurations and performance metrics.  The input data for GoB BF optimization training and inference can be 6
comprised of antenna array parameters, UE mobility/spatial density data, (averaged) traffic density data, timing advance 7
and Angle-of-Arrival measurements (for positioning estimation), power headroom (PH) reports, aggregated and/or pre -8
processed beam -based reference signal measurements (e.g. CSI reporting, covariance matrix),  neighboring cells' 9
beams/interference information, MDT me asurement data, as well as performance measurements such as handover and 10
beam failure statistics. The rApp knows O -RU specifics such as antenna array parameters, O -RU capabilities, beam file 11
format (beamforming configuration update procedure) etc.  12
The output of the inference is the optimized GoB BF configuration, that is, the number of beams and either i) the beam 13
directions, horizontal & vertical beam widths, and power allocation of beams, or ii) the beam weights.  14
Operator objectives regarding adaptive G oB may include desired coverage, defined in terms of the cell geometry (SSB 15
beams), cell capacity requirements (CSI-RS beams), cell edge performance (e.g. minimum throughput per UE), minimum 16
guaranteed throughput (independent of traffic density), weighted average RSRP requirements (e.g., the operator might 17
wish to implement the strategy to cover low -density areas with wide beams and high -density areas with narrow beams), 18
and desired fairness between cell edge and non-edge UEs. 19

3.3 Solution 2 : Beam-based Mobility Robustness Optimization 1
(bMRO) 2
3.3.1 Problem Statement, Solution, and Value Proposition 3
Mobility Robustness Optimization is a well -known SON concept. First supporting measurements have been specified in 4
LTE in Release 9. Its principle is to configure a number of parameters in the eNB that can  delay or advance the HO 5
procedure between two neighboring cells. The two parameters are 1. the cell individual offset (CIO) and 2. the time -to-6
trigger (TTT). One CIO value is a reference signal received power offset specific to one neighbor cell, stored in the eNB.  7
The TTT value is a time offset specific to one neighbor cell, stored in the eNB.  8
By changing the CIO and the TTT, the operator may solve the following problems:  9
1. Unbalanced traffic between neighboring cells. 10
2. Low performance of cell edge users. 11
3. Poor handover performance. 12
 13
In particular, the prime use of optimizing the CIO and the TTT values is reducing the number of anomalous or problematic 14
HO events between neighbor cells. 15
 16
In a 5G mMIMO cell/gNB whose coverage is provided not by a single  beam but by several beams which radiate in 17
different directions. Thus, from the UEs point of view, the border between two neighbor cells is not a single border 18
anymore but degenerates to several beam→cell (or beam→beam) borders, where the one beam is radi ated by the source 19
gNB and another beam is radiated from the target gNB. In this scenario, using a single CIO+TTT value pair on a cell→cell 20
basis will be suboptimal, as one beam→cell (or beam→beam) border might require a different CIO+TTT configuration 21
for optimal HOs than another beam→cell (or beam→beam) border. 22
This solution proposes to employ beam -specific CIO and TTT values between neighbor cells, e.g., instead of a single 23
CIO+TTT value pair for the cell1→cell2 border, to employ a CIO+TTT value pair for  the SourceCell [Beam1] 24
→TargetCell and SourceCell[Beam2] →TargetCell borders. 25
The value of this solution is that the number of anomalous and problematic HOs can be resolved on a beam→cell basis, 26
i.e., with much finer granularity than with the legacy SON cell→cell solution. 27
 28

3.4 Solution 3: AI/ML Based Initial Access ( SS Burst Set), CSI-RS 10
and DMRS Configuration Optimization 11
3.4.1 Problem Statement and Value Proposition 12
3GPP NR based wireless cellular networks promises to provide leaner system design compared to i ts predecessors in a 13
bid to improve spectral efficiency, power consumption performance and reduce interferences. Ultra -lean design aims to 14
minimize “always on” reference signal transmissions in the downlink. Impact is more on Massive MIMO (mMIMO) 15
system and networks where gNB/TRP should transmit downlink reference signals only when necessary. List of “always 16
on” reference signals include s ynchronization signals (SS Burst Sets), CSI -RS TRS, CSI -RS Acquisition, DMRS and 17
system broadcast information. Subsequent sections present AI/ML based optimization problem description with SS Burst 18
Set configuration optimization which operates in slowest c ontrol loop (Non -RT RIC Control Loop) of the O -RAN 19
architecture. Gradually problem statement is extended to CSI -RS and DMRS configuration optimizations operating in 20
Near-RT RIC and O-DU control loops. 21
In beamformed Massive MIMO systems, initial access (IA)  and time frequency tracking (TA) mechanisms require 22
transmission of “Always On” signals SS Burst Sets periodically followed by CSI -RS TRS in the remaining part of the 23
frame stricture. During initial access process, the UE detects a preferred SS/PBCH beam at preferred receive beam. UEs 24
with beam correspondence support can use the same receive beam to transmit PRACH after receiving SIB1 on the 25
scheduled SS/PBCH time -frequency grids. When beam correspondence is not supported, the UE repeats PRACH 26
transmissions in different directions and then listen for the network response using the same beam used to detect the 27
SS/PBCH. From the initial access procedure, a default beam pair is established. After SS burst based synchronization, the 28
UE can start synchronizing with gNB/TRP using SS Block configuration dependent CSI-RS signals.  29
In large scale NR networks with thousands of gNB/TRPs deployed, system configurations derived manually using 30
heuristic methodologies will directly impact the following aspects. 31
a) High power consumptions in both network and UEs leading increased network CAPX and reduced UE battery 32
life respectively and   33
b) Degraded utilization of time-frequency resources affecting e2e spectral efficiency of the system. 34
Issue is compounded by increase in IA laten cy and non -satisfactory reactive tracking performance KPI reports due to 35
non-optimal configuration setting of large numb er of parameters available for SS bursts and CSI -RS TRS configuration 36
supported in NR. UE could observe degraded tracking performances (and related other network KPIs) without appropriate 37
CSI-RS planning. These are important network design aspects for massive MIMO systems with large carrier bandwidth 38
and systems with carrier aggregation support where multiple BWPs are active with multiple time-frequency resource 39
allocations for IA and TA reference signal transmissions. Typically, operators rely on skilled in the art manpower to 40
suggest network wide configuration set which is prone to be sub-optimal. In addition to the demography, time dependent 41
network usage parents have strong influence on designing optimal configuration sets which can lead to spectral and power 42
efficient network design. 43
We propose AI/ML assisted network -wide (multi-gNB/TRP) optimization framework wherein AI/ML agent/engine  can 44
infer optimal SS Burst Set, CSI -RS TRS  configurations per gNB/TRP based on multiple time, location, and usage 45

3.4.3  Feasibility and Gain/Complexity Analysis 11
  Feasibility and Gain Analysis 12
A. Near-RT RIC Deployment Architecture (SS Burst Set Configuration Optimization):  13
This section we present calculated peak SE gain trends with varying SS Burst Configurations, discusses suitability of 14
using AI/ML based optimization techniques for choosing deployment specific, slow time varying optimal SS Bust Set 15
configurations. Also, highlighted tradeoff between choosing lower SS Burst Set configuration and achievable SE gains 16
while meeting initial access latency KPI target set by the operator. 17
Goal of this discussion:  18
One aspect of presenting these results is to highlight the opportunity of significant SE gains when 19
optimal configuration is chosen and applied to the gNB/TRP. Another aspect is to highlight the 20
necessity of applying AI/ML based optimization techniques to these network performance optimization 21
problems.  22
AI/ML based optimization techniques are ideally suitable for 3GPP NR network optimization problems. 23
More and more automations are expected with growing network deployment and optimization 24
complexity. ML based intelligent agents are equipped to handle complex optimizations with trade-offs 25

Impact of SS Burst Set configuration optimization on system overhead resulting in gNB Spectral 5

Presented results are peak spectral efficiency gain trends when different and allowable SS Burst Set 8
configurations (Number of SS Blocks in a SS Bust Set and SS Burst Set periodicity) are chosen. 9
Observed peak spectral efficiency (SE) g ains are the result of system overhead reduction. Job of the 10
AI/ML optimizer is to infer an optimal SS Bust Set configuration based on the target deployment 11
scenario and slow time varying network usage patterns constrained to initial access latency target set 12
by the operator.  13
SE graphs are per gNB Peak SE results (ITU Document 5D/50-E 11 February 2020, 3GPP NR 38.336) 14
for varying SS block number and SS Burst periodicity in 3GPP NR FR1 and FR2 systems with realistic 15
parameters configurations ranges supported by 3GPP specifications for respective frequency bands.  16
One can observe from the presented results below that SE g ain is maximum when lowest SS Burst Set 17
configuration is chosen for the system (within the configuration range supported by 3GPP). However, 18
this would lead to degraded Initial Access (IA) latency KPI. Thus, we need to opt for constrained 19
optimization techn iques to avoid increase in IA latency beyond operator mentioned limit. Another 20
important aspect of the optimizer is to consider slow time varying network usage pattern and time 21
varying UE distribution patterns which can be inferred from the available measurements, observations, 22
PIs. Thus, the optimizer will provide slow time varying SS Burst Set configuration per gNB/TRP which 23
will result in system overhead reduction and hence can achieve optimal SE gain per gNB.  24
AI/ML based constrained optimization techniques are ideally suitable for large-scale (multi-gNB/TRP) 25
and multi-parameter constrained optimization problems and result in proactive and predictable solutions 26
for realistic NR network deployment scenarios. Traditionally one fixed configuration is decided for the 27
network by the skilled manpower and applied during deployment of the network hence results in 28
inefficient network design.        29

Tradeoff between SS Burst Set configuration optimization and SE improvement:  31
Directly selecting the lowest SS Burst Set configuration (without considering the network usage pattern) 32
to reduce reference signaling  overhead will result in reduced SS Block density in both time and 33
frequency directions. This may result in (a) increased initial access latency, (b) reduced mobility support 34
(L3 mobility performance) , (c) inferior time and frequency tracking performance, (d) potentially 35
clustered access to the network  (impacting the L1/2 beam management (for GoB based MIMO 36
schemes), (d) reduced PBCH transmission frequency in SSB a nd hence impact timely availability of 37
the system information blocks.  38
Thus, the important role of an AI/ML optimization techniques is to constrain the SB Block optimization 39
to meet KPI targets set by the operator. Example KPI based constrains are operator defined IA latency 40
target and coverage , target mobility support by the network, beam management KPIs (tracking and 41
acquisition performances). Inferences generated by the AI/ML engine will be usage dependent slow 42
time varying configurations per gNB/TRP. Here optimization techniques should not exclude possibility 43
of joint optimizations example SSB and CSI-RS when required.   44

4 L1 / L2 Beam Management 1
4.1 Overview 2
In 5G NR, mmWave communication using a large bandwidth is one of the key technologies to achieve high-data rate and 3
capacity improvement.  However, mmWave frequencies are suffered from high propagation loss including unexpected 4
signal blockages in a mobile environment. To overcome these challenges, directional -beam transmission enabled by 5
hybrid analogue-digital beamforming with large scale antenna array is typically applied at BS side at least. Under this 6
hybrid beamforming architecture, achieving fine beam alignment betwe en BS and UE beams becomes a prerequisite for 7
successful data transmission and reception. A set of Layer 1 (i.e., physical layer) and Layer 2 (i.e., medium access control 8
layer) procedures to acquire and maintain a set of BS and/or UE beams for DL and UL transmission/reception is referred 9
to as L1/L2 beam management. In 3GPP, the beam management procedures that control the determination and 10
maintenance of the serving beam(s) for each UE within a cell has been firstly specified in Release 15. The O -RAN 11
architecture offer opportunities to improve beam management performance by utilizing Non -RT RIC and /or Near-RT 12
RIC to assist E2 nodes to realize intelligent solution. 13
 14
4.2 Solution 1: AI/ML-assisted Beam Selection Optimization 15
4.2.1 Problem Statement and Value Proposition 16
The L1/L2 beam management procedures that establish and maintain the highly directional transmission link between BS 17
and UE play an important role to enable high quality communications under mmWave frequencies. The current 3GPP 5G 18
NR standard support good flexibility of UE-specific configuration in terms of beam measurement, beam report and beam 19
indication. However, achieving accurate beam alignment between BS and UE is challenging and costly, and a trade -off 20
between network performance improvement and signaling overhead need to be solved. Without employing frequent beam 21
measurement and measurement report , connection between BS and UE may be easily interrupted due to UE movement 22
or blockage, in particular under high mobility scenarios. However, t he number of required resources for frequent beam 23
measurement and measurement report will cause an overhead problem which may decrease the overall network 24
throughput. Without an intelligent solution, the network has to face an either-or situation, either high reliability with large 25
signaling overhead or small signaling overhead with poor reliability. 26
In recent years, application of AI/ML techniques in mobile communication networks has drawn a lot of interest. AI/ML 27
techniques can be also used as a powerful tool which enable RAN to make quicker and smarter decision in the beam 28
management related procedures , which will contribute to improved network performance in terms of throughput and 29
reliability. In particular, how to improve the performance of beam acquis ition and tracking based on the existing 5G NR 30
standard should be considered. AI/ML-assisted solutions can be used to estimate the quality of SSB/CSI-RS beams based 31
on limited beam measurement and/or UE geolocation information, which allows fast beam acquisition and beam failure 32
recovery and ensures reliable radio link against blockage, especially in mmWave.  AI/ML-assisted solutions can also be 33
used to predict a future beam (set), beam switching events or blockage events based on historical measurement reports 34
and/or UE geolocation information. Higher accuracy in beam tracking, lower signaling overhead and low latency in beam 35
switching are essential to guarantee service continuity for UEs. The L1/L2 beam management optimization is particularly 36
relevant for mmWave frequencies but may also be applicable for sub-6GHz frequencies. 37
Potential benefits of AI/ML assisted beam management will include signaling overhead reduction, latency reduction and 38
reliability improvement. 39

5 Non-GoB Beamforming 1
5.1 Overview 2
Non-GoB Beamforming, unlike GoB beamforming, does not rely on predefined beam sets, instead beam wei ghts are 3
determined in real-time at the BS in response to channel measurements, offering the potential for optimum beam patterns 4
to be determined. For downlink, this approach relies on reciprocity in the channel and is therefore primarily aimed at 5
TDD systems. How well the system performs in practice depends on several factors including choice of beamforming 6
algorithm, the channel conditions between BS and UE and the configuration of reference signals. The O-RAN architecture 7
offer opportunities to improve the beamforming performance by utilizing both Non-RT RIC and Near-RT RIC to support 8
intelligent optimization of the Non-GoB beamforming configuration. 9
 10
5.2 Solution 1: AI/ML-assisted non-GoB Optimization 11
5.2.1 Problem Statement and Value Proposition 12
Non- Grid of Beam s (non-GoB) beamforming approaches are an important class of beamforming algorithms  for 5G 13
mMIMO deployments, especially for fully digital, but also potentially for hybrid analog/digital, implementations in sub-14
6GHz frequency bands . Typically Sounding Reference Symbol (SRS) based approaches are used, which rely on 15
uplink/downlink correspondence, where uplink and downlink beam weights are computed “on the fly” in the O-DU / gNB 16
based on channel measurements made using SRS, rather than selecting from a set of predefined beams. The calculated 17
weights are transferred from O-DU / gNB to the O-RU using existing fronthaul mechanisms. SRS based beamforming is 18
particularly attractive for massive MIMO arrays because accurate channel state information can be obtained at the gNB 19
with potentially less overhead in the DL or UL channels. gNB uplink SRS measurements generally have no quantization 20
loss associated with reduction of over the air signaling and are faster availability for downlink adaptation since 21
measurement is done in gNB. On the other hand, SRS measurements might not always be recently available (due to SRS 22
periodicity and/or limited bandwidth), or reliable, especially towards cell edge since the UE transmit power in the uplink 23
is limited. 24
It should be noted that non-GoB algorithms are not standardized, instead these are vendor-specific proprietary algorithms. 25
In addition, multiple algorithm modes or options may be implemented, where some modes may be more suited to 26
particular conditions of the wire less channel , UE location/mobility, interference conditions, or to particular 3GPP 27
configuration options such as SRS periodicity. Examples of beamforming modes include but are not limited to: the use 28
of different SRS channel estimation algorithms; differen t weight calculation approaches (for example matched filter, 29
Eigen, Zero-forcing beamforming); and different time or frequency granularity. 30
Non-GoB beamforming may be applicable to multiple MIMO modes (SIMO, SU-MIMO or MU-MIMO) on the downlink 31
or uplink data channels (PDSCH/PUSCH). GoB beamforming will be used for other channels and may also be used for 32
data channels in some cases where, for example, SRS measurement quality is poor.  33
The problem statement is therefore how a 3rd -party xApp may provide intelligent control over multiple supported non-34
GoB beamforming modes in order to recommend a preferred mode to a gNB / O-DU as a function of channel conditions, 35
UE location, interference conditions, etc. 36

6.1 Overview 3
Massive MIMO holds promise in significantly increasing capacity in practical wireless network environments.  Capacity 4
can depend on the wireless network environment (e.g., user velocity, etc.) and the desired quality of the user wireless 5
experience.  In many practical cases, the capacity of the cell can be increased by, for example, proper choice of parallel  6
user groups, etc. On the other hand, increased number of parallel users can incur increased inter -user interference which 7
can reduce the user quality. In order to properly balance this trade off operators need fundamental observations (aka, 8
“dials”) to assess the network environment and the user quality for both the uplink and the downlink. Further, to optimize 9
the capacity vs. quality for a given cell for a given time and user distribution, the operator needs fundamental parameters 10
to be changed (aka, “knobs”). The following sections will describe three relevant use-cases and list a number of dials and 11
knobs that we believe constitute a fundamental set for observations and manipulation of massive MIMO systems for both 12
Grid of Beams (GoB) and Reciprocity based implementations.   13
To further optimize/automate the complex process, it is proposed that O-RAN interfaces be provided (dials and knobs) to 14
enable a AI/ML model training and update, its inference, and finally the beam optimization to enhance the massive MIMO 15
system performance observe and manipulate the massive MIMO system.  16
 17
6.2 MIMO optimization use-cases 18
The use-cases described in the subsequent sections will show how dials and knobs (specifically referenced) can be used 19
to optimize the three important use cases of downlink transmit power, MIMO pairing enhancement (user separability), 20
and user MIMO mode se lection (Mu -MIMO or Su -MIMO). Each use case will address the proposed solution, value 21
proposition, use of specific dials and knobs, and where applicable the potential gains via analytical studies or simulation.   22
 23
6.2.1 Solution 1: Downlink Transmit power optimization 24
 Problem Statement, Solution and Value Proposition 25
For general downlink precoding, the downlink transmit power is usually evenly distributed across the UEs. However, 26
depending on the UE separability and path loss deltas, this may result in good  cell capacity at the expense of individual 27
UE quality. This can be due to a number of issues such as cell edge UEs having general downlink SINR issues (even 28
without Mu -MIMO), poor UE separability between cell edge UEs, and poor uplink SINR resulting in de graded SRS 29
which are a few example issues. The result of these issues can be manifested by observations of very poor individual UE 30
SINRs (either downlink, uplink, or both) when running in a Mu -MIMO mode. Therefore, although the capacity of the 31
cell has been significantly increased, certain customer experiences may become unacceptable in this Mu -MIMO mode.   32
The solution to the problem described above is to simply provide observations (“dials”) of UE performance in the form 33
of periodic histograms of UE channel quality as well as the overall cell capacity in order to compute an optimal solution 34
via AI/ML with control (knob) of the downlink minimum required SINR threshold to achieve a minimal UE quality 35
requirement that is set by the operator.  The minimum requ ired SINR is a policy and thus doesn’t require real time 36
AI/ML adjustment of transmit power directly but rather leaves this to the scheduler to adjust and optimize consistent with 37
its numerous other priorities and requirements. This adjustment does not impact the normal iBLER (initial BLER) targets, 38
or the CQI/MCS process of adapting the MCS to the UE indicated CQI. Since the received SINR will vary across UEs 39
(see below) and even across PRBs due to frequency selective fading (where narrowband CQI is an opt ion for operators) 40
there is no explicit impact for PRBs. This concept can be extended to the uplink minimum SINR and can improve the 41

 Problem Statement, Solution, and Value Proposition 3
Existing channel orthogonality between multiple users is a key criterion to create us er separability and allow for the 4
opportunity to share radio frequency resources simultaneously. Without so, residual interference will be too high to 5
maintain adequate post pairing radio link signal quality levels required to sustain MU -MIMO mode assignme nts. With 6
mobility there is an added demand to adjust beamforming weight assignments to not only maintain signal power levels at 7
the user end (beam quality), but also to continuously limit the inter user interference experienced between users assigned 8
with the same radio resource allocations. In the absence of creative solutions that capably and in a timely manner respond 9
to the above -described challenges, the 5G massive MIMO deployment will fail to utilize the full capability of large 10
antenna arrays powered by transceivers designed to transmit data channel signals towards a spatially confined direction. 11
Schedulers will also fail to realize potential multiplexing gains as fewer radio resource blocks are shared between users 12
within the same cell, reducing spectral efficiency. 13
Important too is the need to efficiently identify users with low demand for radio resources - sources of bursty traffic. An 14
intelligent assessment of how best such users can be effectively paired, if at all, with other users needs to be p re-15
determined by the radio intelligent controller. Channel estimation mechanisms that can be extrapolated in time and do not 16
risk becoming stale so they can be utilized effectively to generate accurate weights and applied rapidly, is critical. For 17
lower volume buffer data, an unacceptable gap in the above can create failure in MU -MIMO pairing. In summary, this 18
use case suggests various measurement objects (aka dials) that are recommended as input into the AI/ML analytics Apps 19
to optimally determine the outp uts (aka knobs) required to optimize the MU -MIMO feature operation. The following 20
section presents the input and output parameter and possible use in an AI/ML analytics App.  21

Particularly, AI/ML is used to optimize the network capacity utilizing a channel reciprocity -based approach vs. 17
maintenance of the quality of the connection for each user with shared resources. Additionally presented is the approach 18
that involves using a GoB-based solution, similar in objective to the reciprocity one, whereby balance between user quality 19
and capacity realized, is aimed. 20
The dials needed to assess user quality generally consist of measuring the quality (SINR, CQI, etc.) of both the Uplink 21
and Downlink for each user. Other metrics include the Uplink power margin for the user and the user mobility among 22
many other factors (see following sections for a comprehensive list of many of them). Other metrics that provide 23
assessments of capacity include the normal cell volume and cell throughputs along with congestion (active connected 24
users with data in the buffe r). For Massive MIMO the individual “user separability” impacts the MU -MIMO pairing 25
performance (and thus capacity) such that the users cannot be arbitrarily paired or else low user spatial separability can 26
mutually degrade the user link quality due to sig nificant inter-user interference. Thus, user separability must be tracked 27
and used to train the AI/ML component in to optimize the capacity while constrained to insure minimum levels of user 28
quality. The AI/ML component, after training, will output “knobs” to appropriately adjust the capacity to attempt to meet 29
the user volume, throughput, and latency demands. Examples of the knobs for reciprocity are the allocations of Downlink 30
Transmit Power allocation among users (see Section 6.2.1 above), the assignment  of levels of pairing or parallelism, 31
assignment of modes of Su-MIMO vs. Mu-MIMO (see Section 6.2.3 below for more details), etc.   32
An alternative approach to the Reciprocity (mainly for TDD systems) based solution is the GoB approach, which relies 33
upon using the downlink CSI-RS reference signal to estimate the downlink channel (CSI). Here, a quantized model of the 34
CSI is fed back to the base station in order to make proper precoding decisions for the set of MU -MIMO users. This 35
approach can make use of shad ow fading CSI information (type I) that is estimated or even the combination of shadow 36
and fast fading (type II) CSI information that is fed back with, of course, a larger required number of bits.  37
The dials section enumerates a list of the user feedback C SI information that should be monitored to assess the state of 38
the channel and the quality of the channel (CQI, SINR, etc.). In addition, the link quality can also be associated with the 39
choice of multiple downlink beams that are received by the user and t hese need to be properly monitored as well. In 40
addition, various “sub -beam” modalities must be monitored that give more information about the beam characteristics 41
(e.g., beam phase, etc.). In this case, the user beams (vs. the user channels for reciprocity ) need to be assessed for “user 42
separability” as well.   43

assessing the user pairing degree) can be used to ensure user quality.   46

7 Comparison and Conclusions 1
This Technical Report presents the results of the pre-normative phase mMIMO work item. Multiple mMIMO optimization 2
algorithms have been analyzed, incl uding beam-based Mobility Robustness Optimization, Grid of Beam Optimization , 3
Non-Grid of Beam Optimization , L1/L2 Beam Management Optimization , AI/ML assisted optimized SS Burst Set, 4
DMRS and CSI-RS configuration.  5
Beam-based Mobility Robustness Optimization (bMRO) is an autonomous self-optimizing algorithm that improves beam-6
based inter-cell mobility performance by applying beam -specific Cell Individual Offsets (CIO) on the handover triggers 7
between neighbor cells, based on the analysis of beam-specific mobility-related counters. The bMRO algorithm might be 8
hosted in Non-RT RIC or Near-RT RIC. Based in mobility KPIs forwarded to the RIC from the O-CU, the RIC configures 9
the beam-based CIO in the O -DUs. Simulation based evaluation shows a significant reduct ion in too early and too late 10
handovers as well as a reduction of the total UE outage and mobility failure rate, as well as a reduced number of ping -11
pong handovers. The gain was shown for a mixed mobility (UEs with 3 km/h and 30 km/h) scenario in an urban micro 12
deployment according to 3GPP TR38.901. The computational complexity and signaling overhead are a multiple of that 13
of the legacy cell-based MRO where the multiplier is the number of beams that are supported in a cell. Complexity and 14
signaling overhead can be reduced by enhanced proprietary implementations, e.g., forming virtual groups of beams with 15
similar characteristics. bMRO is suggested for normative standardization with the foreseen impact on O -RAN WGs as 16
outlined in Section 3.3.3. There is a dependency on completion of 3GPP Rel.17 for the completion of stage 3 in O-RAN.  17
Grid of Beam Optimization (GoB) provides an automated beam forming configuration tailored to the topology of the cell, 18
the physical environment, as well as the distribution of users and traffic in a cell (e.g. wide beams might cover low-density 19
areas while narrow beams  might cover  high-density areas) . The output of the algorithm are the optimized GoB BF 20
configurations, that are, the number of beams and either i) the beam directions, horizontal & vertical beam widths, and 21
power allocation of beams , or ii) the beam weights, transferred vi a the Open Fronthaul interface. The GoB algorithm 22
might be hosted in Non -RT RIC or Near -RT RIC. First trial results show that the ML based beam pattern optimization 23
algorithm can adapt to the traffic distribution and hence provides a significant gain in terms of weighted downlink RSRP.  24
Uplink throughput enhancements are also expected since SSB beams are used for uplink receive beamforming. The gain 25
was shown for NR TDD at 3.5 GHz with 1 to 20 UEs either stationary or with drive tests in a shopping street s cenario. 26
Different options for input parameters have been identified in the pre -normative phase, which are not supported by 27
existing O-RAN and 3GPP specifications. The output parameters are supported by the existing O -RAN Open Fronthaul 28
specification (i.e. O-FH M-Plane, O-FH CUS-Plane). GoB is suggested for normative standardization with the foreseen 29
impact on O -RAN WGs as outlined in Section 3.2.3, while the preferred option for input parameter still needs to be 30
analyzed and selected. 31
Non-GoB optimization makes use of AI/ML techniques to optimize the beamforming algorithm for each UE, depending 32
on UE and cell conditions. Multiple beamforming algorithm options exist, including both Grid of Beams and Non -Grid 33
of Beams, the latter typically involving beam weights being calculated based on SRS measurements. This itself comprises 34
multiple different algorithm options, which can have differing relative performance depending on UE conditions, cell 35
conditions, SRS configuration, etc. Therefore, it is important that the optimal choice of beamforming algorithm option is 36
applied for each UE. The non-GoB use case takes into account that such beamforming algorithms are generally proprietary 37
and vendor specific. Training is assumed to be cell-based, residing in non-RT RIC, making use of measurements reported 38
from O-DU as well as enrichment data obtained externally, for example related to location and mobility. Inferencing is 39
assuming to be hosted in an xApp in near-RT RIC, based on similar measurements as for training, the output of which is 40
the control of the beamforming modes that should be applied for a given UE. The control may be specified separately for 41
multiple MIMO options (e.g. SU and MU). The update rate is assumed to be 100s of ms, considerably slower than the 42
MAC scheduling update rate. Initial simulation results for a simple SU -MIMO case demonstrate the opportunity for 43
significant performance gains by tailoring beamforming mode to the UE conditions. Per-UE measurement reporting might 44
be specified in 3GPP specifica tions (e.g. 3GPP TS37.320) and referred to in O1/E2 specifications with limited or no 45
impact on O -RAN specifications. Or per -UE measurement reporting might be specified in O -RAN specifications with 46
small impact on WG3 and potentially moderate impact WG5. Non-GoB is suggested for normative standardization with 47
the foreseen impact on O-RAN WGs as outlined in 5.2.3. 48

proactively and autonomously infers optimal configuration per gNB/TRP for SS Burst Set, DMRS and CSI-RS based on 50

Annex A Input and output data and its relation to 3GPP 1
specification 2
One of the objectives of the Massive MIMO pre -normative phase is to analyse and evaluate the set of in put/output 3
parameters for each Massive MIMO use case. Once identified, the impact on O -RAN specification as well as 4
dependencies on 3GPP specifications should be analyzed. This annex provides some background information on how 5
input/output parameters might relate to 3GPP specifications.  6
3GPP defines a very large number of measurements. Among others, these include:  7
• Layer 1 (PHY) UE and gNB measurements    8
o Signal strength and signal quality measurements  9
o TS 38.215 NR; Physical layer measurements 10
• Layer 2 (MAC) UE measurements   11
o Power headroom reporting and buffer status reporting  12
o TS 38.321 NR; Medium Access Control (MAC) protocol specification 13
• Layer 2 (MAC) gNB measurements  14
o Load, delay and packet loss rate measurements   15
o TS 38.314 NR; Layer 2 measurements 16
• Layer 3 (RRC) UE measurements 17
o Mobility measurements and measurement reporting  18
o TS 38.331 NR; Radio Resource Control (RRC); Protocol specification 19
There are many more well -defined measurements often exchanged via peer to peer signalling embedded i n various 20
protocols. Over the generations 3GPP refined its specifications and extended the number of standardized measurements. 21
For instance, the L2 gNB measurements have been specified with the introduction of 3GPP LTE. It can be assumed that 22
most of the measurements as specified in 3GPP are implemented in the UE and at the network side. Besides the accurate 23
specification for implementation, there are also extensive test specifications defined for each measurement, at least for 24
UE measurement reporting.  25
Besides the measurements as such, also means for reporting measurements and other metrics towards the management 26
system are defined. Current 3GPP specifications define different types of metrics. Most relevant specifications in this 27
perspective are:   28
• TS 28.552 specifies 5G Performance Measurements (PMs). These are counters aggregated, e.g., per DU, CU or 29
per core function, for example an AMF Function. An example is the “Number of Active UEs in the DL per cell” 30
which provides information about the mean number of active UEs in a cell. 31
• TS 28.554 specifies 5G end to end Key Performance Indicators (KPIs). These specify further aggregation of the 32
counters defined in TS 28.552. One example is the “maximum registered subscribers of network slice through 33
AMF” (clause 6.2.6) where the subscribers in the AMF that are registered are aggregated per network slice.  34
• TS 37.320 and TS 32.422 specify trace and minimization of drive test (MDT). Trace logs are messages per UE 35
and MDT reports are measurements per UE. The defined MDT measurements for RRC_CONNECTED UEs are 36
for instance RSRP, RSRQ, SINR, Power Headroom, PDCP SDU Data Volume, Average UE throughput, Packet 37
Delay, Packet loss rate etc. Also logging of MDT measurements of RRC_IDLE or RRC_INACTIVE UEs are 38
specified.  39

respective 3GPP specifications and by reusing defined data models such as defined YANG models. Some examples are:  41
