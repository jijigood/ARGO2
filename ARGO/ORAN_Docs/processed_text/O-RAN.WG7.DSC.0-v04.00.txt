1.4 Objectives 47 
Openness is one of the key principles for O-RAN Alliance. To take full advantage of the economies of scale offered by 48 
an open computing platform approach, O-RAN Alliance reference designs will specify high performance, spectral and 49 
energy efficient white-box base station hardware. Reference platforms support a decoupled appro ach and offer detailed 50 
schematics for hardware and software architecture to enable both the  O-DUx and O-RUx. 51 
The promotion of white box hardware is a potential way to reduce the cost of 5G deployment, which will benefit both 52 
the operators and vendors. The objective of the white-box hardware workgroup is to specify and release a complete 53 
reference design, thereby fostering a decoupled software and hardware platform. Currently, there is no open base station 54 
reference design architecture, making it impossible for operators and vendors to develop software for optimizing their 55 
network operation in various application scenarios.  Therefore, it is further envisioned that the group will do research on 56 
all related content to build valuable reference designs.  57

 Base Station Architecture: Background 11 
For any deployment scenarios, the base station architecture falls into three categories: 1) Split Architecture, 2) 12 
Integrated architecture or 3) All-in-one architecture.  The split architecture can be deployed in two ways: 1)  O-CU and 13 
O-DUx are connected via an optional fronthaul multiplexer (FHM) or a switch to multiple O-RUx /optional cascaded O-14 
RUx (See Figure 2.1.1-1 or Figure 2.1.1-2) O-CU and O-DUx are connected via a fronthaul gateway (FHGWx→y) to 15 
multiple O-RUys (See Figure 2.1.1-2).  Note that the term optional is used to indicate there may be a deployment case 16 
where an FHM, or a switch, or a FHGW may not be used for a specific deployment scenario.  The O-CU shall be 17 
located at the data center and the O-DU can be placed either at the data center or at the cell site. O-DUs located at the 18 
cell site can also be placed in a ruggedized cabinet for outdoor deployments. 19 
Depending on how much intelligence one requires to place in O-RUx or O-DUx, there will be a functional split that 20 
splits up functions of PHY layer between O-DUx and O-RUx. O-CU and O-DUx may be designed to be integrated into 21 
one hardware box or as two separate boxes.  For downlink, depending on the deployed architecture, FHM or FHGW 22 
broadcasts the data to all the O-RUxs or O-RUys, respectively. For uplink, it shall combine all the uplink data from 23 
subtending O-RUxs or O-RUys according to the cell sets.  Within working group 7 (and therefore all specifications 24 
stemming from it), all fronthaul interfaces are based on the following three criteria. A fronthaul interface that meets one 25 
of the following criteria can be defined as an open fronthaul interface: 26 
1. O-RAN WG4 (open fronthaul interface group) released interfaces; or 27 
2.  O-RAN approved publicly (e.g., small cell forum or etc.) available external interfaces; or  28 
3. Fronthaul interfaces made available and published as part of an O-RAN approved WG7 reference design. 29

Note that within WG7 and therefore all specifications stemming from it, O-DU7-2x and O-RU7-2x refer to O -DU and O-5 
RU as defined by WG4. 6 
Since in split architecture O-DUx and O-RUx are physically separated from one another by  the FH interface, therefore, 7 
depending on the split option, cert ain functions of base stations may either be performed in the O-DUx or O-RUx. There 8 
are 8 different FH split options available (as defined by 3GPP); however, currently within WG7 the following split options 9 
are considered (as requested by operators for their deployment scenarios): 10 
1. Split option 6: All PHY functions will reside in O-RUx while MAC functions will be performed in O-11 
DUx.  In other words, only un-coded user data is on FH. In this case the terminology O-DU6 and O-12 
RU6 is used. 13 
2. Split option 7-2x, the PHY is split into High and Low PHY functions; where High PHY functions 14 
(coding, rate matching, scrambling, modulations and layer mapping) are  performed in O-DUx while O-15 
RUx performs the Low PHY functions (precoding, remapping, digital beamforming, IFFT and CP 16 
insertion). All I/Q samples are in frequency domain.  In this case the terminology O-DU7-2x and  17 
O-RU7-2x is used. 18 
3. Split Option 8: All PHY functions are performed in O-DUx.  This means that O-RUx function is 19 
limited to RF to baseband conversion and vice versa. The I/Q samples on FH interface are in time-20 
domain.  In this case the terminology O-DU8 and O-RU8 is used. 21 
In some cases, when a FHM/Switch/FHGW is not used, a second O-RU may be cascaded to the first O-RU via the FH 22 
interface. In this way, the coverage area may be further expanded. The FH interface between the two cascaded O-RUs 23 
shall be the same as the FH interface between the O-DU and the first O-RU. For downlink, the first O-RU copies the 24 
data received from the O-DU, then sends it to the second O-RU. For uplink, the first O-RU combines the uplink data 25 
from the second O-RU, and then sends it to the O-DU.  26
