1.3.1 Definitions 14
For the purposes of the present document, the terms and definitions given in 3GPP TR 21.905 [1], O-RAN WG2 15
specifications [2,3] and the following apply. A term defined in the present document takes precedence over the 16
definition of the same term, if any, in 3GPP TR 21.905 [1]. 17
Terminology related to policies are based on ETSI GR ENI 004 [4] with O-RAN specific amendments added as notes: 18
policy: set of rules that are used to manage and control the changing and/or maintaining of the state of one or more 19
managed objects  20
imperative policy: type of policy that uses statements to explicitly change the state of a set of targeted objects  21
NOTE 1: The order of statements that make up the policy is explicitly defined.   22
NOTE 2: In the present document, Imperative Policy will refer to policies that are made up of Events, Conditions, 23
and Actions.  24
NOTE 3: Imperative policies that are made up of Events, Conditions, and Actions are not applicable to the A1  25
interface in O-RAN. 26
declarative policy: type of policy that uses statements to express the goals of the policy, but not how to accomplish those 27
goals  28
NOTE 1: State is not explicitly manipulated, and the order of statements that make up the policy is irrelevant.  29
NOTE 2: Declarative policies are further categorized as: 30
policy objectives – statements with the objective to reach the goal of the policy 31
policy resources – statements expressing the use of resources 32
NOTE 3: Declarative policies are applicable to the A1 interface in O-RAN. 33
intent policy: a higher -level declarative policy, typically expressed in a simplified form (for example using natural 34
language). 35
NOTE 1: Each statement in an Intent Policy may require the translation of one or more of its terms to a form that 36
another managed functional entity can understand.  37
NOTE 2: Intent policies are not applicable to A1 interface. 38
RAN intent: Expression of high-level operational or business goals to be achieved by the radio access network, 39
allowing an operator to specify the desired SLAs that RAN needs to fulfill for all or a class of users in a given area over 40
a period of time. 41
NOTE: RAN intent is not in A1 scope 42

2.1.2 Role of A1 in the O-RAN Architecture 11
The Non-RT RIC function resides in the Service Management and Orchestration (SMO) layer of the O-RAN OAM 12
logical architecture as described in O-RAN WG1: “Operations and Maintenance Architecture” [5] that also handles 13
deployment and configuration as well as data collection of e.g. RAN observables.  14
In the SMO layer there also resides functions that handle AI/ML workflow, e.g. train ing and update of ML Models, as 15
well as functions for deployment of ML models and other applications as described in O-RAN WG2: AI/ML workflow 16
description and requirements [3]. 17
The SMO Layer may also have access to data other than that available in the RAN  network functions and this 18
enrichment information can be used to enhance the RAN guidance and optimization functions.  19
One purpose of the SMO layer is to optimize the RAN performance towards fulfilment of the SLAs in the RAN intent. 20
The purpose of the A1 interface is to enable the Non-RT RIC function to provide policy-based guidance, ML model 21
management and enrichment information to the Near-RT RIC function so that the RAN can optimize e.g. RRM under 22
certain conditions. 23

2.1.3 A1 service architecture 6
2.1.3.1 A1 Policy Management Service 7
Based on high level goals for the system expressed in RAN Intent, and on observables (events and counters) provided 8
over O1, the Non-RT RIC defines policies that are provided to the Near-RT RIC over the A1 interface. The purpose of 9
the A1 policies is to guide the RAN performance towards the overall goal expressed in RAN Intent. The A1 policies are 10
declarative policies that contain statements on policy objectives and policy resources applicable to UEs and cells. 11
The Non-RT RIC manages the A1 policies based on A1 policy feedback, and on the network status provided over O1. 12
The Non-RT RIC uses the O1 observables to continuously evaluate the impact of the A1 policies towards fulfillment of 13
the RAN Intent and will based on internal conditions decide to issue/update the goals expressed in the A1 policies. 14
The Near-RT RIC functions based on its internal functions or applications, the configuration received over O1 and the  15
temporary policies received over A1. The A1 policies do not survive a re-start of the Near-RT RIC.  16
To support the policy enforcement in the Near-RT RIC, the Non-RT RIC can provide enrichment information over the 17
A1 interface. 18
2.1.3.2 A1 Enrichment Information Service 19
Enrichment information is generically defined as information provided to an entity, in addition to the information that is  20
generally available to it, in order to enhance the performance of its tasks. It can be produced based on information from 21
one or more sources, e.g. by relaying, combining, refining or analyzing information from the input source(s).  22
In the O-RAN architecture there are ‘O-RAN internal information sources’ and ‘O-RAN external information sources’. 23
The network functions in the RAN are examples of O-RAN internal information sources. 24
SMO collects information from O-RAN internal and O-RAN external information sources. Based on this information, 25
Non-RT RIC can derive information that could be beneficial for both Non-RT RIC and Near-RT RIC internal functions 26
or applications, e.g. an ML model, to improve its performance. 27

2.1.3.3 A1 ML Model Management Service 11
ML models can be trained and executed in different places in the O-RAN architecture. Two scenarios described in O-12
RAN WG2: “AI/ML workflow description and requirements” [3] that involves the Non-RT RIC are: 13
Scenario 1.1: ML model is trained in the SMO layer and then used by the Non-RT RIC to improve the monitoring 14
and guidance of the RAN based on the O1 observability. The training and the deployment of the ML Model is 15
handled by SMO internal functions. The training data is the same as the data the Non-RT RIC will use for inference, 16
i.e. the O1 observability. 17
Scenario 1.2: ML model is trained in the SMO layer and then used by th e Near-RT RIC to improve the optimization 18
of the RAN. The training of the ML Model is handled by SMO internal functions. The input data for training is the 19
same as the input data the Near-RT RIC will use for inference. The trained ML Model is deployed by t he SMO layer 20
via the O1 interface. The performance evaluation resulting from the execution of the ML model, and the handling of 21
explicit feedback from the ML model itself, is based on O1 observability. 22
To support the ML model inference in the Near-RT RIC, the Non-RT RIC can provide enrichment information over the 23
A1 interface.  24
2.2 A1 interface general principles 25
The general principles for the specification of the A1 interface are as follows: 26
- the A1 interface is an open logical interface within O-RAN architecture between the Non-RT RIC functionality 27
in the SMO and the Near-RT RIC functionality in the RAN; 28
-  the A1 interface enables a multi-vendor environment and is independent of specific implementations of the 29
SMO, Non-RT RIC, and Near-RT RIC; 30
-  the A1 interface is defined in an extensible way that enables new services and data types to be added without 31
needing to change the protocol or the procedures; 32
-  the A1 interface enables policy-based guidance (objective, resource) of radio resource management internal 33
functions or applications that are part of Near-RT RIC; 34
-  the A1 interface can provide basic feedback mechanism from Near-RT RIC that enables Non-RT RIC to monitor 35
the use of policies; 36
-  the policy enforcement service in Near-RT RIC requires that the policies transferred over A1 interface are 37
expressed using a standardized mechanism (language, syntax, …); 38
-  the A1 interface enables transmission of A1 enrichment information from Non-RT RIC to Near-RT RIC as 39
needed by Near-RT RIC; 40
-  the A1 interface enables Near-RT to discover available enrichment information for which secure delivery can be 41
provided, and to request delivery of enrichment information; 42
-  A1 policies are created, modified and deleted by the Non-RT RIC; 43

2.3 A1 interface specification objectives 8
The A1 interface specification facilitates the following: 9
- inter-connection of Non-RT RIC functionality in the SMO with Near-RT RIC functionality in the Radio Access 10
Network supplied by different manufacturers; 11
-  provide policies for individual UEs or groups of UEs; 12
- provide basic feedback on policy state from Near-RT RIC that enables Non-RT RIC to monitor the use of 13
policies; 14
-  provide enrichment information as required by Near-RT RIC. 15
2.4 A1 interface capabilities 16
As described in O-RAN WG2: “Non-RT RIC & A1 Interface: Use Cases and Requirements” [2], the A1 interface 17
supports: 18
- Transfer of policy management information from Non-RT RIC to Near-RT RIC; 19
- Policy feedback from Near-RT RIC to Non-RT RIC; 20
- Discovery and request of A1 Enrichment Information from Near-RT RIC to Non-RT RIC and delivery of A1 21
Enrichment Information from Non-RT RIC to Near-RT RIC. 22
Chapter 3 Functions of the A1 interface 23
3.1 Policy management 24
3.1.1 Introduction 25
The purpose of A1 policies is to enable the Non-RT RIC function in the SMO to guide the Near-RT RIC function, and 26
hence the RAN, towards a better fulfilment of the RAN intent. 27
NOTE: RAN Intent represents e.g. the SLA from the BSS (Business Support System) that the RAN is to fulfil for 28
all users or for a subset of users (e.g. QCI or slice). Based on the permanent configuration and the available 29
resources, the RAN strives to deliver the expected QoS for the users. 30
By utilizing the observability over O1, and the A1 policy feedback, the Non-RT RIC can conclude that the RAN Intent 31
is not achieved. The Non-RT RIC can then decide to use A1 policies that enable the Near-RT RIC to e.g. optimize 32
RRM for a single UEs or for group of UEs. 33
E.g.: When the Non-RT RIC understands that the available resources in a certain area are not enough to fulfil the 34
SLA for all users in a slice, it can then decide to temporarily change the QoS targets for some users (dynamic group 35
of users) belonging to the same slice (pre-defined group of users). 36

There are different types of A1 policies referred to as policy types. A Non-RT RIC need not use all policy types, and a 1
specific function in the Near-RT RIC may only support one specific policy type. Non-RT RIC can discover available 2
policy types over A1. 3
Note: as policy types represent capabilities of the Near-RT RIC they may also be indicated to the Non-RT RIC in 4
other ways. 5
3.1.2 The policy management function 6
The policy management function is used by the Non-RT RIC to provision and manage A1 policies that are enforced by 7
the Near-RT RIC. The Non-RT RIC is responsible for definition and administration of A1 policies. 8
The function is used to create, update and delete A1 policies in the Near-RT RIC. 9
The function is used to query the presence, content and run-time status of an A1 policy in the Near-RT RIC.  10
The function is supported by A1 policy feedback from the Near-RT RIC to the Non-RT RIC.  11
NOTE 1: As the Near-RT RIC cannot change the content of a policy, only a change in enforcement status can be 12
notified. If context is changed in such a way that the enforcement status of a policy is changed, the Non-RT RIC is 13
notified and may decide to delete the policy. 14
NOTE 2: Feedback on the fulfilment of A1 policies in the Near-RT RIC is not transferred over the A1 interface. 15
The Non-RT RIC can estimate the fulfilment of A1 policies based on O1 observables. 16
NOTE 3: Before and after the creation of an A1 policy, the Non-RT RIC will monitor the network to understand the 17
effect of the A1 policy on performance. Performance monitoring or tracing is not handled over the A1 interface.  18
3.1.3 Life cycle aspects of A1 policies 19
Per default, the Near-RT RIC controls the RAN based on the persistent configuration. The RAN operation can be 20
optimized using A1 policies that, compared to the persistent configuration,  21
- is not critical to traffic; 22
- have temporary validity; 23
- handle individual UE or dynamically defined groups of UEs; 24
- acts within and takes precedence over the configuration; 25
- is non-persistent, i.e. does not survive a restart of the Near-RT RIC. 26

PolicyTypeIds. Based on the PolicyTypeId, schemas are identified and used for creation, validation and formulation, 20
and for query of the status, of A1 policies of that type.  21
An A1 policy is identified by a policy identifier (PolicyId) that is assigned by Non-RT RIC. The PolicyId is locally 22
unique within Non-RT RIC and sent in the policy request operations that carry representations of A1 policies.  23
A1 policies consist of a scope identifier and one or more policy statements. The scope identifier represents what the 24
policy statements are to be applied on (e.g. UEs, QoS flows, or cells). The policy statements represent the goals to the 25
Near-RT RIC and covers policy objectives and policy resources. 26

3.2 A1 Enrichment Information 9
3.2.1 Introduction 10
The purpose of A1 Enrichment Information is to enable the Near-RT RIC to improve its RAN optimization 11
performance by utilizing information that is not available within the RAN. The information sources can be O -RAN 12
internal and O-RAN external, and the derived A1 Enrichment Information is provided by the Non-RT RIC over the A1 13
interface. 14
There are different types of A1 Enrichment Information referred to as EI types. A Near-RT RIC may not need to use all 15
EI types, and a specific function in the Near-RT RIC may only need one specific type of A1 Enrichment Information. 16
An EI type is identified by the EI Type Identifier (EiTypeId). Based on the EiTypeId, information is provided about the 17
A1 Enrichment Information properties and how to request delivery of the A1 Enrichment Information.  18
The Near-RT RIC can discover available EiTypeIds over A1 and request delivery of A1 Enrichment Information related 19
to an available EiTypeId. The Non-RT RIC controls the access to A1 Enrichment Information and how the connection 20
for delivery of the enrichment information is made. 21
NOTE: There may be functions in the SMO or the Non-RT RIC that also utilize enrichment information for their 22
tasks, it may be the same or different enrichment information as exposed over the A1 interface. This is outside the 23
scope of this specification to describe. 24
3.2.2 The enrichment information function 25
The enrichment information function is used by the Non-RT RIC to produce and make A1 Enrichment Information 26
available to the Near-RT RIC. The Non-RT RIC is responsible for exposure and secure delivery of A1 Enrichment 27
Information. 28
The function is used to publish available EiTypeIds over A1. 29
The function is used to deliver A1 Enrichment Information for an EiTypeId based on an EI job. 30
3.2.3 Life cycle aspects of A1 Enrichment Information 31
3.2.3.1 Registration of enrichment information source 32
Before the Non-RT RIC can provide a certain EiTypeId over A1, the SMO needs to set-up the collection of input 33
information from O-RAN internal and/or external sources and the Non-RT RIC needs to set-up the functions that will 34
produce the enrichment information based on the input information and deliver it. When onboarding and set-up is 35
complete, the new EiTypeId can be discovered over A1. 36

3.2.3.3 Request and Delivery of enrichment information 14
A1 Enrichment Information can be delivered to the Near-RT RIC in different ways depending on the type of the A1 15
Enrichment Information or on the needs from the using internal function or application.  16
A1 Enrichment Information can be delivered on an as per needed basis, due to an event or continuously. For continuous 17
delivery, different mechanisms can be used depending on the volume of enrichment information to be delivered.  18
Following the discovery that an EiTypeId is available, the request and delivery of A1 Enrichment Information is done in 19
two steps: 20
First the Near-RT RIC requests over A1 to get A1 Enrichment Information for the EiTypeId by creating an EI Job 21
that contains a description of the information that is requested and which delivery mechanism to use.  22
Then the Non-RT RIC sets up the appropriate connection and delivers A1 Enrichment Information according to the 23
agreed details for the EI Job. 24
3.2.3.3.1 Life cycle aspects of EI Jobs 25
The Near-RT RIC is responsible for the creation, update and deletion of EI Jobs. The EI J ob is created when the Near-26
RT RIC needs A1 Enrichment Information of a specific EI Type and the EI Job is deleted when the Near-RT RIC no 27
longer needs the information. 28
In case the Near-RT RIC temporarily cannot use delivered EI, it can discard or buffer the received EI or stop the EI 29
delivery by deleting the EI Job and re-create it when it is ready to receive delivered EI. 30
The Near-RT RIC is responsible for querying existing EI Jobs after its re-start, and either delete, update or keep the EI 31
Jobs if still relevant. 32
3.2.3.3.2 Life cycle aspects of EI Results 33
The Non-RT RIC is responsible for the set-up of connections to the Near-RT RIC for the delivery of EI Results 34
according to the agreed EI Job. The delivery of the EI Results is started when the EI Job is  created and stopped when 35
the EI Job is deleted. 36
In case delivery connections cannot be set-up to the Near-RT RIC, the Non-RT RIC need not buffer any information 37
produced while the delivery connections are not successful. In case the EI cannot be successfu lly produced or delivered 38
according to an agreed EI Job, the Near-RT RIC will detect this through the delivered EI results, or through lack of 39
delivered EI results. 40
The Near-RT RIC would be responsible for evaluating the received EI results. If the receive d EI Results are not 41
according to expectations, the Near-RT RIC will need to update the EI Job or delete it.  42
