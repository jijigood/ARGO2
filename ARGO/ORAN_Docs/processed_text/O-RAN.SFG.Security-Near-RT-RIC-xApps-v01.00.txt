Introduction 1 
The Near-Real Time RAN Intelligent Controller (Near-RT RIC) is a new virtualized function that adds RAN 2 
programmability to existing or new RAN networks, e.g., self-organizing networks (SON) type of functions. It is a 3 
platform designed to use AI/ML-based tools with open interfaces to the RAN (E2) and the management and 4 
orchestration system (A1, O1).  5 
 6 
The Near-RT RIC enables near real time control and optimization of RAN elements and resources via fine-grained 7 
data (e.g., UE basis, Cell basis) collection and actions over E2 interface, and includes open interfaces (A1, O1) to the 8 
management and orchestration system (SMO). 9 
 10 
The Near-RT RIC platform is a software based near – real-time micro - service-based platform for hosting micro 11 
service-based applications - the xApps - that run on the Near-RT RIC platform. A xApp may consist of one or more 12 
microservices and at the point of onboarding it identifies which data it consumes and which data it provides. It is 13 
independent of Near-RT RIC and may be provided by any third party. The E2 interface enables a direct association 14 
between xApp and RAN functionalities.   15

The present document considers the security aspects of the Near-RT RIC platform and xApps, as well as the 2 
associated network and management interfaces (E2, A1, O1) and APIs.  3 
Taking into consideration the threats and risks identified in O-RAN Threat Model specification [1], the study analyzes 4 
a list of key issues in the Near-RT RIC overall architecture and proposes solutions in order to derive security 5 
requirements, intended to be part of O-RAN Security Requirements Specifications [2]. 6

3 Definitions and abbreviations 1 
3.1 Definitions 2 
For the purposes of the present document, the following terms and definitions apply. 3 
Near-RT RIC: O-RAN Near-Real-Time RAN Intelligent Controller: A logical function that enables near-real -time 4 
control and optimization of RAN elements and resources via fine-grained data collection and actions over E2 interface. 5 
Non-RT RIC: O-RAN Non-Real-Time RAN Intelligent Controller: A logical function within SMO that drives the 6 
content carried across the A1 interface . It is comprised of the Non-RT RIC Framework and the Non-RT RIC 7 
Applications.  8 
O-CU-CP: O-RAN Central Unit – Control Plane: a logical node hosting RRC and the control plane part of PDCP 9 
protocol. 10 
O-CU-UP: O-RAN Central Unit – User Plane: a logical node hosting the user plane part of PDCP protocol and SDAP 11 
protocol. 12 
O-DU: O-RAN Distributed Unit: a logical node hosting RLC/MAC/High-PHY layers based on a lower layer functional 13 
split. 14 
O-RU: O-RAN Radio Unit: a logical node hosting Low-PHY layer and RF processing based on a lower layer functional 15 
split.  This is similar to 3GPP’s “TRP” or “RRH” but more specific in including the Low-PHY layer (FFT/iFFT, PRACH 16 
extraction).   17 
O-eNB: An eNB or ng-eNB that supports E2 interface. 18 
O1: An interface between SMO and O -RAN managed elements, for operation and management, by which FCAPS 19 
management, PNF (Physical Network Function) Software management, File management shall be achieved.  20 
SMO: A Service Management and Orchestration system. 21 
A1: An interface between Non- RT RIC and Near -RT RIC to enable policy-driven guidance of Near- RT RIC 22 
applications/functions, and support AI/ML workflow. 23 
E2: An interface connecting Near-RT RIC and one or more O-CU-CPs, one or more O-CU-UPs, or one or more O-DUs. 24 
E2 Node: A logical node terminating E2 interface. In this version of the specification, O -RAN nodes terminating E2 25 
interface are: 26 
- for NR access: O-CU-CP, O-CU-UP, O-DU  27 
- for E-UTRA access: O-eNB. 28 
xApp: An application designed to run on Near-RT RIC. It may consist of one or more microservices and at the point of 29 
on-boarding it identifies which data it consumes and which data it provides. It is independent of Near-RT RIC and may 30 
be provided by any third party. The E2 interface enables a direct association between xApp and RAN functionalities.   31 
O-Cloud: O-Cloud is a cloud computing platform comprising a collection of physical infrastructure nodes that meet O-32 
RAN requirements to host the relevant O-RAN functions (such as Near-RT RIC, O-CU-CP, O-CU-UP, and O-DU), the 33 
supporting software components (such as Operating System, Virtual Machine Monitor, Container Runtime, etc.) and 34 
the appropriate management and orchestration functions. 35 
Solution Provider: An application developer who delivers applications to Service Providers. [22] 36 
Service Provider: A network provider who is planning to deploy applications into their network. [22] 37

4 RIC background, concepts and assumptions 1 
4.1 Introduction  2 
The RIC architecture consists of the Non-RT RIC, which deals with control loops of more than 1 second, and the 3 
Near-RT RIC, with control loops in the order of 10 ms to 1s [3]. The Non-RT RIC is embedded in the Service and 4 
Management Orchestration function and hosts rApps to provide policy-based guidance, machine learning model 5 
management and enrichment information to the Near-RT RIC function for the purpose of RAN optimization [4]. The 6 
Near-RT RIC is a logical function that enables near real-time control and optimization of E2 Nodes functions and 7 
resources, steered via the policies and enrichment data provided from the Non-RT RIC [3]. 8 
4.2 Near-RT RIC architecture 9 
The following diagram shows the building blocks of the Near-RT RIC internal architecture. For more detailed 10 
descriptions of the architecture and platform functions, see WG3 Near-RT RIC Architecture specification [3]. 11

4.4 xApps 20 
As per definition in [3], an xApp is an application designed to run on Near-RT RIC. It may consist of one or more 21 
microservices and at the point of on-boarding it identifies which data it consumes and which data it provides. It is 22 
independent of Near-RT RIC and may be provided by any third party. The E2 interface enables a direct association 23 
between xApp and RAN functionalities.  24

5.5 Key Issue #4: Isolation between xApps 10 
5.5.1 Key issue detail 11 
The Near-RT RIC enables near real time control and optimization of RAN elements and resources via fine-grained 12 
data collection and actions over E2 interface, and includes open interfaces (A1, O1) to the management and 13 
orchestration system (SMO). This will be achieved using a collection of xApps, with each xApp providing a specific 14 
functionality by consuming a specific subset of data or producing data for other xApps or Near-RT RIC platform. To 15 
provide its desired functionality, the xApps would interact with other Near-RT RIC platform components. 16 
In a multi-vendor environment, where 3rd party xApps and those provided by the RIC vendor are deployed together, 17 
an xApp can be used to impact the other co-located xApps/Near-RT RIC platform thus creating a noisy neighbour 18 
effect where an xApp can overconsume the underlying shared resources provided by the Near-RT RIC platform like 19 
CPU, memory, I/O bandwidth, etc. and can negatively affect the performance of other co-located xApps and 20 
potentially other CNFs sharing the same underlying platform.  21 
It’s also possible that xApps have different levels of access to the data and resources available on Near-RT RIC. 22 
Appropriate isolation is needed for xAPPs that have different threat profiles. 23 
 24 
5.5.2 Security threats 25

- overconsume the platform resources shared by all xApps, creating a noisy neighbour effect by leaving other 27 
co-located xApps with a resource deficit. 28

- administer DoS attacks on the Near-RT RIC platform components, impacting the availability of the Near-RT 30 
RIC  31 
- administer unauthorised access to sensitive real-time information from RAN, persistent configuration used 32 
by the Near-RT RIC to control the RAN  33

5.10 Key Issue #9: xApp exploitation via malicious A1 policies 12 
5.10.1 Key issue detail 13 
The Non-RT RIC defines policies that are provided to the Near-RT RIC over the A1 interface. The purpose of the A1 14 
policies is to guide and optimize the RAN, and the policies inform behavior of xApps in the Near-RT RIC. [9] 15 
Unauthorized access to the Non-RT RIC enables the creation of ‘false policies’ that can be issued to the Near-RT RIC 16 
for enforcement. Existing Near-RT RIC policies could also be modified to achieve a false policy. False policies 17 
passed to the Near-RT RIC would be persistent until they were modified or deleted by the Non-RT RIC or the Near-18 
RT RIC power cycles. 19 
5.10.2 Security threats 20 
False policies can be created to have numerous impacts to the normal performance of the RAN. A single false A1 21 
policy can target a specific UE, groups of UEs, or an entire cell. A false policy could influence the Near-RT RIC to 22 
configure the O-DU and O-RU functions to support Denial of Service (DoS) attacks by simply using feedback data to 23 
degrade RAN performance.  24 
False policies could also be used for the purpose of locating a subscriber or group of subscribers. In this case, the false 25 
policy would cause the Near-RT RIC to isolate a subscriber in the O-CU. The Near-RT RIC could also use MIMO 26 
beamforming in the O-DU and O-RU to isolate a user onto a single beam. The data feedback from the RAN can 27 
include UE location or trajectory information from GPS data. The subscriber location would be attained from access 28 
to the Non-RT RIC in the SMO function. 29 
The Near-RT RIC is capable of steering traffic to achieve optimal QoS or QoE performance. A false policy could 30 
notionally cause the Near-RT RIC to steer user data to isolate the data in order to facilitate a cyber-attack. 31

Security protections are defined for the A1 interface and security work is in process for A1 related APIs and the Non-33 
RT RIC. For example, A1 peering should be established with only trusted sources using TLS 1.2, or higher, and PKI 34 
X.509 certificates, as specified in [19].  TLS also provides confidentiality and integrity protection. Additional Near-35 
RT RIC authentication of the A1 policy may be a consideration later, after the above security protections are defined 36 
and evaluated. 37 
A perimeter security approach may enable an internal threat actor to conduct a MitM attack on the A1 interface.  38 
Security for the Near-RT should take a zero-trust approach in which security controls are implemented at the Near-RT 39 
RIC without assumption of a secure perimeter. The Near-RT RIC could implement security controls consistent with a 40 
zero-trust architecture in which there is no assumption of perimeter defenses being provided by other O-RAN 41

5.11 Key Issue #10: Near-RT RIC APIs authentication 1 
5.11.1 Key issue detail 2 
Like any software, APIs can be compromised, and data can be intercepted. APIs can be manipulated causing service 3 
disruptions and can be subject as well to DoS attacks. Authentication, an essential part of secure communication, 4 
ensures that an attribute claimed by a given entity (e.g., its identity) is actually correct. In the context of secure 5 
communication between xApps and Near-RT RIC platform, the platform shall authenticate all xApps instances and 6 
only provide them with the information and services for which they are authorized through the use of the Near-RT 7 
RIC APIs, as a key objective to guarantee the validity of the transferred messages. xApps can also act as service 8 
producers, exposing APIs to be consumed by other xApps for example. In that case, the xApps ‘producers’ shall 9 
authenticate the instances interested in consuming those xApp services. Authentication is also a prerequisite for 10 
conducting authorization which itself is another key issue for API Security.  11 
Note that using the xApp registration procedure Near-RT platform performs authentication and validation of the 12 
xApp, addressed in solution #1, however xApps, as consumers of the services exposed by Near-RT RIC APIs, are not 13 
authenticated as per current O-RAN specifications at the time of requesting those services.    14 
5.11.2 Security threats 15 
Not mutually authenticating xApps and Near-RT RIC platform APIs could potentially allow attackers to perform the 16 
following type of attacks [17]: 17 
- Operating malicious xApp claiming to be genuine in order to request certain services (theft of services) or 18 
information (data leakage) 19 
- Man in the middle attacks between a genuine xApp and a Near-RT RIC platform API 20 
- Querying network or UE information from a compromised xApp to Near-RT RIC platform (e.g. database via 21 
SDL API), thereby leaking potentially sensitive data about network and/or UE (potential privacy issues)  22 
- Subscribing a malicious xApp to services provided by the Near-RT RIC platform, such as API-related events 23 
notifications, discovery of APIs, E2SM, etc.  24 
The use of weak credentials in the process of API authentication can compromise the overall system. The 25 
user/password combination isn’t considered safe, not only for password related attacks (e.g., brute-force), but also it 26 
would represent a high risk to allow xApps, especially 3rd party xApps, to store the user/password combo. This 27 
approach would extend the attack surface into xApps side.    28 
As a reference, OWASP API Security Top 10 report [10] indicates that authentication mechanisms are often 29 
implemented incorrectly, allowing attackers to compromise authentication tokens or to exploit implementation flaws 30 
to assume other user's identities temporarily or permanently. Compromising system's ability to identify the client/user, 31 
compromises API security overall. 32 
5.11.3 Potential security requirements 33 
The communication between xApps and Near-RT RIC platform APIs shall be authenticated at least at the transport 34 
layer (for e.g., with mutual TLS). 35 
At application layer complementary end to end authentication mechanisms based on JSON Web Tokens (JWTs) may 36 
be considered as well.  37

6.4 Solution #3: Authentication schema for APIs 1 
6.4.1 Introduction 2 
REST APIs and gRPC APIs are being considered as valid network solutions for Near-RT RIC API. REST APIs use 3 
HTTP and support Transport Layer Security (TLS). REST APIs utilizes JavaScript Object Notation (JSON) as 4 
encoding protocol. gRPC APIs use HTTP/2 and support as well TLS. In contrast with REST APIs, gRPC used 5 
Protobuf as encoding protocol.  6 
Time critical E2 related APIs are currently specified to run over SCTP/Protobuf network protocol stack. The security 7 
network layer specified for E2 interface is IPSec (SEC-CTL-E2 [2]). DTLS is proposed as optional mechanism. 8 
The solution proposes a basic network authentication schema for transactional APIs (REST and gRPC) based on 9 
mutual TLS (mTLS) authentication via X.509 certificates. The use of mTLS serves a dual purpose: 10 
- Mutual authentication 11 
- Confidentiality protection for the authorization framework (solution #4) 12 
mTLS is fully aligned with Zero Trust principles of O-RAN security architecture. 13 
Additionally, the network authentication schema can be complemented by a client authentication schema at 14 
application layer, with the use of secure tokens (e.g. JWT, secured with digital signatures based on JWS). 15 
Authentication is a prerequisite of the authorization framework, applicable to those APIs. 16 
For time critical E2 related APIs, secured by IPSec, the solution proposes IKEv2 certificate-based authentication 17 
implementation according to O-RAN Security Protocols specification [19], which is aligned with 3GPP TS 33.310 18 
specification [20] in terms of certificates and IKEv2 profiles.  19 
 20 
6.4.2 Solution details 21 
6.4.2.1 mTLS authentication 22 
In mTLS both the client (e.g, xApp as API consumer) and the server (e.g, platform as API producer) require a 23 
certificate, and both sides authenticate each other using their public/private key pair. The authentication process 24 
includes the following steps: 25 
- the client connects to server and requests access 26 
- the server presents a server certificate 27 
- the client verifies the server certificate 28 
- the client presents a client certificate 29 
- the server verifies the client certificate 30 
- the server grants access 31 
- client and server can exchange information over encrypted TLS connection 32

certificate by the producer 39

6.12 Solution #11: Mitigation for conflicting xApps 5 
6.12.1 Introduction 6 
WG3 has already defined a process to seek guidance from the Near-RT RIC Conflict Mitigation function prior to 7 
initiating a RIC function procedure. The purpose of the Conflict Mitigation function is to resolve potentially 8 
overlapping or conflicting request from multiple xApps. These are currently optional procedures [3].  9 
This guidance can be initiated by 4 different actors at different points in the life cycle of an xApp:  10 
- Initiated by an authorized xApp  11 
- Initiated by Subscription Management function  12 
- Initiated by message monitoring by the Conflict Mitigation function 13 
- Initiated by Conflict Mitigation function 14 
For these procedures, the assumption from WG3 is that the Conflict Mitigation function has access to sufficient 15 
information to detect a potential conflict and take a decision on an optimal mitigation solution [3]. 16 
6.12.2 Solution details 17 
This solution proposes to make the first procedure, Conflict Mitigation guidance initiated by an authorized xApp, 18 
mandatory during the E2 Subscription procedure, which is performed at the end of xApp Registration. The E2 19 
Subscription procedure is detailed in [3] in Section 9.3.2.1, with the xApp initiated E2 Guidance Request procedure 20 
described in Section 9.3.3.1 [3]. 21 
Performing this guidance request during xApp Registration would mitigate conflicts arising from newly deployed 22 
xApps. Additional consideration is needed for conflicts arising after deployment, once additional Conflict Mitigation 23 
messages and procedures are defined in WG3.  24

6.13 Solution #12: A1 Policy authentication 29 
6.13.1 Introduction 30 
A1 policies are provided by the Non-RT RIC to the Near-RT RIC over the A1 interface, which already has security 31 
protections as defined in [2]. The security control for the A1 interface is TLS at the transport layer. In addition, the 32 
Near-RT RIC APIs include A1 related APIs for A1 Policy Setup, Update, and Delete procedures [3]. Security for 33 
these APIs is considered in key issues 10 and 11 and solutions 3, 4, and 13 of this technical report. 34 
6.13.2 Solution details 35 
For a malicious A1 policy to successfully affect the Near-RT RIC, the policy first must contain a valid policy type 36 
supported by the Near-RT RIC, in order to pass the policy type check at the A1 Termination in the Near-RT RIC. The 37 
policy must then be mapped to an xApp in the Near-RT RIC. Finally, to exploit an xApp for malicious intent as 38
