A term defined in the present document takes precedence over the definition of the same term, if any, in TR 21.905 [1]. 14
Non-RT RIC(O-RAN non-real-time RAN Intelligent Controller): a logical function that enables non-real-time control 15
and optimization of RAN elements and resources, AI/ML workflow including model training and updates, and policy-16
based guidance of applications/features in Near-RT RIC. 17
Near-RT RIC (O-RAN near-real-time RAN Intelligent Controller): a logical function that enables near-real-time control 18
and optimization of RAN elements and resources via fine-grained (e.g. UE basis, Cell basis) data collection and actions 19
over E2 interface. 20
O-CU: O-RAN Central Unit: a logical node hosting RRC, SDAP and PDCP protocols. 21
O-CU-CP: O-RAN Central Unit – Control Plane: a logical node hosting the RRC and the control plane part of the PDCP 22
protocol. 23
O-CU-UP: O-RAN Central Unit – User Plane: a logical node hosting the user plane part of the PDCP protocol and the 24
SDAP protocol. 25
O-DU: O-RAN Distributed Unit: a logical node hosting RLC/MAC/High-PHY layers based on a lower layer functional 26
split. 27
O-RU: O-RAN Radio Unit: a logical node hosting Low-PHY layer and RF processing based on a lower layer functional 28
split.  This is similar to 3GPP’s “TRP” or “RRH” but more specific in including the Low-PHY layer (FFT/iFFT, PRACH 29
extraction).   30
O-eNB (O-RAN eNB): an eNB or ng-eNB that supports E2 interface. 31
O1: Interface between orchestration & management entities (Orchestration/NMS) and O-RAN managed elements, for 32
operation and management, by which FCAPS management, Software management, File management and other similar 33
functions shall be achieved.  34
SMO: Service Management and Orchestration system. 35
A1: Interface between Non-RT RIC and Near-RT RIC to enable policy-driven guidance of Near-RT RIC 36
applications/functions, and support AI/ML workflow.  37
E2: Interface connecting the Near-RT RIC and one or more O-CU-CPs, one or more O-CU-UPs, and one or more O-DUs. 38

2 Overview of Non-RT RIC 27
2.1 Non-RT RIC in O-RAN Overall Architecture 28
In the O-RAN Logical Architecture, as seen in Figure 2.1-1 below, the Non-RT RIC is portrayed as a function that resides 29
within the Service Management and Orchestration Framework and which has a direct association with the A1 interface. 30
The Non-RT RIC’s position internal to, as opposed to interfacing with, the SMO is intended to communicate that the 31
Non-RT RIC is comprised of a subset of functionality of the SMO itself.   32
The direct association with the A1 interface is intended to convey that the functionality of the Non-RT RIC is directly 33
responsible for driving that which is sent and received across the A1 interface. The O1 and O2 interfaces are shown as 34
being directly associated with the SMO Framework itself, that which is not specific to the Non-RT RIC. This 35
representation is intended to communicate that the SMO Framework functionality, but not the Non-RT RIC functionality 36
therein, is directly responsible for driving that which is carried across these interfaces.   37

Similar to the Near-RT RIC, there is business value in visualizing the Non-RT RIC as being modularly extensible, that 9
functional extensibility being accomplished through modular applications that can be understood as running within the 10
Non-RT RIC function itself. Such applications have different lifecycles from the “framework” in which they run. For 11
convenience we will use the term “rApp” to refer to such applications. 12
Because rApps are associated with the Non-RT RIC, any framework services exposed to the rApps can be considered as 13
being exposed by the Non-RT RIC. Some of these framework services, for example access to O1, are not specific to the 14
Non-RT RIC, whereas others, such as access to A1, are specific to the Non-RT RIC.   15
There is a clear business benefit to allow “porting” of a Non-RT RIC application from one SMO Framework 16
implementation to another. Hence it would be useful for the O-RAN Alliance to define an open and standard interface 17
through which the Non-RT RIC exposes SMO Framework functionalities to “rApps” via the R1 Services exposure 18
functionality. We will refer to this as the “R1” interface.   19
With the above understandings, the Non-RT RIC itself can now be described as follows: 20
 Non-RT RIC – That functionality that drives the content carried across the A1 interface to the RAN. It can also 21
access other SMO Framework functionality, for example influencing that which is carried across the O1-CM 22
interface. It is comprised of two sub-functions: 23
o Non-RT RIC Framework – That functionality internal to the SMO Framework itself that: 24
 Logically terminates the A1 interface to the Near-RT RIC. 25

As observed above, rApps will require services from the R1 interface that are not necessarily associated with the Non-RT 10
RIC Framework, such as access to O1, data sharing services, and access to RAN inventory. The Non-RT RIC Framework, 11
via its R1 Services exposure functionality, would be responsible for exposing all of these services to the rApps, 12
irrespective of the location within the SMO Framework of the provider of those services. 13
A declared business need to “port” a Non-RT RIC Framework from one SMO Framework implementation to another 14
would drive the need for a formal interface within the SMO Framework between the Non-RT RIC Framework and the 15
rest of the SMO Framework. Absent this, different implementations of SMO Framework could make different design 16
choices as to how to manifest the boundary between the Non-RT RIC Framework and the rest of the SMO Framework, 17
or even to choose not to implement a clear boundary at all.   18
The lack of a clear boundary between the Non-RT RIC and the rest of the SMO Framework need not negatively impact 19
the ability to proceed with a formal definition of the R1 interface nor impact definition of the R1 Services exposure 20
functionality needed to support this interface. Rather, such definitions can proceed, agnostic to the source of the SMO 21
Framework services being exposed. However, it may be useful to define some terms that we can use to help us in 22
discussion of these topics. 23
Particularly it would be useful to have terms to distinguish between: 24
 An indication that a particular R1-exposed functionality is sourced from the Non-RT RIC Framework. This 25
functionality would be considered “inherent” to the Non-RT RIC Framework. 26
 An indication that it is left as an SMO Framework implementation decision whether a particular R1-exposed 27
functionality is sourced from the Non-RT RIC Framework or not.  28
 An indication that a particular R1-exposed functionality is not sourced from the Non-RT RIC Framework. This 29
functionality would be considered “inherent” to the SMO Framework itself, but not the Non-RT RIC Framework. 30
The functionality that is inherent to the Non-RT RIC Framework is that functionality related to the two interfaces that the 31
Non-RT RIC “owns”, specifically that functionality needed to drive the A1 and R1 interfaces. The O1 and O2 interfaces 32
are clear examples of functionality of the third bullet.   33
The term “Implementation Variable Functionality” is descriptive of that functionality described in the second bullet.  34
Examples might include functionalities such as “Data Sharing”, “Analytic Services”, “Policy”, “RAN Inventory”, 35
“General ML/AI Model LCM/Catalog”, RAN Mediation/Control”, “RAN Configuration Database”, “PM Short Term 36
Data Repository”, “Operations Intent Interface” and “External Interfaces”. 37

Note that each of the endpoint functional blocks of the SMO Framework’s external interfaces have interface termination 7
functions and functionalities associated with them to drive that respective interface. Thus, the Non-RT RIC Framework 8
has associated with it the “A1 Functionalities”, i.e., A1 Policy, A1 EI and A1 ML, required to drive the A1 interface, as 9
well as the A1 interface logical termination functions. Examples of R1 Services exposure functionality would include 10
what is needed to manage the runtime interactions of individual rApps with the Non-RT RIC Framework itself. 11
The Non-RT RIC architecture lends itself to be described by using two views, based on different approaches: a functional 12
approach and a service-based approach. In the functional approach, the architectural view focuses on “packaging” 13
functionalities into functions, i.e., logical entities with well-defined behaviour, connected by interfaces. In a service-based 14
approach, the architectural view focuses on describing the functionalities as capabilities offered through services by 15
service producer entities to service consumer entities, inspired by the service-based design principles defined in ETSI 16
ZSM [12]. Service consumer and service producer entities are interconnected by means of communication support that 17
enable adequate communication patterns e.g. 1-1, 1-many, pub-sub, routed etc. In general, a functional architectural view 18
can be regarded as a deployment option, or specific realization, allowed by the service-based architectural view, and both 19
provide the same functionalities. As described in the following, this principle is valid also for the Non-RT RIC case. 20
Following the principles above, Section 2.2 provides the functional view of the Non-RT RIC architecture, whereas Section 21

2.2a Non-RT RIC Architecture Service-based View 21
The service-based architectural approach is today widely adopted by telco industry. Service-based principles allow to 22
describe an architecture not based on fixed functional components and interfaces between them but based on the definition 23
of services as the central point of architecture consideration. Though there’s no formal definition available, a service-24
based architecture follows principles of which some are listed below: 25
 Modularity: The functionality is modularized into a set of services at the appropriate granularity level. 26
 Extensibility: The architecture allows simple extensibility by adding new services and making them 27
discoverable. 28
 Functional abstraction: Service-based architecture abstracts from the complexity and details of the underlying 29
functions that produce the services. 30
 Discoverability: Services are searchable for their availability. 31
 Composability: Services can be composed, forming new services.  32
 Reusability: Since services are modularized, they can be re-used and can be invoked at multiple stages of the 33
business process. 34
 Loose coupling: Services are loosely coupled and independent from one-another. 35
Designing an architecture in a service-based fashion allows a great degree of deployment flexibility and future-36
proofedness, as it leaves the choice of components that produce and/or consume certain services to the deployment, yet 37
facilitating multi-vendor interoperability through the definition of standardized services respectively standardized service 38
interfaces. Services are produced by service producers and consumed by service consumers through service endpoints. 39
Service-based architecture allows modularization of the services rather than the components of a deployment. Service 40
consumers need to be authenticated and authorized to access services at runtime by policies, rather than pre-assigning 41
fixed consumer-producer relationships at the time the standard is developed. Produced services are registered in a registry 42
from which service consumers can discover the available services, and the technical parameters how to consume them, 43

3.4.2 General Principles 3
As described in section 5.2 of [11], at least some ML applications may profitably be deployed either to the Non-RT RIC, 4
to the Near-RT RIC, or to both. This is certainly not true for all ML applications, as the same interfaces are not available 5
in both platforms (e.g., E2, A1 downstream) nor do each have the same real-time requirements. However, for the types 6
of interactions needed by the application types for which such portability would be beneficial, the design choices of the 7
R1 interface should not unnecessarily become an obstacle for such portability. 8
 Leverage Existing Work: Because rApps will share some common functionality with xApps (startup, messaging, 9
data sharing, etc.), the prior work done for xApps should be analyzed for applicability to the R1 use cases and 10
leveraged where appropriate. 11
 Aligned integration services: Because integration or enablement services (such as registration, discovery, 12
authorization, authentication, etc.) are needed to integrate rApps with the Non-RT RIC as well as to integrate 13
xApps with the Near-RT RIC, the work done for xApp integration/enablement should be analyzed for 14
applicability to rApp integration/enablement and leveraged where appropriate. 15
 Behavioral Alignment Where Practical: Because at least some ML applications will be deployable to the Non-16
RT RIC, Near-RT RIC, or both, the design choices made for R1 interactions should strive to minimize the 17
refactoring needed of an rApp to enable deployment into a Near-RT RIC environment. 18
 Decoupled Interface: Although rApps will share some common functionality with xApps, they will also have 19
many unique functionalities and needs that are not applicable to the Near-RT RIC.  Therefore, the analysis and 20
design choices made for the R1 interface should be done in parallel and not considered dependent on that of 21
xApps. 22
 The R1 interface is the sole interface between an rApp and the functionality of the Non-RT RIC and SMO.  23
Therefore, the R1 interface should be defined to meet all functional needs of rApps, with appropriate interface 24
extensibility capabilities as needed. 25
The R1 interface will provide a level of abstraction between rApps that are consumers of data and their data sources: 26

 Data Registration Services 20
This set of services is used in the setup of data routing paths between rApps and their data sources.   21
“Data Registration” functionality allows an rApp to communicate information about the data types that it consumes and 22
produces to the Non-RT RIC Framework. Data registration interactions occur when an rApp is deployed on the Non-RT 23
RIC Framework, and can be re-initiated by either entity thereafter as conditions change in the Non-RT RIC Framework. 24
Note that “data registration” interactions are different than “data subscription” interactions in that “data registration” 25
involves only data types and their periodicity, whereas “data subscription” involves specific sets of data within a given 26
“scope”.1 Thus, the purpose of “data registration” interactions is to enable the Non-RT RIC Framework to facilitate data 27
type level value-added services, such as: 28
 Authorizing an rApp to produce data of a certain data type. For example, a service provider may want to enforce 29
a policy that two separate rApp instances should not produce the same data type within the same Non-RT RIC 30
Framework instance. 31
 Authorizing an rApp to consume data of a certain data type. For example, a service provider may want to restrict 32
the availability of certain data types, such as data types that relate to sensitive data. 33
 Accounting for the number of rApp consumers that exist for a given data type. For example, licensing of certain 34
data-producing rApps may be sensitive to the number of consumer instances of that data type. 35
As part of registration processing, the Non-RT RIC Framework will match the needs of data type consumers against the 36
capabilities of data type producers. A valid data type producer for matching purposes would include previously registered 37

As part of data registration interactions, the rApp will first communicate to the Non-RT RIC Framework the data types 6

the data types that an rApp is capable of producing in some cases will depend on the availability of data types that it 8

sources. In many cases the data type consumed will be standard RAN O1 data.   10
As part of its data registration functionality, the Non-RT RIC Framework might also perform some added value 11
functionality such as: 12
 Keeping track of the number of rApp data consumers per data type and data source (e.g., for licensing or 13
accounting purposes). 14
 Ensuring that there is no more than one producer, within a Non-RT RIC instance, of a given data type, or if there 15

different “scopes” (e.g., geography or time). 17

data” requirements.   19
Only the rApp itself knows whether a data source is critical to the proper functioning of that application. Thus, it would 20
be inappropriate for the Non-RT RIC Framework to fail registration processing due to a lack of data source. However, it 21
is useful to have the Non-RT RIC Framework inform the rApp that a source cannot be found for one of its data type 22
inputs, because the lack of a data input could, as noted above, affect the data type(s) the rApp is capable of producing. 23
The rApp would determine how to react given that the data source is unknown: continue with the registration interactions 24
or fail the registration.    25
Note that a source of missing data could be found later, such as when an rApp later initiates data registration interactions 26
declaring its ability to produce that data type. To handle such a case, the design of the R1 interface can avoid requiring 27
an rApp to re-declare its desire to consume that data type by having the Non-RT RIC Framework use an anonymous 28
pub/sub paradigm to set up a routing rule for a data consumer rApp, even though no known source of this data currently 29
exists. With such an approach, the Non-RT RIC Framework need only inform the previously registered rApp that the data 30
type with a previously unknown data source now has a known data source. This interaction would inform the rApp that 31
data subscription requests can now be initiated for that data type. 32
Of course, the presence of a “new” data source may enable the previously registered rApp to produce a data type that it 33
previously could not due to lack of critical input data. Thus, it is necessary that an rApp be allowed to initiate R1 data 34
registration interactions with the Non-RT RIC Framework even after initial deployment. 35
Just as rApp data registration can result in new data-consumption potential for rApps that have previously registered, so 36
too can the deletion of an rApp result in the loss of data-consumption potential for previously registered rApps. This 37
possibility requires that the Non-RT RIC Framework also be able to inform a previously registered rApp that a data type 38
with a previously known data source now has an unknown data source. Because that data source may be a critical data 39
source for the previously registered rApp in producing its own data type, it is necessary that an rApp be able to initiate 40
R1 data registration interactions, even after initial deployment, to notify the Non-RT RIC Framework that it will no longer 41
be producing a particular data type. 42

A.1 Introduction to Illustrative Example 4
We will make use of the following fictitious rApps in some of the subsequent sections of this Annex. In this illustration, 5
there are three separate rApps that execute in a given Non-RT RIC Framework instance. 6
 rApp “R” – An RF signal predictor. This rApp consumes RAN (O1) measurements of the actual RF signal 7
experienced by a UE for its serving and neighbor cells (RFan,s) as well as other measurements useful in 8
determining that UE’s actual location (La). This rApp’s role is to output a future time (e.g., t = 10 seconds) 9
prediction of the location of that UE (Lpt) as well as a prediction of the RF signal that will at that location be 10
experienced by that UE for its serving and neighbor cells (RFptn,s). 11
 rApp “U” – A cell utilization predictor. This rApp consumes RAN (O1) cell utilization measurements regarding 12
the actual capacity utilization for a given cell site (CUa) over time. This rApp’s role is to trend that input so as 13
to allow it to output a future time (e.g., t = 60 minutes) prediction of that cell site’s utilization (CUpt). 14
 rApp “Q” – A UE QoE predictor. This rApp consumes measurements regarding a UE’s RF signal (either an 15
actual RAN measurement or a future time prediction) as well as a measurement of a cell site’s capacity utilization 16
(either an actual RAN measurement or a future time prediction). This rApp’s role is to calculate the QoE 17
experienced by that UE. Thus, this rApp can: 18
o Estimate the actual QoE currently being experienced by a UE (Qas), by consuming measurements of 19
the actual RF signal currently experienced by that UE relative to its serving cell (RFas) along with the 20
actual cell utilization of that serving cell (CUa).  21
o Estimate the QoE that a UE would experience were it currently connected instead to a neighbor cell 22
(Qpn), by consuming measurements of the actual RF signal currently experienced by that UE relative 23
to that neighbor cell (RFan) along with the actual cell utilization of that neighbor cell (CUa). 24
o Estimate the QoE that will be experienced by a UE at a future time (t) assuming that UE will remain 25
connected to its serving cell (Qpts), by consuming future time (t) predicted measurements of the RF 26
signal that will be experienced by that UE relative to its serving cell (RFpts), along with the future time 27
(t) predicted cell utilization of that serving cell (CUpt). 28
o Estimate the QoE that would be experienced by a UE at that future time if that UE were to be connected 29
instead to a neighbor cell (Qptn), by consuming future time (t) predicted measurements of the RF signal 30
that would be experienced by that UE relative to that neighbor cell (RFptn), along with the future time 31
(t) predicted cell utilization of that neighbor cell (CUpt). 32
