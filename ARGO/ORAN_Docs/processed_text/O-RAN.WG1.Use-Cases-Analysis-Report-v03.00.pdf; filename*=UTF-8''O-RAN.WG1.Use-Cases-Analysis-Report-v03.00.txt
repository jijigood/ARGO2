Chapter 3 Use Cases 1
3.1 Use case 1: Context-Based Dynamic HO Management for V2X 2
3.1.1 Background Information 3
V2X communication allows for numerous potential benefits such as increasing the overall road safety, reducing 4
emissions, and saving time. Part of the V2X architecture is the V2X UE (SIM + device attached to v ehicle) which 5
communicates with the V2X Application Server (V2X AS). The exchanged information comprises Cooperative 6
Awareness Messages (CAMs) from UE to V2X AS  [15], radio cell IDs, connection IDs, and basic radio measurements 7
(RSRP, RSPQ etc.). 8
3.1.2 Motivation 9
As vehicles traverse along a highway, due to their high speed and the heterogeneous natural environment V2X UE -s are 10
handed over frequently, at times in a suboptimal way, which may cause handover (HO) anomalies: e.g., short stay, ping-11
pong, and remote cell.  Such suboptimal HO sequences substantially impair the functionality of V2X applications. Since 12
HO sequences are mainly determined by the Neighbour Relation Tables (NRTs), maintained by the xNBs, there is hardly 13
room for UE-level customization. 14
This UC aims to present a method to avoid and/or resolve problematic HO scenarios by using past navigation and radio 15
statistics in order to customize HO sequences on a UE level.  To this end, the AI/ML functionality that is enabled by the 16
Near-RT RIC is employed. 17
3.1.3 Proposed Solution 18
 19
Figure 3.1.3- 1: Dynamic Handover Management for V2X use case 20
In order to prevent anomalous HO sequences causing performance degradation for the V2Xs, an xApp can be built with 21
two main functionalities. Applying long-term analytics by using AI/ML algorithms is the first function expected from the 22
xApp. As it is suggested by O-RAN framework, non-real-time intelligent management of RAN functions are deployed in 23
the Non-RT RIC. V2X AS maintains the UE-based HO events and mobility data which are shared with Non-RT RIC over 24
O1 interface. Hence, Non-RT RIC can identify causes of HO anomalies and discover optimal HO sequences. Finally, a 25
data base is maintained to keep track of resolutions to anomalies based on these feedbacks. The other function of the 26

3.2 Use case 2: Flight Path Based Dynamic UAV Radio Resource 22
Allocation 23
3.2.1 Background Information 24
This use case provides the background, motivation, and requirements for the support the use  case of flight path based 25
dynamic UAV Radio Resource Allocation, allowing operators to adjust radio resource allocation policies through the O-26
RAN architecture, reducing unnecessary handover and improving radio resource utilization. 27
3.2.2 Motivation 28
The field trials’ results show that the coverage for low altitude is good and can provide various services for terrestrial UEs 29
with good performance. However, since the site along the flight is mainly for terrestrial UEs, the altitude of the UAV is 30
always not within the main lobe of the ground station antenna. And the side lobes give rise to the phenomenon of scattered 31
cell associations particularly noticeable in the sky. The cell association pattern on the ground is ideally contiguous area 32
where the best cell is most often the one closest to the UE. As the UE move up in height, the antenna side lobes start to 33
be visible, and the best cell may no longer be the closest one. The cell association pattern in this particular scenario 34

3.2.3 Proposed Solution 10
To manage resource allocation of UAVs based on Flight Path, Non-RT RIC retrieves necessary Aerial Vehicles related 11
metrics from network level measurement reports. Moreover , flight path information o f Aerial V ehicle, climate 12
information, flight forbidden/limitation area information and Space Load information are received from an application, 13
e.g. UTM (Unmanned Traffic Management) for constructing/training relevant AI/ML model. For example, this could be 14
UL/DL interference from/to Aerial vehicles,  the detection of Aerial Vehicle UEs, and available radio resource (e.g. 15
frequency, cell, beam, BWP, numerology) prediction. The Near-RT RIC supports deployment and execution of AI/ML 16
model from Non-RT RIC. Based on this, Near-RT RIC can perform the radio resource allocation for UAVs considering 17
the radio channel condition, flight path information and other application information  with on -demand coverage . 18
Moreover, Aerial Vehicles performance reports should be sent to Non-RT RIC for evaluation and optimization of the ML 19
model. 20

3.3 Use case 3: Radio Resource Allocation for UAV Application 1
Scenario 2
3.3.1 Background Information 3
As shown in Figure 3.3.1-1, this scenario refers to a Rotor UAV flying at low altitude and low speed and carrying cameras, 4
sensors and other devices mounted. The Operation Terminal uses the 5.8 GHz frequency band remote control the UAV 5
for border/forest inspection, high voltage/base station inspection, field mapping, pollution sampling, and HD live 6
broadcast. 7
At the same time, the UAV control mobile station and the UAV anti-weapon combination provide the UAV control, fight 8
against illegal UAV and other services to ensure low-altitude safety in special areas. 9
The UAV Operation terminal, the anti -UAV weapon, and the UAV control mobile station are connected with the UAV 10
Control Vehicle using 5G network. UAV Control Vehicle deploys network equipment, including O-CU,O-DU, the Non-11
RT RIC, the Near R T RIC function modules and Application Server (In this use case, it is an Edge computing Service 12
Platform) to provide reliable network services through 5G networks. 13
 14

3.6 Use case 6: Massive MIMO Beamforming Optimization 24
3.6.1 Background Information 25
Massive MIMO is seen as one of the key technologies for 5G. Due to the multi-antenna transmission and reception, this 26
technology can inherently to provide diversity and improve capacity by targeting high gain antenna beams towards one 27
or multiple subscribers, thus improving the receive power levels and spatially filtering the interference from neighboring 28
subscribers and transmission points. In addition, a spatial multiplexing operations regime can improve the network 29
capacity by transferring multiple data streams towards/from one or different sub scribers utilizing a spatial re -use of the 30
scarce time/frequency resource blocks. Further advantages include the controlling of electromagnetic (EM) emissions or 31
advanced network management technologies like beam -based load balancing, beam failure reductio ns, or adaptive cell 32
coverage areas, especially in highly dense urban 3D environments and mobile subscribers.  In order to further optimize 33
networks, e.g., maximize spectral efficiency, optimize coverage, or maximize cell capacity, fully digital beamforming  34
(BF) methods are to be employed for below 6 GHz frequency wireless telecommunications. Grid of Beams (GoB) is a 35
BF method which aims at selectively covering regions of interest with a suitable subset of radio beams (chosen from a 36
dictionary of possible beams). 37

3.7 Use case 7: RAN Sharing 1
3.7.1 Background Information 2
One of the main challenges for network operators is to deploy a massive number of services, while providing different 3
Quality of Service (QoS) requirements and keeping reasonable the level of investment in the network. Network sharing 4
is envisioned as an efficient and sustainable way to accelerate the deployment of 5G, while taking advantage of a common 5
pool of physical infrastructures and resources, made available for two or more partner operators.  6
Besides, regulatory requirements often force operators to  provide coverage in not business attractive areas, causing 7
profitability issues. To this end, RAN sharing is seen as a promising solution that should reduce network costs, increase 8
network capacity and coverage, while enhancing customer satisfaction. Accordingly, the open and multivendor nature of 9
the O-RAN architecture can accelerate the introduction and development of RAN sharing solutions, by enhancing the 10
deployment of virtual network functions (VNF) on commodity shared hardware, while taking into acco unt diverse QoS 11
requirements. 12
Among the different RAN-sharing models that have been experimented so far, a special focus is put here on the evaluation 13
of the compatibility of the “Geographical Split” RAN sharing model with the O -RAN architecture. In such a  model, a 14
coverage area is split between two or more operators; each operator manages the RAN in a specific area, while offering 15
access to its RAN resources to its operator partners. Two main configurations  have been used worldwide  [17] Multi 16
Operator Core Network (MOCN) and Multi Operator RAN (MORAN). In MOCN, both the RAN infrastructure and 17
carriers are pooled. Even though this model enables further cost saving, esp ecially in rural areas due to a lower number 18
of carriers, it requires the presence of a regulatory entity that takes care of the allocation of different parts of the shar ed 19
spectrum between operators. Conversely, in MORAN, each operator utilizes a separate  carrier, while getting more 20
freedom and independency on the control of the radio resources. MORAN is the most widely used sharing configuration 21
as it can provide appropriate independency to each sharing partner operator, while maximizing the benefits of sharing in 22
terms of CAPEX and OPEX. 23
Besides, the O -RAN architecture can provide new opportunities for implementing this RAN sharing model in a more 24
efficient way, thanks to its multi -vendor interfaces and abstraction control provided by the RIC (RAN Intelli gent 25
Controller). Accordingly, O-RAN can accelerate the deployment of 3GPP-based solutions by providing more flexibility 26
in the management of the shared resources. 27
3.7.2 Motivation 28
Currently, in 3GPP there are ongoing discussions for identifying which RAN functions should be included in the RAN 29
sharing procedures in 5G [4][11]. Specifically, it has been analysed the feasibility to share the whole or a part of the DU 30
or CU functional blocks while assuming the sharing of a common physical layer (PHY). In  [14], a first agreement has 31
been achieved stating that multiple logical CU -CP/ DU can control the same PHY radio resources but coordination 32
between logical CU-CP needs to be ensured by an appropriate implementation (not standardized).  33
In this context, O -RAN can be seen as the ideally enabler of the 3GPP RAN sharing model by providing the required 34
coordination between the shared network nodes. To this end, the RIC can enable the co ordination of multiple CU -CP/ 35
DU via the E2 interface, while opening the road for diverse RAN sharing scenarios. Moreover, O -RAN can accelerate 36
the deployment of compliant 5G RAN sharing solutions by taking advantage of the multi -vendor nature of the F1, X 2 37
interfaces. 38

3.8 Use case 8: QoS Based Resource Optimization 22
3.8.1 Background Information 23
QoS based resource optimization can used when the network has been configured to provide some kind of preferential 24
QoS for certain users. One such scenario can be related to when the network has been configured to support e2e slices. In 25
this case, the network has functionality that ensures resource isolation between slices as well as functionality to monitor 26
that slice Service Level Specifications (SLS) are fulfilled.  27
In RAN, it is the scheduler that ensures that Physical Resource Block (PRB) resources are isolated between slices in the 28
best possible way and also that the PRB resources are used in an optimal way to best fulfill the SLS for different  slices. 29
The desired default RAN behavior for slices is configured over O1. For example, the ratio of physical resources (PRBs) 30
reserved for a slice is configured at slice creation (instantiation) over O1. Also, QoS can be configured to guide the RAN 31
scheduler how to (in real-time) allocate PRB resources to different users to best fulfill the SLS of a specific slice. In the 32
NR NRM this is described by the resource partition attribute. 33
Instantiation of a RAN sub -slice will be prepared by rigorous planning to understand to what extent deployed RAN 34
resources will be able to support RAN sub-slice SLS. Part of this procedure is to configure RAN functionality according 35
to above. With this, a default behavior of RAN is obtained that will be able to fulfill slice SLSs for most situations. 36

3.11.1 Background Information 7

functions. Towards this, we identify the intelligent control functions which can be realized as a DSS applicatio n to 24

3.13 Use case 13: Local Indoor Positioning in RAN 1
3.13.1 Background Information 2
Cellular network based positioning is an important technology for 5G vertical industries, individuals and oper ators, 3
especially in local indoor scenarios. For instance, Mall can provide value-added services, such as local indoor navigation 4
and shop recommendation by leverage real -time indoor positioning. Industrial manufacturing field shall send real -time 5
safe warning to remind operators keep away from the dangerous area which would also need real-time indoor positioning. 6
NR positioning is introduced by 3GPP Rel.16. The location management function (LMF) resides in core network acts as 7
a location server. LTE positioning protocol (LPP) is reused for the UE measurements and NR positioning Protocol “a” 8
(NRPPa) based on LPPa is used for the gNB measurements [8]. The NR Positioning Protocol A (NRPPa) PDUs carry E-9
CID, OTDOA are routed between eNB/gNB and the LMF via AMF. The AMF routes the NRPPa PDUs transparently 10
based on a Routing ID corresponding to the involved LMF over NG -C interface without knowledge of the involved 11
NRPPa transaction. This long route messages  between eNB/gNB and centralized LMF may  suffer network jitters and 12
leads to un-real-time UE location results. For some local indoor scenario, e.g., mall and industrial manufacturing, it would 13
be better to directly deploy the positioning function in the RAN side and expose UE location to local applications.  14
3.13.2 Motivation 15
For local indoor scenarios, the positioning function inside RAN is envisioned to be a promising solution. It not only 16
reduces the latency of positioning but also can reuse the edge cloud infrastructure  in RAN. In the context of O -RAN 17
architecture, the positioning function can be deployed as a positioning xApp in t he Near-RT RIC. The positioning xApp 18
computes the UE location and optional velocity based on the positioning measurement obtained via the E2 interface.  19
One example is that distributed indoor small base station in the operator’s domain can provide UE positioning service by 20
adding Near-RT RIC with positioning xApp. The positioning related information report from small base station  to the 21
positioning xApp inside Near-RT RIC can be measured based on the uplink SRS information.  By leveraging multi-point 22
positioning, field strength positioning and other positioning algorithms , positioning xApp inside Near-RT RIC can 23
conduct a real-time position of UE. 24
3.13.3 Proposed Solution 25
Cellular network based positioning procedure mainly includes two main steps: 1) positioning measurements reports; and 26
2) position computation and optional velocity estimation based on the measurements.  The local indoor positioning in 27
RAN use case mainly involves the Near-RT RIC, E2 nodes and potentially Non-RT RIC in the O-RAN architecture. To 28
enable the local indoor positioning in RAN, Near-RT RIC should be able to discover the location measurement capability 29
of the E2 nodes and subscribe the positioning measurements based on the chosen positioning algorithm and positioning 30
QoS requirements.  31
Non-RT RIC can also be leveraged to provide the AI/ML model if machine learning based algorithm is selected for the 32
positioning. In this case, Non-RT RIC trains the AI/ML position model based on the historical positioning measurements 33
(e.g., RSSI) and the labeled user location (e.g. by manual or by minimal drive test (MDT). Then, the trained positioning 34
AI/ML model can be deployed to Near-RT RIC for real-time positioning inference. 35
The E2 nodes are expected to provide positioning measurements to Near-RT RIC as required. The measurements report 36
can be periodical or event driven  based on Near-RT RIC subscription. Near-RT RIC may also adjust the corresponding 37
positioning measurement configurations in E2 nodes to assure the measure requirement. What kind of  positioning 38
measurements need to be reported from E2 Node depend on the subscription request from positioning xApp in Near-RT 39
