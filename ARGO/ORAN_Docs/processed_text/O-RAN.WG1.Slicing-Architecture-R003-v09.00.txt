Chapter 2.  Slicing Overview 1 
Network Slicing is expected to play a critical role in 5G networks because of various use cases and services 5G will 2 
support. It allows a network operator to provide services tailored to customers’ requirements. Network slice is defined as 3 
a logical network with a bundle of specified network services over a common network infrastructure.  A single physical 4 
network is sliced into multiple virtual networks that can suppo rt different service types over a single RAN. 3GPP has 5 
standardized 4 different service types: eMBB, URLLC, MIoT and V2X  [3].  6 
3GPP defined 5G architecture and procedures containing network slicing and related concepts in Release 15. Furthermore, 7 
management and orchestration of 5G networks featuring slicing was defined in the 3GPP specifications. Other standard 8 
groups e.g. GSMA, ETSI NFV-MANO, ETSI ZSM and ONAP focus on the different aspects of network slicing. Further 9 
information regarding network slicing and other SDO’s contributions was discussed in the Study on O-RAN Slicing 10 
Technical Report [24]. 11 
A sample RAN slicing deployment of O-RAN network functions based on the select initial deployment option, option B 12 
as described in [20], is shown in Figure 2-1, with some of the network functions shared between RAN slice subnets (such 13 
as O-CU-CP, O-DU, O-RU) and some network functions dedicated to a particular RAN slice subnet (such as O-CU-UP). 14

Chapter 3.  High-Level O-RAN Slicing Use Cases 1 
This section contains high-level O-RAN slicing use cases that O -RAN is expected to support. Slicing requirements will 2 
include the requirements derived from the specified use cases.   Additional use cases will be added as prioritized by the 3 
O-RAN community in future versions of this document. It should be noted that not all of the use cases presented here are 4 
currently supported by O-RAN specifications and these use cases will be addressed in future O-RAN work. 5 
3.1 O-RAN Slice Subnet Management and Provisioning Use 6 
Cases 7 
Network slicing is conceived to be an end -to-end feature that includes the core network, the transport network and the 8 
RAN. Although 3GPP has started defining network slicing support with Release 15, slicing in O-RAN needs to be further 9 
addressed in line with 3GPP to achieve deployable network slicing in an open RAN environment.   10 
Management aspects of network slicing such as Network Sli ce Instances (NSI) and Network Slice Subnet Instances 11 
(NSSI) are defined by 3GPP. While NSI refers to an end -to-end network slice, NSSI refers to a part of it such as a RAN 12 
slice subnet. The provisioning of network slicing includes the four phases which ar e preparation, commissioning, 13 
operation and decommissioning. The NSI/NSSI provisioning operations include:  14 
- Create an NSI/NSSI; 15 
- Activate an NSI/NSSI; 16 
- De-active an NSI/NSSI; 17 
- Modify an NSI/NSSI; 18 
- Terminate an NSI/NSSI. 19 
Further details of NSI and NSSI lifecycle management and provisioning can be found 3GPP TS28.531 [6], Section 4.1. 20 
This chapter provides the use cases and procedures necessary for O -RAN Slice Subnet Management that is in -line with 21 
3GPP Slice Management framework. For this purpose, use cases such as slice subnet creation, activation, modification, 22 
deactivation, termination, configuration and feasibility check are considered for O-RAN architecture. 23

3.2 O-RAN Slicing Use Cases 1 
3.2.1 Use Case 1: RAN Slice SLA Assurance 2 
In the 5G era, network slicing is a prominent feature which provides end-to-end connectivity and data processing tailored 3 
to specific business requirements. These requirements include customizable network capabilities such as the support of 4 
very high data rates, traffic densities, service availability and very low latency. According to 5G standardization efforts, 5 
the 5G system should support the needs of the business through the specification of several service needs such as data 6 
rate, traffic capacity, user density, latency, reliability, and availability. These capabilities are always provided based on a 7 
Service Level Agreement (SLA) between the mobile operator and the business customer, which brought up interest for 8 
mechanisms to ensure slice SLAs and prev ent its possible violations. O -RAN’s open interfaces and AI/ML based 9 
architecture will enable such challenging mechanisms to be implemented and help pave the way for operators to realize 10 
the opportunities of network slicing in an efficient manner.   11 
RAN slice SLA assurance scenario involves Non-RT RIC, Near-RT RIC, E2 Nodes and SMO interaction.  The scenario 12 
starts with the retrieval of RAN specific slice SLA/requirements (possibly within SMO or from NSSMF depending on 13 
Operator deployment options). Based on  slice specific performance measurements from E2 Nodes, Non -RT RIC and 14 
Near-RT RIC can fine-tune RAN behavior aligned with O -RAN architectural roles to assure RAN slice SLAs. Non -RT 15 
RIC monitors long-term trends and patterns for RAN slice subnets’ performance, and employs AI/ML methods to perform 16 
corrective actions through SMO (e.g. reconfiguration via O1) or via creation of A1 policies. Non -RT RIC can also 17 
construct/train relevant AI/ML models that will be deployed at Near -RT RIC. A1 policies possibly incl ude scope 18 
identifiers (e.g. S-NSSAI) and statements such as KPI targets. On the other hand, Near -RT RIC enables optimized RAN 19 
actions through execution of deployed AI/ML models in near-real-time by considering both O1 configuration (e.g. static 20 
RRM policies) and received A1 policies, as well as received slice specific E2 measurements.  21

This use case enables multiple slices with functions provided by multi-vendors, such as slice #1, composed of DU(s) and 16

vendor who provides vO-DU and vO-CU function may have a strength of a customized scheduler for a certain service. 23 
With accomplishment of multi-vendor circumstances, following benefits can be expected: 24 
1) More flexible and time to market deployment 25 
Operators can maximize options to choose suitable vO -DU/scheduler and vO -CU to offer various slices.  For 26

scheduler for URLLC service. Or, vendor A can provide vO -DU/scheduler and vO-CU suitable for URLLC earlier 28 
than vendor B, therefore operators can choose vO -DU and vO -CU functions from vendor A to meet their service 29 
requirements. 30

less consideration for existing vendors if multi -vendor circumstance was realized. This may help expand vendor’s 32

3.2.3 Use Case 3: NSSI Resource Allocation Optimization  1 
5G networks are becoming increasingly complex with the densification of millimeter wave small cells, and various new 2 
services, such as eMBB (enhanced Mobile Broadband), URLLC (Ultra Reliable Low Latency Communications), and 3 
mMTC (massive Machine Type Communications) that are characterized by high speed high data volume, low speed ultra-4 
low latency, and infrequent transmitting low data volume from huge number of emerging smart devices, respectively. It 5 
is a challenging task for 5G networks to allocate resources dynamically and efficiently among multiple network nodes to 6 
support various services. However, eMBB, URLLC, and mMTC services in 5G are typically realized as NSI(s) (Network 7 
Slice instance(s)). Therefore, the resources allocated to NSSI (Network Slice Subn et Instance) to support the O -RAN 8 
nodes can be optimized according to the service requirements. 9 
As the new 5G services have different characteristics, the network traffic tends to be sporadic, where there may be 10 
different usage pattern in terms of time, location, UE distribution, and types of applications. For example, most IoT sensor 11 
applications may run during off-peak hours or weekends. Special events, such as sport games, concerts, can cause traffic 12 
demand to shoot up at certain times and locations. The refore, NSSI resource allocation optimization function trains the 13 
AI/ML model, based on the huge volume of performance data collected over days, weeks, months from O -RAN nodes. 14 
It then uses the AI/ML model to predict the traffic demand patterns of 5G netwo rks in different times and locations for 15 
each network slice subnet, and automatically re-allocates the network resources ahead of the network issues surfaced.  16 
The resource quota policies associated with RAN NFs (E2 Nodes) included in the respective NSSIs enable 5G network 17 
providers to optimize or prioritize the utilization of the RAN resources across slices and supports the flexibility to share 18 
resources optimally across critical service slices during resource surplus or scarcity. For example, an NSSI alloc ated for 19 
premium service can receive a major share of the resources compared to a slice allocated for a standard/best-effort service.  20 
Another such example is the scenario of additional resource allocation for emergency services. An important 21 
consideration here is that the NSSI resource quota polic ies focuses on maximization of resource utilization across the 22 
NSSIs .The resource quota policies can be used as a constraint for resource allocation that defines the range of resources 23 
that can be allocated per s lice. One use case for applying such a constraint is the analysis a nd decision based on history 24

respective SLAs through SMO. In addition, Non-RT RIC may retrieve enrichment information from 3rd party applications 18 
enabling advanced RAN slicing technology to be applied in O-RAN framework.  19 
In order to construct AI/ML models to be deployed in the Near-RT RIC, Non-RT RIC retrieves slice specific performance 20 
metrics, configuration parameters and required attributes of the RAN slice subnets from the SMO framework. Complex 21

Near-RT RIC is the component which enables near-real-time RAN slice subnet optimization through execution of slicing 8 
related xApps and communicating necessary parameters to O-CU and O-DU through E2 interface. Deployed xApps may 9 
utilize either AI/ML based models or other control schemes which can further be guided by A1 policies that are generated 10 
by Non-RT RIC.  11 
In order to drive sliced RAN resources properly, Near-RT RIC should have the knowledge of existing RAN slice subnets 12 
as well as their requirements. This information will be received through O1 interface during provisioning of RAN slic e 13 
subnets. Therefore similar to Non -RT RIC, Near-RT RIC will be aware of RAN slice  subnets through O-RAN specific 14 
information models and provisioning procedures.    15 
In O-RAN slicing architecture, configuration of slice resources on E2 nodes can be achieved  through slow loop with O1 16 
configuration and through fast loop with E2 configuration. This architecture enables advanced slicing use cases such as 17 
RAN slice SLA assurance and further enhances 3GPP slicing capability without misalignment. In the context of the RAN, 18 
the SLA assurance parameters sent over the A1 interface help the Near -RT RIC guide the behavior of the E2 Nodes (O -19 
CU-UP, O-CU-CP, O-DU). While Near-RT RIC is capable for fast-loop configuration, slicing related O1 configuration, 20 
such as RRM policy information sent to O-CU, configured by the SMO framework will be taken into account. Moreover, 21 
slice specific near -RT performance data will be monitored through E2 interface  which needs proper PM mechanisms 22 
between E2 nodes and Near-RT RIC as well. 23 
5.3 O-RAN Central Unit (O-CU) 24 
O-CU, which includes a single O -CU-CP and possibly multiple O -CU-UP(s), which are communicating through E1 25 
interface, needs to support slicing features as defined by 3GPP. Depending on slice requirements, O-CU-UP can be shared 26 
across slices or a specific instance of O -CU-UP can be instantiated  per slice. On top of 3GPP slicing features, O -RAN 27 
further enhances slicing through the utilization of E2 interface and the assistance of Near-RT RIC dynamic slice 28 
optimizations along with the enhanced O1 interface to support additional slice configuration parameters.  29

resource allocation and isolation strategies. These stacks are initially configured through O1 interface based on the slice 31 
specific requirements and then dynamically updated through E2 interface via Near-RT RIC for various slicing use cases.  32

and E2 interfaces respectively, where the PMs can be used for slice performance monitoring and slice SLA assurance 34 
purposes. 35

5.5 A1 Interface 4 
A1, which is the interface between the Non -RT RIC and the Near -RT RIC, supports policy management, ML model 5 
management and enrichment information services [18]. These three services will be utilized for various slicing use cases, 6 
such as slice SLA assurance. Policy management will be used by Non-RT RIC to send slice specific (e.g. S-NSSAI based) 7 
policies to guide Near -RT RIC with slice resource allocations and slice specific control activities, as well as to receive 8 
slice specific policy feedback for the policies deployed on the Near-RT RIC. 9 
For the use cases that make use of external enrichment data or where Non-RT RIC produces enrichment information, A1 10 
enrichment interface will be used to send slice specific enrichment data to Near -RT RIC. 11 
It should be noted that slice specific A1 policies are not persis tent (do not survive the restart of Near-RT RIC) and while 12 
they may take precedence over O1 slice specific configurations, they should be aligned and not deviate significantly from 13

5.6 E2 Interface 16 
E2, which is the interface between the Near -RT RIC and the E2 nodes, supports E2 primitives (Report, Insert, Control 17 
and Policy) to control the services exposed by E2 nodes [19]. These primitives will be used by slice specific applications 18 
(xApps) to drive E2 nodes’ slice configurations and slice specific behaviour, such as slice based radio resource 19 
management, radio resource allocations, MAC scheduling policies and other  configuration parameters used by various 20 
RAN protocol stacks.  21 
E2 will be used to configure and receive slice specific reports and performance data from E2 nodes. Thes e reports may 22 
include 3GPP defined slice specific PMs (such as PRB utilization, average delay, etc.  [10]) and new PMs that can be 23 
defined by O-RAN to support various slicing use cases. 24 
Note: It is intended to add examples for the usage of E2 primitives for slicing use cases in the next version of this document. 25 
5.7 O1 Interface 26 
O1, which is the interface between O-RAN managed elements and the management entity as defined in [14], will be used 27 
to configure slice specific parameters of O-RAN nodes based on the service requirements of the slice. 3GPP have defined 28 
some of the slice specific information models in TS 28.541 [9], including the RRM policy attributes to provide the ratio 29 
of PRBs and the split of these PRBs among slices. To support O-RAN slicing use cases and their requirements , 3GPP 30 
information models may be extended and additional information models may be defined to capture slice profiles and slice 31 
specific configuration parameters, which will be carried over O1 interface as well. 32 
O1 will also be used to configure and gather slice specific performance metrics and slice specific faults from O -RAN 33 
nodes. 34 
Note: It is intended to add examples for the usage of O1 for the configuration, performance and fault management of 35 
slicing use cases in the next version of this document. 36

C.1 Slicing and 3GPP Slice Modeling Terminology Awareness 16 
This section intends to provide further information and clarified awareness for some of the fundamental network slicing 17 
concepts and 3GPP modeling aspects.  18 
C.1.1 Conceptual Differences Between 3GPP SA2 and SA5 19 
(System Architecture) Network Slice (3GPP SA2): Even though this is a system architecture group, the RAN architecture 20 
is out of the scope of SA2. RAN3 owns the RAN architecture. SA2 service -based architecture (SBA) is a functional 21 
architecture with mostly service-based interfaces. 22 
(Management Aspect) Network Slice (3GPP SA5): SA5 describes all the management aspects of a network, which is 23 
modular and model driven. It is a service -based management architecture (SBMA), using service -based API allowing 24 
implementations to pick and choose appropriate API and compliance per API. See 3GPP TS 28.533 [8].  25 
A Network Slice Instance (NSI), a SA2 term, is a set of functions. SA2 also introduced the concept of an instance because 26 
there would be more than one slice of the same type for the purpose of scaling. What is managed by an operator is a group 27 
of functions (an NSI). 28 
A NetworkSlice Managed Object Instance (MOI) , a SA5 usage, is an object exposing to an exte rnal consumer. Once an 29 
operator builds a something to satisfy expectations, it needs to build something that is exposed to an external consumer 30 
which is a NetworkSlice MOI. Scale in/Scale out is an operation within Life Cycle Management which is under the 31 
purview of SA5. In the context of SA5, a NSI is just an attribute carrying signaling “NSI” value semantic. From a 32 
management perspective, the abbreviation NSI is sometimes used as a replacement of the proper term NetworkSlice MOI. 33 
However, this is just an improper use of terminology.  34 
A NetworkSliceSubnet MOI is sometimes called an NSSI. However, in SA5, any occurrences of the term NSSI should 35 
be perceived as a NetworkSliceSubnet MOI. 36

(eMnSs) from E_NSSMS_C e.g., service endpoint termination, throttling, translation between external and 32 
internal information, re-exposure of service operations and events, topology hiding, routing, etc.) 33 
- related to registry and catalog specific aspects (i.e. , capabilities to manage various data records and profiles to 34 
support registration, discovery, publishing, identity management, authentication, authorization, access policies, 35 
data translation rules, topology hiding policies etc.)  36
