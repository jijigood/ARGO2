Chapter 2.  Slicing Overview 1 
Network Slicing is expected to play a critical role in 5G networks because of various use cas es and services 5G will 2 
support. It allows a network operator to provide services tailored to customers’ requirements. Network slice is defined as 3 
a logical network with a bundle of specified network services over a common network infrastructure.  A single  physical 4 
network is sliced into multiple virtual networks that can support different service types over a single RAN. 3GPP has 5 
standardized 4 different service types: eMBB, URLLC, MIoT and V2X [3].  6 
3GPP defined 5G architecture and procedures containing network slicing and related concepts in Release 15. Furthermore, 7 
management and orchestration of 5G networks featuring slicing was defined in the 3GPP specifications. Other standard 8 
groups e.g. GSMA, ETSI NFV-MANO, ETSI ZSM and ONAP focus on the different aspects of network slicing. Further 9 
information regarding network slicing and other SDO’s contributions was discussed in the Study on O-RAN Slicing 10 
Technical Report [14]. 11 
A sample RAN slicing deployment of O-RAN network functions based on the select initial deployment option, option B 12 
as described in [13], is shown in Figure 1, with some of the network functions shared between RAN slices (such as O -13 
CU-CP, O-DU, O-RU) and some network functions dedicated to a particular RAN slice (such as O-CU-UP). 14

Chapter 3.  High-Level O-RAN Slicing Use Cases 1 
This section contains high-level O-RAN slicing use cases that O-RAN is expected to support. Slicing requirements will 2 
include the requirements derived from the specified use cases.   Additional use cases will be added as prioritized by the 3 
O-RAN community in future versions of this document.  4 
3.1 O-RAN Slice Subnet Management and Provisioning Use 5 
Cases 6 
Network slicing is conceived to be an end -to-end feature that includes the core network, the transport network and the 7 
RAN. Although 3GPP has started defining network slicing support with Release 15, slicing in O-RAN needs to be further 8 
addressed in line with 3GPP to achieve deployable network slicing in an open RAN environment.   9 
Management aspects of network slicing such as Network Slice Instances (NSI) and Network Slice Subnet Instances 10 
(NSSI) are defined by 3GPP. While NSI refers to an end-to-end network slice, NSSI refers to a part of it such as a RAN 11 
slice. The provisioning of network slicing includes the four phases which are preparation, commissioning, operation and 12 
decommissioning. The NSI/NSSI provisioning operations include: 13 
- Create an NSI/NSSI; 14 
- Activate an NSI/NSSI; 15 
- De-active an NSI/NSSI; 16 
- Modify an NSI/NSSI; 17 
- Terminate an NSI/NSSI. 18 
Further details of NSI and NSSI lifecycle management and provisioning can be found 3GPP TS28.531 [5], Section 4.1. 19 
This chapter provides the use cases and procedures necessary for O -RAN Slice Subnet Management that is in -line with 20 
3GPP Slice Management framework. For  this purpose, use cases such as slice subnet creation, activation, modification, 21 
deactivation, termination, configuration and feasibility check are considered for O-RAN architecture. 22

3.2 O-RAN Slicing Use Cases 1 
3.2.1 Use Case 1: RAN Slice SLA Assurance 2 
In the 5G era, network slicing is a prominent feature which provides end-to-end connectivity and data processing tailored 3 
to specific business requirements. These requirements include customizable network capabilities such as the support of 4 
very high data rates, traffic densities, service availability and very low latency. According to 5G standardization efforts, 5 
the 5G system should support the needs of the business through the specification of several service needs such as data 6 
rate, traffic capacity, user density, latency, reliability, and availability. These capabilities are always provided based on a 7 
Service Level Agreement (SLA) between the mobile operator and the business customer, which brought up interest for 8 
mechanisms to ensure slice SLAs and prev ent its possible violations. O -RAN’s open interfaces and AI/ML based 9 
architecture will enable such challenging mechanisms to be implemented and help pave the way for operators to realize 10 
the opportunities of network slicing in an efficient manner.   11 
RAN slice SLA assurance scenario involves Non-RT RIC, Near-RT RIC, E2 Nodes and SMO interaction.  The scenario 12 
starts with the retrieval of RAN specific slice SLA/requirements (possibly within SMO or from NSSMF depending on 13 
Operator deployment options). Based on  slice specific performance measurements from E2 Nodes, Non -RT RIC and 14 
Near-RT RIC can fine-tune RAN behavior aligned with O -RAN architectural roles to assure RAN slice SLAs. Non -RT 15 
RIC monitors long-term trends and patterns for RAN slice subnets’ performance, and employs AI/ML methods to perform 16 
corrective actions through SMO (e.g. reconfiguration via O1) or via creation of A1 policies. Non -RT RIC can also 17 
construct/train relevant AI/ML models that will be deployed at Near -RT RIC. A1 policies possibly incl ude scope 18 
identifiers (e.g. S-NSSAI) and statements such as KPI targets. On the other hand, Near-RT RIC enables optimized RAN 19 
actions through execution of deployed AI/ML models in near-real-time by considering both O1 configuration (e.g. static 20 
RRM policies) and received A1 policies, as well as received slice specific E2 measurements. 21

applications may run during off-peak hours or weekends. Special events, such as sport games, concerts, can cause traffic 9 
demand to shoot up at certain times and locations. Therefore, NSSI resource allocation optimization function trains the 10 
AI/ML model, based on the huge volume of performance data collected over days, weeks, months from O -RAN nodes. 11 
It then uses the AI/ML model to predict the traffic demand patterns of 5G networks in different times and locations for 12 
each network slice subnet, and automatically re-allocates the network resources ahead of the network issues surfaced. 13

Chapter 5.  O-RAN Reference Slicing Architecture 1 
This section provides O-RAN reference slicing architecture along with the high level roles and responsibilities of O-RAN 2 
network functions.  3 
 4

Figure 2: O-RAN Reference Slicing Architecture 6 
 7 
O-RAN reference slicing architecture includes slice management functions along with O-RAN architectural components. 8 
As O-RAN’s general principle is to be as compliant as possible with 3GPP architecture, these slice management functions 9 
are 3GPP defined NSMF and NSSMF with extensions for O-RAN network functions. Various deployment options of the 10 
location of NSMF and NSSMF have been presented in [14] and more detailed architectural implementation options for 11 
SMOs including NFV-MANO and ONAP are being presented in Appendix A. 12 
 13 
5.1 Non-RT RIC  14 
The fundamental role of the Non-RT RIC in O-RAN slicing architecture is to gather long term slice related data through 15 
interaction with the SMO framework and apply AI/ML based approaches interworking with the Near-RT RIC to provide 16 
innovative RAN slicing use cases. For this purpose, Non -RT RIC should be aware of RAN slices and their respective 17 
SLAs through SMO. In addition, Non-RT RIC may retrieve enrichment information from 3rd party applications enabling 18 
advanced RAN slicing technology to be applied in O-RAN framework.  19 
In order to construct AI/ML models to be deployed in the Near-RT RIC, Non-RT RIC retrieves slice specific performance 20 
metrics, configuration parameters and required attributes of the RAN slice subnets from the SMO framework. Complex 21

5.2 Near-RT RIC 7 
Near-RT RIC is the component which enables near-real-time RAN slice optimization through execution of slicing related 8 
xApps and communicating necessary parameters to O-CU and O-DU through E2 interface. Deployed xApps may utilize 9 
either AI/ML based models o r other control schemes which can further be guided by A1 policies that are generated by 10 
Non-RT RIC.  11 
In order to drive sliced RAN resources properly, Near-RT RIC should have the knowledge of existing RAN slices as well 12 
as their requirements. This informat ion will be received through O1 interface during provisioning of RAN slices.  13 
Therefore similar to Non-RT RIC, Near-RT RIC will be aware of RAN slice subnets through O-RAN specific information 14 
models and provisioning procedures.    15 
In O-RAN slicing architecture, configuration of slice resources on E2 nodes can be achieved through slow loop with O1 16 
configuration and through fast loop with E2 configuration. This architecture enables advanced slicing use cases such as 17 
RAN slice SLA assurance and further enhances 3GPP slicing capability without mis -alignment. While Near-RT RIC is 18 
capable for fast -loop configuration, slicing related O1 configuration, such as RRM policy information  sent to O -CU, 19 
configured by the SMO framework will be taken into account. Moreover, slice specific near -RT performance data will 20 
be monitored through E2 interface which needs proper PM mechanisms between E2 nodes and Near-RT RIC as well. 21 
5.3 O-RAN Central Unit (O-CU) 22 
O-CU, which includes a single O -CU-CP and possibly multiple O -CU-UP(s), which are communicating through E1 23 
interface, needs to support slicing features as defined by 3GPP. Depending on slice requirements, O-CU-UP can be shared 24 
across slices or a specific instance of O -CU-UP can be instantiated  per slice . On top of 3GPP slicing fea tures, O-RAN 25 
further enhances slicing through the utilization of E2 interface and the assistance of Near-RT RIC dynamic slice 26 
optimizations along with the enhanced O1 interface to support additional slice configuration parameters. 27 
O-CU stacks, which are th e upper layer protocols of the RAN stack, should be slice aware and execute slice specific 28 
resource allocation and isolation strategies. These stacks are initially configured through O1 interface based on the slice 29 
specific requirements and then dynamically updated through E2 interface via Near-RT RIC for various slicing use cases.  30 
Based on the PM requests from SMO and Near-RT RIC, O-CU may need to generate and send specific PMs through O1 31 
and E2 interfaces respectively, where the PMs can be used for slice performance monitoring and slice SLA assurance 32 
purposes. 33 
5.4 O-RAN Distributed Unit (O-DU) 34 
O-DU, which runs the lower layer protocols of RAN stack, should support slice specific resource allocation strategies as 35 
well. Based on the initial O1 configuration of PRB allocation levels along with O-CU directives over F1 interface and the 36 
dynamic guidance received from Near-RT RIC over E2 interface, MAC layer needs to allocate and isolate relevant PRBs 37 
to specific slices.  38

5.5 A1 Interface 4 
A1, which is the interface between the Non -RT RIC and the Near -RT RIC, supports policy management, ML model 5 
management and enrichment information services [11]. These three services will be utilized for various slicing use cases, 6 
such as slice SLA assurance. Policy management will be used by Non-RT RIC to send slice specific (e.g. S-NSSAI based) 7 
policies to guide Near-RT RIC with slice resource allocations and slice specific control activities, as well as to receive 8 
slice specific policy feedback for the policies deployed on the Near-RT RIC. 9 
For the use cases that make use of external enrichment data or where Non-RT RIC produces enrichment information, A1 10 
enrichment interface will be used to send slice specific enrichment data to Near-RT RIC. 11 
It should be noted that slice specific A1 policies are not persistent (do not survive the restart of Near-RT RIC) and while 12 
they may take precedence over O1 slice specific configurations, they should be aligned and not deviate significantly from 13 
O1 configurations.  14 
Note: It is intended to add examples for the usage of A1 services for slicing use cases in the next version of this document. 15 
5.6 E2 Interface 16 
E2, which is the interface between the Near -RT RIC and the E2 nodes, supports E2 primitives (Report, Insert, Control 17 
and Policy) to control the services exposed by E2 nodes [12]. These primitives will be used by slice specific applications 18 
(xApps) to drive E2 nodes’ slice configurations and slice specific behaviour, such as slice based radio resource 19 
management, radio resource allocation s, MAC scheduling policies and other  configuration parameters used by various 20 
RAN protocol stacks.  21 
E2 will be used to configure and receive slice specific reports and performance data from E2 nodes. These reports may 22 
include 3GPP defined slice specific PM s (such as PRB utilization, average delay, etc.  [7]) and new PMs that can be 23 
defined by O-RAN to support various slicing use cases. 24 
Note: It is intended to add examples for the usage of E2 primitives for slicing use cases in the next version of this document. 25 
5.7 O1 Interface 26 
O1, which is the interface between O-RAN managed elements and the management entity as defined in [9], will be used 27 
to configure slice specific parameters of O-RAN nodes based on the service requirements of the slice. 3GPP have defined 28 
some of the slice specific information models in TS 28.541 [6], including the RRM policy attributes to provide the ratio 29 
of PRBs and the split of these PRBs among slices. To support O-RAN slicing use cases and their requirements, 3GPP 30 
information models may be extended and additional information models may be defined to capture slice profiles and slice 31 
specific configuration parameters, which will be carried over O1 interface as well. 32 
O1 will also be used to configure  and gather slice specific performance metrics and slice specific faults from O -RAN 33 
nodes. 34 
Note: It is intended to add examples for the usage of O1 for the configuration, performance and fault management of 35 
slicing use cases in the next version of this document. 36

5.8 O2 Interface 1 
O2, which is the interface between the SMO and O-Cloud as introduced in [13], will be used for life cycle management  2 
of virtual O-RAN network functions. As part of RAN NSSI creation and provisioning, RAN NSSMF, in interaction with 3 
SMO, triggers the instantiation of necessary O-RAN functions (such as Near-RT RIC, O-CU-CP, O-CU-UP and O-DU) 4 
based on slice requirements. After the creation of RAN NSSI,  NSSMF in interaction with SMO can execute NSSI 5 
modification and NSSI deletion procedures. 6 
Since Non-RT RIC is part of SMO and would be instantiated along with other SMO functions, O2 is not expected to be 7 
used for lifecycle management of Non-RT RIC. 8
