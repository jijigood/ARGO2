3 Scope 1 
This Technical Specification has been produced by the O-RAN Alliance. The document is intended to describe 2 
best practices for O -RAN transport Network Element (TNE) management interfaces. Beyond the solutions 3 
described in this document, other management  solutions may be a dequate for transport network element 4 
management and can be considered in future versions of this document. In this document, f irst, generic 5 
requirements are given in section 6 and 7 for these interfaces and then informative content in section 8 and 9 6 
on device, network and service interfaces. 7 
 8 
This document uses information published by O-RAN, 3GPP, IEEE, ITU-T, ONF, IETF, CableLabs, NGMN 9 
and many other standard bodies and industry associations. 10 
 11 
Phase one of this document in early 2021 covered: 12 
• Microwave,  13 
• TDM-PON,  14 
• optical transport (fronthaul and mid-and backhaul) 15 
 16

• IP/Ethernet for O-RAN slicing phase 1 18

 20

 26

6 Modelling Schema 1 
YANG (RFC 7950 [1]) is widely adopted in the industry to model equipment at the different transport layers. 2 
For consistency it makes sense to use the same schema for all layers. YANG therefore is the recommended 3 
schema for modelling in O-RAN. 4 
 5 
[R6.1]: model MUST use YANG as schema. 6 
 7 
It is desirable to define an open and complete interface, largely independent of specific supplier’s 8 
implementations. Complete here means Configuration Management (inventory and provisioning), Failure and 9 
Performance Management, as well as life c ycle management. If there is a need to augment the interface, an 10 
augmentation mechanism needs to be defined, as well as a clear modular structure of the model. 11 
 12 
[R6.2]: model SHOULD be under open-source license. 13 
 14 
[R6.3]: models MUST have modular structure with clearly defined augmentation mechanism. 15 
 16 
[R6.4] model selected MUST be independent of specific supplier attributes or features. 17 
 18 
[R6.5]: vendor specific augmentations MUST be temporary only (temporary SHALL be defined here as less 19 
than one year). 20 
 21 
[D6.6]: model SHOULD support backwards compatibility and future proof enhancement solution. 22

7 Device Communication Protocols 1 
This section defines the communication Protocols for the different applications. 2 
 3 
7.1 Configuration Management  4 
For configuration management (CM), the current industry standard used in conjunction with the YANG 5 
schema is the NETCONF protocol (RFC6241 [2]). Security for NETCONF can either be configured over SSH 6 
[3] or TLS [4]. Other working groups in O-RAN use NETCONF over TLS, but some industry implementations 7 
use SSH. A single way for NETCONF security is recommended. The security focus group inside O-RAN has 8 
published their specifications [5]. 9 
 10 
[R7.1]: MUST support NETCONF over SSH, according to [5], subsection 2.1. 11 
[D7.2]: SHOULD support NETCONF over TLS. 12 
[R7.2]: in case NETCONF over TLS is being supported, TLS SHALL be supported according to [5], 13 
subsection 2.2 14 
 15 
7.2 Fault and Performance management 16 
For Fault a nd Performance Management (FM/PM), there are multiple implementations in the industry, 17 
dependent on models used and technologies supported. One option is to use NETCONF also for FM/PM, 18 
which has the advantage of only having to implement a single protocol on a network element. Another popular 19 
method for retrieving PM/FM is streaming telemetry. The most widely used protocol there is OpenConfig’s 20 
streaming telemetry using the gNMI protocol  [6]. Another emerging standard is streaming using VES (VNF 21 
event streaming), defined by OPNFV and ONAP [7], which is already used in O-RAN. 22 
While it would be desirable to have a single retrieval mechanism for FM/PM for all transport layers, reality is 23 
that the industry has implemented different standards and it may be needed to set this on a per-technology basis 24 
inside O-RAN. The following section looks at the different transport mechanisms and some of the models and 25 
protocols used in the industry. While there may be many more models being defined, the work here 26 
concentrates on models that have been implemented in production networks and devices. Implementations in 27 
mediators and production field trial are acceptable as well. 28 
 29 
[R7.3]: Fault management MUST support NETCONF, gNMI or VES streaming. 30 
[R7.4]: Performance management MUST support NETCONF, gNMI or VES streaming. 31 
 32 
7.3 Life cycle management 33 
Unfortunately, very few models in the industry today support life cycle management, such as firmware or 34 
software upgrades, database backup and restore or any other such features required during the life cycle of the 35 
equipment in the network. That drives the need for yet another interface, often manufacturer proprietary and 36 
proprietary software tools to perform those functions. 37 
Inside O-RAN, there has already been some work for software management and zero touch provisioning. There 38 
is also zero-touch provisioning work in progress at BBF. 39 
 40 
[R7.5]: Model MUST support life cycle management functions, supported with a single NETCONF interface. 41 
 42 
[D7.6]: The model SHOULD support full-service lifecycle as for example specified in the ONAP Architecture, 43 
ITU G8052.1 or the MEF 22. 3.1 Transport Service Orchestration and Lifecycle Service Orchestration  44 
Framework. 45

 3 
A comparison was done between models which can be used to represent microwave systems: on one side the 4 
ONF Core Model version 1.4  [8], along with the different technology specific packages (PACs) defined in 5 
TR532 version 2.0  (still a draft version) [9] and on the other side the ietf -microwave-radio-link model 6 
(RFC8561) [10] and other IETF models. 7 
 8 
For describing equipment information, the compared models have a somewhat similar approach: 9 
• ietf-hardware abstracts any type of component (while the type is an extensible enumeration), while the 10 
core-model differentiates in each equipment instance between connectors and contained -holders 11 
(while the type is just a string , so a flexibility for vendors, but can result in different approaches for 12 
different vendors), 13 
• ietf-hardware can contain sensor data, which is rather abstract and lets vendors build any type of data, 14 
which can result in different approaches for different v endors, while the ONF Core Model does not 15 
include sensor data, 16 
• ONF Core Model differentiates between actual and expected equipment, which is not the case in the 17 
ietf-hardware, 18 
• ietf-hardware provides some information about revisions (hardware, software etc.) and hardware 19 
information, while the ONF Core Model provides a lot more details about the hardware (physical, 20 
mechanical, spatial, environmental etc.), 21 
• both models offer parent-child relationships between the equipment instances for describing an entire 22 
hierarchy. 23 
 24 
For describing interfaces and their attributes, the comparison focused on ietf-interfaces model (augmented by 25 
ietf-microwave-radio-link) and the ONF Core Model augmented by different technology specific PACs): 26

• the ietf-interfaces are only augmented to provide air-interfaces (microwave specific parameters), while 1 
the ONF Core Model is augmented to provide different types of interfaces (air -interfaces, ethernet 2

problems which are missing from the ietf-interfaces could be covered by the ietf-alarms yang model 8 
• both ietf-interfaces and the ONF Core Model support layering of interfaces (higher and lower layer, 9 
and respectively client-server relationships) 10 
• ietf-interfaces modelling is compliant with the NMDA (Network Management Datastore Architecture 11 
– RFC8342), section 5.3, while the augmentations for the ONF Core Model are not. To be more precise, 12 
section 5.3 of RFC8342 states that the operational datastore is a read-only datastore which consists of 13 
all “config false” and “config true” attributes defined in the yang models (while the original 14 
NETCONF specification indicated that the operational datastore should contain only “config false” 15 
parameters). The advantage of the new approach is that it enables the ability of nodes to expose all 16 
operational settings without needing to replicate definitions in the data model. The modelling of ietf-17 
interfaces complies with this architecture, while the core -model defines different attributes for 18 
configuration and for status of a particular configuration.  19 
 20 
For group definitions, the comparison focused on the ietf -microwave-radio-link and augmentations for the 21 
ONF Core Model: 22 
• the ietf-microwave-radio-link describes XPIC and MIMO groups, while augmentation of core-model 23 
describe also ALIC (Adjacent Link Interference Calculation) groups 24 
Both models describe radio link protection groups, with the difference that in the ietf model the type of group 25 
is an extensible enumeration, while in the ONF Core Model the type is just a string. 26 
 27 
For FW/SW Management, mature YANG models do not exist yet. Inside the ONF there are ongoing 28 
discussions for providing such capabilities for microwave systems, but a draft YANG model is yet to be 29 
provided. 30 
 31 
From the user management and certificate handling perspective, a generic model that allows a centralized 32 
control does not exist yet. Each individual node can nevertheless rely of the standard NETCONF Server models 33 
for that purpose, there is no need for having a microwave specific model. 34 
 35 
Both the ONF Core Model with its technology specific augmentations and the IETF models, which were part 36 
of the comparison, could be used to describe the management interface of a microwave system. Both mode ls 37 
have gaps in their air -interface and in the network interface attributes, which need to be communicated to 38 
relevant standard groups for future consideration. The ONF model strives for a complete set of harmonized 39 
attributes, in order to prevent need for proprietary augmentations. The IETF model focuses on a smaller set of 40 
attributes which describe an air -interface, but which would cover a big amount of use cases, while allowing 41 
proprietary augmentations for attributes which are not yet considered in the model. The IETF core model is 42 
designed with flexibility in mind taking advantage of the wealth of features and models developed across the 43 
IETF/IEEE std organizations especially the one related to models supporting many packet -based features, 44 
while maintaining strict backwards compatibility to its Core Model and YANG versioning modules that require 45 
updating a published YANG module to follow the rules in clause 11 of the IETF RFC 7950 [1] prohibiting 46 
Non-Backwards Compatible (NBC) changes. Models describing other types of interfaces present in microwave 47 
systems (wire interfaces, ethernet, VLAN etc.) exist both in ONF (proposed as augmentations for the ONF 48 
Core Model) and in the IETF, but they were not part of the gap analysis. 49

8.2 Optical access  1 
The optical access refers to an optical fiber equipment and infrastructure lying between an optical termination 2 
(here, typically the antenna cell site) and the central office (aggregation node). Optical line termination (OLT) 3 
and Optical Network Unit (ONU) are the active  equipment which serve as the service provider endpoints at 4 
each end of the access passive optical network . The OLT shelf supports two different medium connectivity 5 
based on specific cards & interfaces.  6 
i. The first one is the PON (Passive Optical network) which is used for FTTHome based on PtMP 7 
optical infrastructure (also named ODN optical Distribution network). Several PON generations 8 
are available: G -PON ( Gigabit capable Passive Optical Network ), XGS-PON (10 Gigabit 9 
symmetrical capable Passive Optical Network) , and the coming HS -PON (High-Speed capable 10 
Passive Optical Network).  11 
ii. The second supported connectivity at OLT is Ethernet PtP based on regular transceivers. This PtP 12 
interfaces are used for business and the existing mobile backhaul. 13 
So an OLT is an equipment w hich supports these two types of access interfaces to reach customers, a 14 
backplane, one or several uplinks port to reach metropolitan nodes and a management. 15 
 16 
Ideally, the management plane of an OLT should natively be vendor agnostic. If it were the case, all operational 17 
procedures would be defined once and used and reused for all the equipment of a given type. Over the last 18 
decades, attempts for standardizing the management interfaces of  the network elements have been largely 19 
unsuccessful.  Consequently, proprietary management systems are commonly used. This obliges the operator 20 
to adapt its OSS (Operations Support System) to different proprietary management interfaces, which is time 21 
consuming and requires quite a significant effort in terms of OSS development. But for this new decade, things 22 
are changing. Thanks to the emergence of SDN and the lobbying efforts from network service providers to 23 
better automate its operational processes, the introduction of software in the telecommunication industry gives 24 
SDAN (Software- Defined Access Network) solutions and opportunity allowing telcos to manage the access 25 
nodes themselves as well as create new kinds of orchestration at the service layer. Last but not least, the SNMP 26 
(Simple Network Management Protocol) management protocol and its associated data-modelling language in 27 
use today are pretty old and were inherently not well designed to comply with data -models promoted by the 28 
standards. SNMP is a lso currently used for pulling statistics and counters from the network elements 29 
(performance monitoring, troubleshooting). However, SNMP is not well designed for massive data collections. 30 
NETCONF (Network Configuration Protocol) interfaces and YANG (Yet A nother Next Generation) data -31 
models for PON are now proposed (cf. IETF RFC 6241 [2] and BBF TR385 [13], respectively). They are 32 
gaining some momentum so we forecast that they will replace SNMP. The coming decade will correspond to 33 
a network migration and o peration with natively NETCONF/YANG access equipment leveraging on a clear 34 
separation between the network node management layer and the service design and management layer. 35 
Two references implementation are proposed for SDAN by: 36 
i. Open Network Foundation with SDN-Enabled Broadband Access (SEBA) including Virtual OLT 37 
Hardware Abstraction (VOLTHA) 38 
ii. Broad Band Forum with Cloud Central Office (Cloud CO)  including BBF’s Access Abstraction 39 
Layer (BAA). Cloud CO is standardized, as for the use cases which are propose d under BBF’s 40 
“TR-416 [11]: Cloud CO Use Cases and Scenarios”. 41

It is a converged end to end packet switched infrastructure, beginning at the cell site, located in the edge of 5 
the access part of the transport layer, and stretching to the core of the transport layer. The packet switching 6 
TNEs are IP/MPLS or SRv6 QoS enabled, high capacity, low latency devices interconnected by point-to-7

point fibres or via a WDM infrastructure.  It incorporates data centers suitably placed across the transport 9 
network infrastructure to support virtual and physical NFs associated with mobile and fixed services but also 10 
potentially the placement of “Application Functions” associated with value-add services and customer 11 
specific application. 12 
 13 
The logical architecture is based on a common underlay packet switching infrastructure based on either 14 
MPLS or SRv6 overlaid with a L2 / L3 service infrastructure (VPNs) that uses the capabilities of the 15 
underlay packet switched network to support the mobiles interfaces.  16 
The underlay packet switching infrastructure provides basic network services such as any-to-any 17 
connectivity between TNEs, scaling, fast convergence, shortest path and traffic engineered forwarding, 18 
packet-based Quality of Service (QoS) and timing.  19 
 20 
The service layer supports native Ethernet services, using EVPN technology and IP VPN services, using MP-21 
BGP based L3VPNs. These services can utilise the facilities offered by the underlay packet switched 22 
infrastructure to support the different mobile interfaces in an appropriate fashion. Where possible transport 23 
services are built on an end-to-end basis without intermediate stitching / switching points within the transport 24 
infrastructure. This approach has been taken to minimise the transport service orchestration overhead. 25

8.4.1 Provisioning and managing a packet switched infrastructure 27 
There are two main phases associated with the provision and management of a packet switched 28 
infrastructure: 29 
• Building and managing the basic infrastructure. This is a process that occurs when the network is 30 
first built and later when new TNEs are installed or when TNEs are modified or upgraded. It covers 31 
the basic set-up of devices, the configuration of the management infrastructure, set-up of interfaces, 32 
core QoS and routing protocols. This function is undertaken by the network operations team and is 33 
normally largely transparent to the users of the network. 34 
• Services configuration and management. In the architecture outlined in Xhaul Packet Switched 35 
Architectures and solutions (O-RAN.WG9.XPSAAS-v03 [26]) services, such as a transport slice, are 36 
MP-BGP based on L2VPNs and L3VPNs. The construction of a service involves multiple actions 37 
and components such as building VPNs, Edge QoS, service level OAM and potentially mapping 38 
services to underlay policies. In contrast to the basic infrastructure this is an on-going process and 39

8.4.2.1.3 Native YANG models 7 
All vendors typically have their own YANG models which describe their NOS capabilities in their entirety, 8 
including both standards-based features and vendor proprietary features.  These models should (but do not 9 
always) offer the guarantee that for the given vendor platform all available features can be automated in a 10 
structured way by an automation system. One advantage to using all native models can be to avoid the 11 
complexity of taking incomplete standards-based models and trying to create augmentations for different 12 
vendors by cherry picking pieces of the native models. On the other hand, the advantage of taking standards-13 
based models and augmenting the missing pieces with native models can create synergies in network 14 
automation software between different platforms. 15 
Where working with cutting edge features the reality is that often the only approach practical is that of using 16 
a vendor-native model until such a time as open alternatives are updated. An additional area where 17 
standardization is problematic is the area of QoS. While there are efforts in both IETF and Openconfig to 18 
create standard models, it has proved very difficult (at least in IETF) to agree on certain aspects of this 19 
model. One of the challenges is the fact that vendor implementations vary considerably in the approaches to 20 
implementing QoS technologies, largely because this has been an area where vendors have deliberately 21 
sought to gain competitive advantage through their individual innovation efforts.   22

When it comes to Performance Monitoring, both Openconfig and IETF models expose almost the same 8

pkts” as the sum of the unicast, multicast, broadcast and error. Openconfig also defines explicitly the received 17

8.4.3  Conclusion 1 
In general, there is much work that has already been done to standardize the interfaces used between the IP 2 
packet network controller and the IP nodes on the O -RAN network.  When it comes to the configuration 3 
interfaces there would appear currently to be more comprehensive coverage with OpenConfig YANG models 4 
compared to those that are available in the IETF, this said neither option provides all features that we expect 5 
would be required for a fully automated O-RAN network.  6 
This considered, while it is recommended to adopt standard models as t his should help with interoperability 7 
and API stability, it is acknowledged that proprietary extensions or use of native models may be required  in 8 
cases where a single YANG model is not available that provides the full range of features required for a 9 
particular use case.  Where working with cutting edge features the reality is that often the only approach 10 
practical is that of using a vendor -native model until such a time as open alternatives are updated.  The 11 
advantage of taking standards-based models and augmenting the missing pieces with native models can create 12 
synergies in network automation software between different platforms. 13 
For the telemetry interfaces, there are no gaps in IETF or OpenConfig and both, gRPC or NETCONF can be 14 
used for retrieval. 15 
In the control plane there are mature IETF standards which are widely deployed and should be leveraged as 16 
fully as possible. 17

9.1.1 History / modeling concepts 26 
Since 3GPP Rel-10 3GPP SA5 has been working on the concept of Federated Network Models (FNM) based 27 
on the following principles (see clause 6.3 of 3GPP TR 32.828): 28 
- Not one authority (e.g. SDO) can be responsible for the development, maintenance and evolution of the 29 
whole model.  Different organizations are responsible for the development, maintenance and evolution 30 
of their own domain specific model 31 
- Operators may use the whole FMC model or part of the FMC model depending on their own business 32 
cases. 33 
- Vendors can supply products using part of the FMC model depending on their own business cases. 34

- The model needs to hold thousands of inter-related modelled entities.  Different versions of modelled 1 
entities can co-exist in the model. 2 
Essential FNM features include the ability to reference classes of "external" models, ability to import models 3 
designed elsewhere, independence of tool and platform, independence of solution technology and access 4 
protocol design. The key aspect of FNM approach is partitioning of the overall model into fragments with 5 
clear rules for inter-relationship of fragments. The advantages of FNM motivating its introduction 6 
documented in clause 6.3.2 of 32.828 are: 7 
- It removes the need to keep the evolution of various fragments in synchrony.  For example, it is a valid 8 
model implementation where one fragment has evolved (requiring new solution) while other fragment 9 
remained unchanged (does not require new solution). 10 
- Domain experts (e.g. LTE experts) can focus his design on its fragments and (can, if wanted to) be 11 
ignorant of contents of other fragments (e.g., mobile backhaul networks experts). 12 
In 3GPP Rel-11 a new set of NRM specifications (28.xxx series replacing 32.xxx series) has been published 13 
by 3GPP. It includes the "Federated Network Information Model (FNIM) Umbrella Information Model 14 
(UIM)" jointly published by 3GPP (as TS 28.620) and TMF (as FNIM). The structure of all 3GPP NRM 15 
follows the FNM approach: 16 
- at the root, there is a number of classes that form the UIM (see clause 4.1 of 3GPP TS 28.620). These 17 
classes are represented in UML and are implementation neutral views in that they only capture the 18 
semantics of the model from both a purpose neutral and purpose specific perspective. They do not 19 
include syntax or representation of the information in a system or on-the-wire between systems; and 20 
do not relate to the protocol used to create/delete/read/write/modify the NM information. 21 
- Various SDOs and organizations are expected to use the UIM classes for definition of 22 
Domain/Technology-specific model classes. This procedure will maximize the probability of the 23 
domain/technology specific concrete classes (from various SDOs) being semantically consistent, a 24 
necessary characteristic for FMC NM purposes. 25 
 - 3GPP specifies Generic NRM IRP (3GPP TS 28.622) where it defines abstract classes aligned with 26 
UIM and other NRM IRPs such as E-UTRAN NRM IRP (3GPP TS 28.652), 5G NRM (3GPP TS 27 
28.541) where it defines concrete classes. 28 
9.1.2 5G Slicing 29 
Starting from 3GPP Rel-15 a new management framework - Services Based Management Architecture 30 
(SBMA) (see 3GPP TS 28.533 for details) has been introduced. SBMA eliminates the need for normatively 31 
prescribing functional blocks as Management Functions (MnF) with mandatory sets of functional 32 
requirements and shifts the focus of standardization to definitions of Open APIs as Management Services 33 
(MnS). According to SBMA concepts, there is no restriction on what entities are allowed to consume MnS 34 
and what entities are mandated to produce the MnS - these details are left up to Operator's deployment 35 
preferences. In SBMA the management of slicing is not separated from the management of 5G System - 36 
Network Slicing is just a feature (among many others) supported by 5GS and by 5G management and 37 
orchestration. 38 
3GPP TS 28.533 clause 4.1 defines the fundamental building block of the SBMA as the MnS. A MnS is a set 39 
of offered capabilities for management and orchestration of network and services. The entity producing an 40 
MnS is called MnS producer. The entity consuming an MnS is called MnS consumer. An MnS provided by 41 
an MnS producer can be consumed by any entity with appropriate authorization and authentication.  42 
An MnS producer offers its services via a standardized service interface composed of individually specified 43 
MnS components. A MnS is specified using different independent components. A concrete MnS is 44 
composed of at least two of these components. Three different component types are defined, called MnS 45 
component type A (a group of management operations and/or notifications that is agnostic with regard to the 46 
entities managed), MnS component type B (management information represented by information models 47 
representing the managed entities) and MnS component type C (performance and fault information of the 48 
managed entity). 49 
3GPP TS 28.541 (annex L) illustrates the role of the MnS component type B (3GPP NRM) in the slice 50 
fulfilment and assurance processes, and the relationship between GSMA GST, ServiceProfile and 51 
SliceProfile defined in clause 6 of TS 28.541. 52

9.3 Uniform network model approach 20 
The Service Management and Orchestration (SMO) framework inside the O-RAN Architecture is responsible 21 
for the Operations, Administration and Maintenance (OAM) functions. For facilitating these functions, the 22 
SMO should communicate with the RAN network functions, as well as with the Transport Network network 23 
functions. This implies different YANG models for different technologies (e.g., Packet, Optical, Microwave, 24 
etc.), and even different YANG models from different SDOs for the same technology domain. It wo uld be 25 
beneficial if all this complexity would not be transferred through the Northbound Interface (NBI) to the other 26 
SMO components/applications (some examples might include a Transport Slice Controller – which handles 27 
network slicing use cases, or AI/ML applications doing, for example, predictive maintenance, or other possible 28 
Closed Loop end -to-end use cases, etc.). This is possible by adding an abstraction and model 29 
translation/mapping layer that would expose a uniform network model to other SMO compone nts (NBI) and 30 
that would communicate southbound with different technologies having different YANG models. This layer 31 
would also need a method to add some Enrichment information (be it from a database or some other means) 32 
that would express, for example, links between domains, in cases where the network functions themselves are 33 
not able to express them. Figure 19 shows how such an abstraction layer could fit into the archi tecture of the 34 
SMO. 35
