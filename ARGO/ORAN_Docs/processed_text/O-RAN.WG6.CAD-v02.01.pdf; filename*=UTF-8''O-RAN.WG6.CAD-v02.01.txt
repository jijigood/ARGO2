Figure 2:  Major Components Related to the Orchestration and Cloudification Effort 174
A key principle is the decoupling of RAN hardware and software for all components including near-RT RIC, O-CU (O-175
CU-CP and O -CU-UP), O -DU, and O -RU, and th e deployment of software components on commodity server 176
architectures supplemented with programmable accelerators where necessary.  177
Key characteristics of cloud architectures which we will reference in this document are:  178
a) Decoupling of hardware from software.  This aims to improve flexibility and choice for operators by decoupling 179
selection and deployment of hardware infrastructure from software selection,  180
b) Standardization of hardware specifications across software implementations , to simplify ph ysical deployment 181
and maintenance.  This aims to promote the availability of a multitude of software implementation choices for a 182
given hardware configuration.   183
c) Sharing of hardware.  This aims to promote the availability of a multitude of hardware implementation choices 184
for a given software implementation. 185
d) Flexible instantiation and lifecycle management through orchestration automation.   This aims to reduce 186
deployment and ongoing maintenance costs by promoting simplification and automation throughout the 187
hardware and software lifecycle through common chassis specifications and standardized orchestration 188
interfaces.   189
This document will define various deployment scenarios that can be supported by the O -RAN specifications and are of 190
either current or relative ly near -term interest.  Each scenario is identified by a specific grouping of functionality a t 191
different key locations (Cell Site, Edge Cloud, and Regional C loud, which will be defined shortly), and an identification 192
of whether functionality  at a given loc ation is provided by a n O-RAN Physical NF based solution where software and 193
hardware are tightly integrated and sharing a single identity, or by a cloud architecture that meets the above requirements. 194
The scope of this work clearly includes supporting all 5G technologies, i.e. E-UTRA and NR with both EPC-based Non-195
Standalone (NSA) and 5GC architectures. This implies that cloud/orchestration aspects of NSA (E -UTRA) are also 196
supported. However, this version primarily addresses 5G SA deployments. 197
This technical report examines the constraints that drive a specific solution, and discuss the hierarchical properties of each 198
solution, including a rough scale of the size of each cloud and a sense of the number of sub clouds expected to be served 199
by a higher cloud.  Figure 3 shows as example of how multiple cell sites feed into a smaller number of Edge Clouds, and 200
how in turn multiple Edge Clouds feed into a Regional Cloud.  For a given scenario, the Logical Functions are distributed 201
in a certain way among each type of cloud, and the “cardinality” of the different functions will be discussed.   202
This has implications on the processing power needed in each type of cloud, as well as implications on the environmental 203
requirements.  This document will also discuss considerations of hardware chassis and components that are reasonable in 204
each scenario, and the implications of managing such a cloud.   205

 Multiple Operating Systems are expected to be supported; e.g., open source Ubuntu, CentOS Linux , or 219

 Management of a cloudified RAN introduces some new management considerations, because the mapping 221

that is chosen.  Thus, management of aspects that ar e related to platform aspects rather than RAN functional 223
aspects need to be designed with flexibility in mind from the start.  For example, logging of physical functions, 224
scale out actions, and survivability considerations are affected.   225
 These management considerations are introduced in this document, but management documents will 226
address the solutions. 227
 The transport layer will be discussed, but only to the extent that it affects the architecture and design of the 228
network.  For example, the chosen L1 technology may affect the performance of transport.  As another example, 229
the use of a Fronthaul Gateway will affect economics as well as the placement options of certain Network 230
Functions.  And of course, t he existence of L2 switches in a cloud platform deploym ent will be required for 231

4.2 Degree of Openness 421
In theory, every architecture component could be open in every sense imaginable, but in practice it is likely that different 422
components will have varying degrees of openness due to economic and other implementation considerations.  Some 423
factors are significantly affected by the deployment scenario; for example, what might be viable in an indoor deployment 424
might not be viable in an outdoor deployment.   425
Increasing degrees of openness for a n O-RAN Physical Network Function or O-RAN Cloudified Network Function(s) 426
are: 427
A. Interfaces among Network Functions are open; e.g., E2, F1, and Open Fronthaul are used. Therefore, Network 428
Functions in different O-RAN Physical NFs/clouds from different vendors can interconnect. 429
B. In addition to having open c onnections as described above , the chassis of servers in a cloud are open and can 430
accept blades/sleds from multiple vendors.  However, the blades/sleds have RAN software that is not decoupled 431
from the hardware. 432
C. In addition to having open connections and an open chassis, a specific blade/sled uses software that is decoupled 433
from the hardware.  In this scenario, the software could be from one supplier, the blade/sled could be from another, 434
and the chassis could be from another.   435
Categories A and B have O-RAN Physical NFs/clouds, while Category C is an open solution that we are calling a n O-436
Cloud, and is subject to the cloudification discussion and requirements. 437
In this document, the degree of openness for each O-RAN Physical NF/cloud can vary by scenario. The question of which 438
Network Functions should be split vs. combined, and the degree of openness in e ach one, is addressed in the discussion 439
of scenarios.  440

Almost all Scenarios to be discussed in this document involve a degree of centralization of O -DU.  In this section it is  607

Managing O-DU in equipment at individual cell sites (via on-site BBUs today) has multiple challenges, including: 611
 If changes are needed at a site (e.g., adding radio carriers), then adding equipment is a coarse -grained activity – 612

increment of additional capacity might result in poor utilization and thereby prevent expansion at that site.   614
 Cell sites are in many separate locations, and each requires  establishment and maintenance of an acceptable 615
environment for the equipment.  In turn this requires separate visits for any physical operations.  616
