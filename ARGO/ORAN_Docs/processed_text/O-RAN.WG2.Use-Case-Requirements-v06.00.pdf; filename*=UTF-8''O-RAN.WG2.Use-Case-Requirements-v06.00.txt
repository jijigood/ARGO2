Chapter 3 Use cases 1
3.1 Use case 1: Traffic steering use case 2
This use case provides the motivation, description, and requirements for traffic steering use case, allowing operators to 3
specify different objectives for traffic management such as optimizing the network/UE performance, or achieving 4
balanced cell load. 5
3.1.1 Background and goal of the use case 6
5G systems will support many different combinations of access technologies namely; LTE (licensed band), NR (licensed 7
band), NR -U (unlicensed band), Wi -Fi (unlicensed band). Several different multi -access deployment scenarios are 8
possible with 5GC to support wide variety of applications and satisf y the spectrum requirements of different service 9
providers; 10
- Carrier aggregation between licensed band NR (Primary Cell) and NR-U (Secondary Cell) 11
- Dual connectivity between licensed band NR (Primary Cell) and NR-U (Secondary Cell) 12
- Dual connectivity between licensed band LTE (Primary Cell) and NR-U (Secondary Cell) 13
 14
The rapid traffic growth and multiple frequency bands utilized in a commercial network make it challenging to steer the 15
traffic in a balanced distribution. Further in a multi -access system there i s need to switch the traffic across access 16
technologies based on changes in radio environment and application requirements and even split the traffic across multiple 17
access technologies to satisfy performance requirements. The different types of  traffic and frequency bands in a 18
commercial network make it challenging to handle the complex QoS aspects, bearer selection (Master Cell Group (MCG) 19
bearer, Secondary Cell Group (SCG) bearer, Split bearer), bearer type change for load balancing, achieving low latenc y 20
and best in class throughput in a multi -access scenario with 5GC networks (see TS 37.340 [3]). Typical controls are 21
limited to adjusting the cell reselection and handover parameters; modifying load calculations and cell priorities; and are 22
largely static in nature when selecting the type of bearers and QoS attributes.  23
Further, the RRM (Radio Resource Management) features in the existing cell ular network are all cel l-centric. Even in 24
different areas within a cell, there are variations in radio environment, such as neighboring cell coverage, signal strength, 25
interference status, etc. However, base station s based on traditional control strategies treat all UEs in a similar way and 26
are usually focused on average cell-centric performance, rather than UE-centric.  27
Such current solutions suffer from following limitations: 28
1) It is hard to adapt the RRM control to diversified scenarios and optimization objectives. 29
2) The traffic management strategy is usually passive, rarely taking advantage of capabilities to predict network and 30
UE performance. The strategy needs to consider aspects of steering, switching and splitting traffic across different access 31
technologies in a multi-access scenario. 32
3) Non-optimal traffic management, with slow response time, due to various factors such as inability to select the 33
right set of UEs for control action. This further results in non -optimal system and UE performance, such as suboptimal 34
spectrum utilization, reduced throughput and increased handover failures. 35
Based on the above reasons, the main objective of this use case is to allow operator s to flexibly configure the desired 36
optimization policies, utilize the right performance criteria, a nd leverage machine learning to  enable intelligent and 37
proactive traffic management. 38

3.1.2 Entities/resources involved in the use case 1
1) SMO (including Non-RT RIC):  2
a) Retrieve necessary performance, configuration, and other data for defining and updating policies to guide the 3
behavior of traffic management function in Near-RT RIC. For example, the policy could relate to specifying 4
different optimization objectives to guide the carrier/band preferences at per-UE or group of UE granularity. 5
b) Retrieve necessary performance, configuration, and other data for performing data statistical analysis that will 6
provide enrichment information for Near-RT RIC to assist in the traffic steering function. For example, this 7
could be an analysis method to construct radio fingerprint based on UE measurement report with 8
RSRP/RSRQ/CQI information for serving and neighbouring cells.  9
c) Support communication of policies to Near-RT RIC. 10
d) Support communication of measurement configuration parameters to RAN nodes. 11
e) Support communication of enrichment information to Near-RT RIC, e.g., radio fingerprint information, etc. 12
2) Near-RT RIC: 13
a) Support interpretation and enforcement of policies from Non-RT RIC. 14
b) Support using enrichment information to optimize control function, e.g., Near -RT RIC can use radio finger 15
print to directly predict the inter -frequency cell measurement based on the intra -frequency cell measurement 16
result to speed up the traffic steering with much reduced signaling overhead. 17
3) E2 Nodes: 18
a) Support data collection with required granularity to SMO over O1 interface. 19

3.2.1 Background and goal of the use case 1
The highly demanding 5G native applications such as Cloud VR are both bandwidth consuming and latency sensitive. 2
However, for such traffic -intensive and highly interactive applications, current semi -static QoS framework can’t 3
efficiently satisfy diversified QoE requirements especially taking into account potentially significant fluctuation of radio 4
transmission capability. It is expected that QoE estimation/prediction from application level can help deal with such 5
uncertainty and improve the efficiency of radio resources, and eventually improve user experience.  6
The main objective is to ensure QoE optimization be supported within the O -RAN architecture and its open interfaces. 7
Multi-dimensional data, e.g., user traffic data, QoE measurements, n etwork measurement report , can be acquired and 8
processed via ML algorithms to support traffic recognition, QoE prediction, and QoS enforcement decisions. ML models 9
can be trained offline and model inference will be executed in a real-time manner. Focus should be on a general solution 10
that would support any specific QoE use case (e.g. Cloud VR, video, etc.).  11

3.3 Use case 3: QoS based resource optimization 1
This use case provides the background and motivation for the O -RAN architecture to support RAN QoS based resource 2
optimization. Moreover, some high -level description and requirements over Non-RT RIC and A1 interfaces are 3
introduced.  4
3.3.1 Background and goal of the use case 5
QoS based resource optimization can used when the network has been configured to provide some kind of preferential 6
QoS for certain users. One such scenario can be related to when the network has been configured to support e2e slices. In 7
this case, the network has functionality that ensures resource isolation between slices as well as functionality to monitor 8
that slice Service Level Specifications (SLS) are fulfilled.  9
In RAN, it is the scheduler that ensures that Physical Resource Block  (PRB) resources are isolated between slices in the 10
best possible way and also that the PRB resources are used in an optimal way to best fulfill the SLS for different slices. 11
The desired default RAN behavior for slices is configured over O1. For example, t he ratio of physical resources (PRBs) 12
reserved for a slice is configured at slice creation (instantiation) over O1. Also, QoS can be configured to guide the RAN 13
scheduler how to (in real-time) allocate PRB resources to different users to best fulfill the S LS of a specific slice. In the 14
NR NRM this is described by the resource partition attribute. 15
Instantiation of a RAN sub -slice will be prepared by rigorous planning to understand to what extent deployed RAN 16
resources will be able to support RAN sub-slice SLS. Part of this procedure is to configure RAN functionality according 17
to above. With this, a default behavior of RAN is obtained that will be able to fulfill slice SLSs for most situations. 18
However, even through rigorous planning, there will be times and p laces where the RAN resources are not enough to 19
fulfill SLS given the default configuration. To understand how often (and where) this happens, the performance of a RAN 20
slice will continuously be monitored by SMO. When SMO detects a situation when RAN SLS c annot be fulfilled, Non-21
RT RIC can use A1 policies to improve the situation. To understand how to utilize A1 policies and how to resolve the 22
situation, the non RT-RIC will use additional information available in SMO.  23
Take an emergency service as an exampl e of a slice tenant. For this example, it is understood (at slice instantiation) that 24

3.4 Use case 4: Context-based dynamic handover management for 26
V2X 27
This use case provides the background, motivation, and requirements for the Context-based Dynamic HO Management 28
for V2X  use case, allowing operators to adjust radio resource allocation policies through the O -RAN architecture, 29
reducing latency and improving radio resource utilization. 30
3.4.1 Background and goal of the use case 31
V2X communication allows for numerous potential benefits such as increasing the overall road safety, reducing 32
emissions, and saving time. Part of the V2X architecture is the V2X UE (SIM + device attached to vehicle) which 33
communicates with the V2X Application Server (V2X AS). The exchanged information c omprises Cooperative 34
Awareness Messages (CAMs) (from UE to V2X AS)  [2], radio cell IDs, connection IDs, and basic radio measurements 35
(RSRP, RSPQ etc.) 36
As vehicles traverse along a highway, due to their high speed and the heterogeneous natural environment V2X UE -s are 37
handed over frequently, at times in a suboptimal way, which may cause handover (HO) anomalies: e.g., short stay, ping -38
pong, and remote cell. Such suboptimal HO sequences substantially impair the functionality of V2X applications. Since 39
HO sequences are mainly determined by the Neighbor Relation Tables (NRTs), maintained by the xNBs, there is hardly 40
room for UE-level customization. 41
This UC aims to present a method to avoid and/or resolve problematic HO scenarios by using past navigation and radio 42
statistics in order to customize HO sequences on a UE level. To this end, the AI/ML functionality that is enabled by the 43
near-RT RIC is employed. 44

this kind of SLA violation, load balancing is effective. 6

3.6 Use case 6: NSSI Resource Optimization 1
This use case provides the background, objectives, solution, and requirements for the NSSI resource optimization, an 2
rApp implemented in Non -RT RIC, which leverages AI/ML inference on slice performance measurement data to 3
determine the actions to automatically optimize the resource allocation for network slice instances.  4
 5
3.6.1 Background and goal of the use case 6
Network slicing is essential to 5G, as it enables many new services across manufacturing, autonomous driving, gaming, 7
and many more via the provision of ultra -low latency in URLLC and huge data volume in eMBB features that require 8
different or contrasting QoS require ments exploiting a shared RAN node. The goal of this use case is to ensure the 9
resources are allocated dynamically and efficiently among multiple network slices sharing the RAN node. 10
As the new 5G services have different characteristics, the network traffi c tends to be sporadic, where there may be 11
different usage pattern in terms of time, location, UE distribution, and types of applications. For example, most IoT sensor 12
applications may run during off-peak hours or weekends. Special events, such as sport games, concerts, can cause traffic 13
demand to shoot up at certain time and locations. Cars with autonomous driving capability tend to require more URLLC 14
services in the morning or afternoon rush hours in major freeways in big cities, while subscribers tend to consume eMBB 15
services to watch video streaming at night in residential areas. Therefore, NSSI resource optimization rApp trains the 16
AI/ML model, based on the huge volume of performance data collected over days, weeks, months from O -RAN nodes. 17
It then performs inference function on the  model with input measurements to predict the traffic demand patterns of 5G 18
networks in different times and locations for each network slice, and automatically optimize the resource allocation for 19
network slice instances accordingly. 20
3.6.2 Entities/resources involved in the use case 21
1) Non-RT RIC:  22
a) Receive measurements to monitor the usage of RRM resources (e.g., PRB, RRC, DRB) identified by S-NSSAI 23
from E2 nodes via the O1 interface   24
b) Perform the model training with input measurements data received from E2 nodes to create the model. 25
c) Perform the inference function on the model with the input measurements data to determine if any actions 26
should be executed to update the resources on the E2 nodes.  27
d) Configure the resources at the E2 nodes via O1 interface. 28
e) Receive notifications from E2 nodes indicating the resource re-configuration was done. 29
f) Update the O-cloud resources via the O2 interface. 30
g) Receive notifications from O-Cloud indicating the resource was updated 31
2) E2 nodes (O-CU-CP, O-CU-UP, D-DU): 32
a) Support the collections and reporting of measurements that are used to monitor the resource usage on per 33
network slice basis via the O1 interface. 34
b) Support the re -configuration of attributes to update the resources allocated to each network slice via the O1 35
interface.  36

3.7.1 Massive MIMO Grid-of-Beams Beamforming (GoB BF) Optimization 6
3.7.1.1 Background and goal of the use case 7
Massive MIMO (mMIMO) is among the key methods to increase performance and QoS in 5G networks. Capacity 8
enhancement is obtained by means of beamforming of the transmitted signals, and by spatially multiplexing data streams. 9
Beamforming can increase the received signal power and simultaneously decrease the interference generated for other 10
users, hence resulting in higher SINR and higher user throughputs. Grid-of-Beams (GoB) with the corresponding beam 11
sweeping has been introduced to allow beamforming of the control channels used during initial access as well as for data 12
transmission and reception, mainly for high frequency (but can be used also for the sub -6 GHz band) MIMO operation. 13
The physical properties of the antenna array and its possible configurations characterize  the span of the beams, namely 14
the horizontal and vertical aperture in which beamforming is supported, and therefore the coverage area and the shape of 15
the cell. mMIMO can be deployed in 5G macrocell clusters as well as in heterogeneous networks, where macrocells and 16
small cells co-exist and complement each other for better aggregated capacity and coverage. In order to obtain optimal 17
beamforming and cell resources (Tx power, PRB) configuration, one will have to look at a multi-cell environment instead 18
of a single cell. Moreover, different vendors may have different implementations in terms of the number of beams, the 19
horizontal/vertical beam widths, azimuth and elevation range, to achieve the desired coverage. In a multi -node/multi-20
vendor scenario , centralized monitoring and control is required to offer optimal coverage, capacity and mobility 21
performance as well as control over electromagnetic emissions in order to comply with regulatory requirements.   22
The problem associated with traditional mMIMO BF is that its performance is highly dependent on the choice of the 23
Beam Forming (BF) pattern. Manual configuration is usually based on the empirical knowledge and manual test results 24
of the domain expert(s), and is performed in a semi -static way. That is, (near-)real time contextual, per -site information 25

3.7.2 Massive MIMO Non-GoB Beamforming (Non-GoB BF) Optimization 9
This use case provides the background and motivation for the O -RAN architecture to support Non -Grid of Beams 10
beamforming optimization. Non-RT RIC could be used to train AI/ML models for Non-GoB BF selection xApps, which 11
intelligently recommend best Non-GoB BF modes to a O-gNB or O-DU.  12
Note that non -AI/ML based solutions for Non -GoB BF optimization is not precluded. The AI/ML model training, 13
deployment, and inference described below are not applicable to non-AI/ML based solutions. 14
3.7.2.1 Background and goal of the use case 15
Non-GoB BF approaches are an important class of beamforming algorithms  for 5G  massive MIMO deployments , 16
especially for implementations in sub-6 GHz frequency bands . For example, beam weights can be computed at the O -17
gNB or O-DU based on channel measurements of sounding reference signals (SRS) without predefined beam sets, 18
assuming uplink and downlink correspondence. 19
Noted that Non-GoB BF modes are not standardized, instead the y are vendor-specific proprietary algorithms. Multiple 20
Non-GoB BF modes can be implemented, as some modes perform better than others under particular wireless conditions. 21
Non-GoB BF modes can differ in the following aspects: 22
· MIMO modes (i.e., SU-MIMO or MU-MIMO) 23
· Channel estimation algorithms 24
· Beam weight calculation approaches (e.g., matched filter (MF), zero-forcing (ZF), eigen-beamforming, etc.) 25
· Time and frequency granularity of beamforming, etc. 26
To select the best Non -GoB BF modes, the SMO/Non -RT RIC and the Near -RT RIC are not required to understand  the 27
details of a specific Non -GoB BF modes, e.g., how the beamforming weights are computed under a mode. They only 28
need to know how many Non-GoB BF modes are supported in the O-DU and the performance of each mode.  29
The goal of this use case is therefore to provide an intelligent control over multiple supported Non -GoB BF modes in 30
order to recommend a preferred mode to a BS as a function of wireless conditions, such as channel quality, UE location 31
and mobility, interference condition, PHY layer configuration, etc. 32

3.7.3 MIMO Optimization via MIMO DL Tx Power Optimization, MU-MIMO 4
Pairing, and MIMO Mode Selection 5
This use case will provide the objective, solutions, and data requirements related to MIMO optimization based on three 6
key sub-features involving downlink transmit power, MIMO pairing enhancement (user separability), and user MIMO 7
mode selection (MU-MIMO or SU-MIMO) that are described in detail in the O-RAN.WG1.MMIMO-USE-CASES-TR 8
[13]. The use-case leverages Non-RT RIC to train and host the relevant models and applications that rely on O1 interface 9
services to intelligently optimize MIMO capacity and user experience. 10
3.7.3.1 Background and goal of the use case 11
3.7.3.1.1 MIMO downlink transmit power optimization 12
For general downlink precoding, the downlink transmit power is usually evenly distributed across the UEs.  However, 13
depending on the UE separability and path loss deltas, this may result in good  cell capacity at the expense of individual 14
UE quality.  This can be due to several issues such as cell edge UEs having general downlink SINR issues (even without 15
MU-MIMO), poor UE separability between cell edge UEs, and poor uplink SINR resulting in degraded SRS which are a 16
few example issues.  The result of these issues can be manifested by observations of very poor individual UE SINRs 17
(either downlink, uplink, or both) when running in a M U-MIMO mode. Therefore, although the capacity of the cell has 18
been significantly increased, certain customer experiences may become unacceptable in this M U-MIMO mode.   19
The solution to the problem described above is to simply provide observations of UE performance in the form of periodic 20
histograms of UE channel quality as well as the overall cell capacity in order to compute an optimal solution via AI/ML 21
with control of the downlink minimum required SINR threshold to achieve a minimal UE quality requirement that is set 22
by the operator. The minimum required SINR is a threshold recommendation and thus doesn’t require real time AI/ML 23
adjustment of transmit power directly but rather leaves this to the scheduler to adjust and optimize consistent with its 24
numerous other priorities and requirements. 25
The value of this observability and adjustability allows the operator to optimize the trade-off between cell capacity and 26
individual user/customer quality which is essential to provide the best customer experience. The trade -off, for example, 27
can reduce a very high cell centre data rate (which would likely be unnoticeable for the user) to allow more power to be 28
allocated to the cell edge user (who is noticing low tput and large latencies) to improve the cell edge data rate situation.  29
 30
3.7.3.1.2 MU-MIMO Pairing Enhancement (user separability) 31
Existing channel orthogonality between multiple users is critical to create user separability and allow for the opportunity 32
to share radio frequency resources simultaneously. Failing this, residual interference will be too high to maintain adequate 33
post pairing radio link signal quality levels required to sustain M U-MIMO mode assignments. With mobility there is an 34
added demand to adjust beamforming weight assignments to not only maintain signal power levels at the user end (beam 35
quality), but also to continuously limit the inter user interference experienced between users assigned with the same radio 36
resource allocations. If these challenges are left unaddressed, a 5G massive MIMO deployment will fail to utilize the full 37
capability of large antenna arrays powered by transceivers designed to transmit data channel signals towards a spatially 38
confined direction. Further, the network will also fail to realize potential multiplexing gains as fewer radio resource blocks 39
are shared between users within the same cell, reducing spectral efficiency.  40

Another important aspect is the need to efficiently identify users with low demand for radio resources - sources of bursty 1
traffic. An intelligent assessment of how best such users can be effectively paired, if at all, with other users , needs to be 2
pre-determined by the RIC. In summary, this use case suggests various measurement objects that are recom mended as 3
input into the AI/ML analytics Apps to optimally determine the outputs required to optimize the M U-MIMO feature 4
operation. 5
The AI/ML assisted modelling and training output, along with the Non-RT RIC based enhancement/inference, will strive 6
to deliver end goal solution selections and system configuration options that upon adoption within the respective domains 7
where they reside, realize an optimization framework that maximizes the potential of a M U-MIMO feature. Capacity 8
augmentation will be realized by successfully assigning MU-MIMO layers to a greater number of users simultaneously, 9
more often, and more uniformly across the serving area of each gNB. 10
3.7.3.1.3 MIMO Mode Selection Optimization (MU-MIMO vs SU-MIMO selection) 11
A successful MU-MIMO operation involves the realization of as many orthogonal radio frequency channel links between 12
multiple spatially separated users as possibly as supported by the implementation software at the digital domain. Key to 13
such realization is the successful beamforming weight determination that enables not only the phase addition of multipath 14
signals at the user receiver, but also the choice of precoding algorithms which limit the residual interference between the 15
paired users. It can make sense for the scheduler to prioritize the assignment of radio resources to a MU-MIMO mode of 16
operation during periods of congestion or when high latency requiring applications are supported (to free up other 17
resources that can be assigned sooner). However, doing so at the expense of undesirably lower spectral efficiency on these 18
assigned radio resources will reduce overall sector throughput levels and create poor user experience. It is important to 19
find a means through the AI/ML agent to distinguish users and identify sectors where op timal operation means a greater 20
assignment of Su-MIMO modes independently to users, especially those requiring higher throughput, using devices that 21
are capable of supporting higher layer SU-MIMO count, and operating in an environment that sustains a great er channel 22
rank. 23
With increased loading Massive MIMO systems will incur rising levels of interference on the uplink from connected 24
users and on the downlink from the gNB. In addition to normal SINR measurements, the diagnosis of interference from 25
all spati al directions uniformly (white spatial noise) versus specific directions (spatially correlated noise) will be of 26
interest and will require MIMO modes (SU-MIMO vs MU-MIMO) to be properly selected for assignment on a user basis.  27
Such implementation will optimize the per user and per cell throughputs, taking into consideration channel orthogonality 28
conditions rank realizable, and per user effective bandwidth requirement. 29
