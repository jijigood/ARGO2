3.1 Use case 1: Context-Based Dynamic HO Management for V2X 2 
This  use  case  provides  the  background,  motivation,  and  requirements  for  the  Context-based Dynamic HO 3 
Management for V2X   use  case,  allowing operators to adjust radio resource allocation policies through the O -RAN 4 
architecture, reducing latency and improving radio resource utilization. 5 
3.1.1 Background and goal of the use case 6 
V2X communication allows for numerous potential benefits such as increasing the overall road safety, reducing 7 
emissions, and saving time. Part of the V2X architecture is the V2X UE (SIM + device attached to vehicle) which 8 
communicates with the V2X Applicat ion Server (V2X AS). The exchanged information comprises Cooperative 9 
Awareness Messages (CAMs), (from UE to V2X AS) [12], radio cell IDs, connection IDs, and basic radio measurements 10 
(RSRP, RSPQ, etc.) 11 
As vehicles traverse along a highway, due to their high speed and the heterogeneous natural environment V2X UE-s are 12 
handed over frequently, at times in a suboptimal way, which may cause handover (HO) anomalies: e.g., short stay, ping-13 
pong, and remote cell.  Such suboptimal HO sequences substantially impair the functionality of V2X applications. Since 14 
HO sequences are mainly determined by the Neighbour Relation Tables (NRTs), maintained by the xNBs, there is hardly 15 
room for UE-level customization. 16 
This UC aims to present a method to avoid and/or resolve problematic HO scenarios by using past navigation and radio 17 
statistics in order to customize HO sequences on a UE level. To this end, the AI/ML functionality that is enabl ed by the 18 
Near-RT RIC is employed. 19 
3.1.2 Entities/resources involved in the use case 20 
1) Non-RT RIC:  21 
a) Retrieve necessary performance, configuration, and other data for constructing/training relevant AI/ML models 22 
that will be deployed in Near-RT RIC to assist in the V2X HO management function. For example, this could 23 
be a clustering algorithm that classifies traffic situations and radio conditions that (probably) do or do not lead 24 
to HO anomalies. 25 
b) Support deployment and update of AI/ML models into Near-RT RIC xApp. 26 
c) Support communication of intents and policies (system-level and UE-level) from non-RT RIC to Near-RT RIC. 27 
d) Support communication of non-RAN data to enrich control functions in Near-RT RIC (enrichment data). 28 
2) Near-RT RIC: 29 
a) Support update of AI/ML models retrieved from Non-RT RIC. 30 
b) Support interpretation and execution of intents and policies from Non-RT RIC. 31 
c) Support necessary performance, configuration, and other data for defining and updating intents and policies for 32 
tuning relevant AI/ML models. 33 
d) Support communication of configuration parameters to RAN.  34 
3) RAN: 35 
a) Support data collection with required granularity to SMO over O1 interface. 36 
b) Support near-real-time configuration-based optimization of HO parameters over E2 interface. 37

3.1.3.1 Context-based Dynamic Handover Management for V2X 7 
Use Case Stage Evolution / Specification <<Uses>> 
Related use  
Goal Drive V2X UE HOs in  RAN according to defined intents, policies, and 
configuration while enabling AI/ML-based solutions.  
Actors and Roles 
Non-RT RIC: RAN policy control function. 
Near-RT RIC: RAN policy enforcement function. 
RAN: policy enforcement for configuration updates. 
SMO: termination point for O1 interface. 
V2X AS: termination point for V1 interface and enrichment data provider. 
 
Assumptions All relevant functions and components are instantiated.  
A1, O1, E2 interface connectivity is established.  
Pre conditions 
Network is operational. 
SMO has established the data collection and sharing process, and Non-RT RIC 
has access to this data. 
Non-RT RIC analyzes the historical data from RAN and V2X AS for training the 
relevant AI/ML models to be deployed or updated in the  Near-RT RIC, as well 
as AI/ML models required for real -time optimization of configuration and 
policies. 
 
Begins when  Operator specified trigger condition or event is detected.  
Step 1 (M) Non-RT RIC deploys/updates the AI/ML model in the Near-RT RIC via O1 or 
Non-RT RIC assigns/update the AI/ML model for the Near-RT RIC xApp via A1.  
Step 2 (M) 
Non-RT RIC communicates relevant policies/intents and enrichment data to the 
Near-RT RIC over the A1 interface. The enrichment data from the non -RAN 
data may include V2X UE location, trajectory, navigation information, GPS data, 
CAMs, DENMs. 
 
Step 3 (M) 
The Near-RT RIC receives the relevant info from the non -RT RIC over the A1 
interface and from the RAN over the E2 interface, interprets the policies a nd 
updates the AI/ML models. 
 
Step 4 (M) 
The Near-RT RIC infers optimal RAN configuration (UE -specific NRTs) 
according to the trained AI/ML models and communicates the result to the RAN 
over E2 interface. 
 
Step 5 (M) RAN deploys the configuration received from the Near-RT RIC over the E2 
interface.  
Step 4 
If required, Non-RT RIC can configure specific performance measurement data 
to be collected from RAN to assess the performance of the V2X HO 
management function in Near-RT RIC, or to assess the outcome of the applied 
policies and configuration. 
 
Ends when Operator specified trigger condition or event is satisfied.  
Exceptions None identified.  
Post Conditions 
Non-RT RIC monitors the performance of the V2X HO related function in Near-
RT RIC by collecting and monitoring the relevant performance KPIs and 
counters from the RAN and the V2X AS.

3.2.1 Background and goal of the use case 1 
The field trials’ results show that the coverage for low altitude is good and can provide various services for terrestrial UEs 2 
with good performance. However, since the site along the flight is mainly for terrestrial UEs, the altitude of the UAV is 3 
always not within the main lobe of the ground station antenna. And the side lobes give rise to the phenomenon of scattered 4 
cell associations particularly noticeable in the sky. The cell association pattern on the ground is ideally contiguous area 5 
where the best cell is most often the one closest to the UE. As the UE move up in height, the antenna side lobes start to 6 
be visible, and the best cell may no longer be the closest one. The cell association pattern in this particular scenario 7 
becomes fragmented especially at the height of 300m and above. Hence, at higher altitudes, several challenges that lead 8 
to a different radio environment are: 9 
a) LOS propagation/uplink interference  10 
b) Poor KPI caused by antenna side lobes for base stations 11

3.5 Use case 5: Traffic Steering 6 
This use case prov ides the motivation, description, and requirements for traffic steering use  case, allowing operators to 7 
specify different objectives for traffic management such as optimizing the network/UE performance, or achieving 8 
balanced cell load. 9 
3.5.1 Background and goal of the use case 10 
5G systems will support many different combinations of access technologies namely; LTE (licensed band), NR (licensed 11 
band), NR-U (unlicensed band), Wi -Fi (unlicensed band)  [11]. Several different multi -access deployment scenarios are 12 
possible with 5GC to support wide variety of applications and satisfy the spectrum requirements of different service 13 
providers; 14 
- Carrier aggregation between licensed band NR (Primary Cell) and NR-U (Secondary Cell) 15 
- Dual connectivity between licensed band NR (Primary Cell) and NR-U (Secondary Cell) 16 
- Dual connectivity between licensed band LTE (Primary Cell) and NR-U (Secondary Cell) 17 
- Dual connectivity between licensed band NR (Primary Cell) and Wi-Fi (Secondary Cell) 18 
Note: The scenario of dual connectivity between NR and Wi-Fi is for future study 19 
The rapid traffic growth and multiple frequency bands utilized in a commercial network make it challenging to steer the 20 
traffic in a balanced distribution. Further in a multi -access system there is need to switch the traffic across access 21 
technologies based on changes in radio environment and application requirements and even split the traffic across multiple 22 
access technologies to  satisfy performance requirements. The different types of  traffic and frequency bands in a 23 
commercial network make it challenging to handle the complex QoS aspects, bearer selection (Master Cell Group (MCG) 24 
bearer, Secondary Cell Group (SCG) bearer, Split bearer), bearer type change for load balancing, achieving low latency 25 
and best in class throughput in a multi -access scenario with 5GC networks  [8]. Typical controls are limited to adjusting 26 
the cell reselection and handover parameters; modifying load calculations and cell priorities ; and are largely static in 27 
nature when selecting the type of bearers and QoS attributes.  28 
Further, the RRM (Radio Resource Management) fe atures in the existing cell ular network are all cell -centric. Even in 29 
different areas of within a  cell, there are variations in radio environment, such as neighboring cell coverage, signal 30 
strength, interference status, etc. However, base station s based on traditional control strategies treat all UEs in a similar 31 
way and are usually focused on average cell-centric performance, rather than UE-centric.  32 
Such current solutions suffer from following limitations: 33 
 It is hard to adapt the RRM control to diversified scenarios including multi-access deployments and optimization 34 
objectives. 35 
 The traffic management strategy is usually passive, rarely taking advantage of capabilities to predict network and 36 
UE performance. The strategy needs to consider aspects of steering, switching and splitting traffic across different 37 
access technologies in a multi-access scenario. 38 
 Non-optimal traffic management, with slow response time, due to various factors such as inability to select the 39 
right set of UEs for cont rol action. This further results in non -optimal system and UE performance, such as 40 
suboptimal spectrum utilization, reduced throughput and increased handover failures. 41

3.6.1 Background and goal of the use case 1 
Massive MIMO (mMIMO) is among the key levers to increase performance and QoS in 5G networks . Capacity 2 
enhancement is obtained by means of beamforming of the transmitted signals, and by spatially multiplexing data streams 3 
for both single user (SU) and for multi user (MU) MIMO. Beamforming increases the received signal power, while 4 
decreasing the interference generated on other users, hence resulting in higher SINR and user throughputs. Beamforming 5 
can be codebook based (mainly for FDD), or non-codebook based (TDD). Grid of Beams (GoB)  with the corresponding 6 
beam sweeping [9][10] has been introduced to allow beamforming the control channels used during initial access, mainly 7 
for high frequency (but can be used also for the sub -6 GHz band) MIMO operation. The codebook and the GoB  define 8 
the span of the beams, namely the horizontal and vertical aperture in which beamforming is supported, and therefore the 9 
coverage area and the shape of the cell.  Massive MIMO can be deployed in 5G macro -cells as well as in heterogeneous 10 
network, where macro-cells and 3D-MIMO small cells co-exist and complement each other for better aggregated capacity 11 
and coverage. In order to obtain an optimal beamforming and cell resources (Tx power, PRB) configuration, one will 12 
have to look at a multi -cell enviro nment instead of a single cell.  Moreover, d ifferent vendors may have different 13 
implementations in terms of the number of beams, the horizontal/vertical beam widths, azimuth and elevation range, to 14 
achieve the desired coverage. In a multi node/multi-vendor scenario, centralized monitoring and control  is required to 15 
offer optimal coverage, capacity and mobility performance as well as control over EM emissions in order to comply with 16 
regulatory requirements. Additionally, the number of such co mbinations of adjustable parameters is in the thousands, 17 
hence it is prohibitive for the traditional human expert system to work out the optimal configuration, and a new method 18 
is in need. 19 
State of the art solutions suffer from the following problems: 20 
mMIMO macro - and small-cells benefit from a flexible way of serving u sers in their coverage area thanks to 21 
beamforming. However the coverage area itself is defined by (vendor specific) fixed mMIMO system parameters such as 22 
the azimuth and elevation angle range, or the GoB parameters. Hence due to user and traffic distribution and terrain 23 
topology, the mMIMO cell may suffer from e.g. 24 
1) High inter-cell interference 25 
2) Unbalanced traffic between neighboring cells 26 
3) Low performance of cell edge users 27 
4) Poor handover performance  28 
Moreover, load balancing functions may be activated in the network nodes, e.g. gNB adapting mobility parameters in 29 
order to distribute load between the beams of the neighbor cell s, relying on load information exchange over network 30 
interfaces. This approach however is partly limited by the cell footprint statically fixed at the initial configuration.  31 
The objective of this use case is to allow the operator to flexibly configure a mMIMO system parameters by means of 32 
policies and configuration assisted by machine learning techniques, according to objectives defined by the operator.   33

3.7 Use case 7: RAN Sharing 15 
This use case provides the motivation, description, and requirements for RAN sharing use case. The goal of this use case 16 
is to enable multiple operators to share the same O -RAN infrastructure, while allowing them to remotely configure and 17 
control the shared resources via a remote O1, O2 and E2 interface.    18 
3.7.1 Background and goal of the use case 19 
RAN sharing is envisioned as an efficient and sustainable way to reduce the network deployment costs, while increasing 20 
network capacity and coverage. Among the different RAN sharing models that have been experimented so far, a special 21 
focus is put here on the evaluation of the compatibility of the “Geographical Split” RAN sharing model with the O-RAN 22 
architecture. In such a model, a coverage area is split between two or more operators; each operator manages the RAN in 23 
a specific area, while sharing its RAN infrastructure and computing resources with its partner operators.  24 
Specifically, this use case analyzes the Multi Operator RAN (MORAN) sharing scenario, wherein each operator utilizes 25 
a separate carrier in order to achieve more freedom and independency on the control of the radio resources.   Accordingly, 26 
the goal of this use case is to propose a sharing-compliant O-RAN architecture that lets operators to configure the shared 27 
network resources independently from configuration and operating strategies of the other sharing operators. Specifically, 28 
it is proposed that a Home Operator (Operator A) makes available its O-RAN infrastructure and computing resources  to 29 
host the virtual RAN functions (VNF) of a second operator (Operator B), allowing it to configure and control such remote 30 
VNFs via a remote O1, O2 and E2 interface. 31

3.8 Use case 8: QoS Based Resource Optimization 1 
This use case provides the background and motivation for the O -RAN architecture to support RAN QoS based resource 2 
optimization. Moreover, some high -level description and requirements over Non-RT RIC and A1 interfaces are 3

3.8.1 Background and goal of the use case 5 
QoS based resource optimization can used when the network has been configured to provide some kind of preferential 6 
QoS for certain users. One such scenario can be related to when the network has been configured to support e2e slices. In 7 
this case, the network has functionality that ensures resource isolation between slices as well as functionality to monitor 8 
that slice Service Level Specifications (SLS) are fulfilled.  9 
In RAN, it is the scheduler that ensures  that Physical Resource Block (PRB) resources are isolated between slices in the 10 
best possible way and also that the PRB resources are used in an optimal way to best fulfill the SLS for different slices. 11 
The desired default RAN behavior for slices is confi gured over O1. For example, the ratio of physical resources (PRBs) 12 
reserved for a slice is configured at slice creation (instantiation) over O1. Also, QoS can be configured to guide the RAN 13 
scheduler how to (in real-time) allocate PRB resources to differen t users to best fulfill the SLS of a specific slice. In the 14 
NR NRM this is described by the resource partition attribute. 15 
Instantiation of a RAN sub -slice will be prepared by rigorous planning to understand to what extent deployed RAN 16 
resources will be able to support RAN sub-slice SLS. Part of this procedure is to configure RAN functionality according 17 
to above. With this, a default behavior of RAN is obtained that will be able to fulfill slice SLSs for most situations. 18 
However, even through rigorous planni ng, there will be times and places where the RAN resources are not enough to 19 
fulfill SLS given the default configuration. To understand how often (and where) this happens, the performance of a RAN 20 
slice will continuously be monitored by SMO. When SMO detec ts a situation when RAN SLS cannot be fulfilled, Non-21 
RT RIC can use A1 policies to improve the situation. To understand how to utilize A1 policies and how to resolve the 22 
situation, the non RT-RIC will use additional information available in SMO.  23 
Take an emergency service as an example of a slice tenant. For this example, it is understood (at slice instantiation) that 24 
50% of the PRBs in an area should be enough to support the emergency traffic under normal circumstances. Therefore, 25 
the ratio of PRBs for the  emergency users is configured to 50% as default behavior for the pre -defined group of users 26 
belonging to the emergency slice. Also, QoS is also configured in CN and RAN so that video cameras of emergency users 27 
get a minimum bitrate of 500 kbps. 28 
Now, suppose a large fire is ongoing and emergency users are on duty. Some of the personnel capture the fire on video 29 
on site. The video streams are available to the Emergency Control Command. Because of the high traffic demand in the 30 
area from several emergency  users (belonging to the same slice), the resources available for the Emergency slice is not 31 
enough to support all the traffic. In this situation, the operator has several possibilities to mitigate the situation. Depending 32 
on SLAs towards the Emergency slice c ompared to SLAs for other slices, the operator could reconfigure the amount of 33 
PRB reserved to Emergency slice at the expense of other slices. However, there is always a risk that Emergency video 34 
quality is not good enough irrespective if all resources are  used for Emergency users. It might be that no video shows 35 
sufficient resolution due to resource limitations around the emergency site.  36 
In this situation, the Emergency Control Command decides, based on the video content, to focus on a selected video 37 
stream to improve the resolution. The Emergency Control System gives the information about which users to up - and 38 
down-prioritized to the e2e slice assurance function (through e.g. an Edge API) of the mobile network to increase 39 
bandwidth for selected video  stream(s). Given this  additional information, the Non-RT RIC can influence how RAN 40 
resources are allocated to different users through a QoS target statement in an A1 policy. By good usage of the A1 policy, 41 
the Emergency Control Command can ensure that dyn amically defined group of UEs  provides the video resolution that 42 
is needed. 43

3.9 Use case 9: RAN Slice SLA Assurance 14 
The 3GPP standards architected a sliceable 5G infrastructure which allows creation and management of customized 15 
networks to meet specific service requirements that may be demanded by future applications, services and business 16 
verticals. Such a flexible architecture needs different requirements to be specified in terms of functionality, performance 17 
and group of users which may greatly vary from one service to the other. The 5G standardization efforts have gone into 18 
defining specific slices and their Service Level Agreements (SLAs) based on application/service type [3]. Since network 19 
slicing is conceived to be an end-to-end feature that includes the core network, the transport network and the radio access 20 
network (RAN), these requirements should be met at any slice subnet during the life-time of a network slice [4], especially 21 
in RAN side. Exemplary slice performance requirements are defined in terms of throughput, energy efficiency, latency 22 
and reliability at a high level in SDOs such as 3GPP [2] and GSMA [15]. These requirements are defined as a reference 23 
for SLA/contractual agreements for each slice, which individually need proper handl ing in NG-RAN.  24

Although network slicing support is started to be defined with 3GPP Release 15, slice assurance mechanisms in RAN 1

use cases. This use case is intended to clarify necessary mechanisms and parameters for RAN slice SL A assurance.   5 
3.9.1 Background and goal of the use case 6 
In the 5G era, network slicing is a prominent feature which provides end-to-end connectivity and data processing tailored 7 
to specific business requirements. These requirements include customizable ne twork capabilities such as th e support of 8 
very high data rates, traffic densities, service availability and very low latency. According to 5G standardization efforts, 9 
the 5G system should support the needs of the business through the specification of several service needs such as data 10 
rate, traffic capacity, user density, latency, reliability, and availability. These capabilities are always provided based on a 11 
Service Level Agreement (SLA)  between the mobile operator and the business customer , which brought up interest for 12 
mechanisms to ensure slice SLAs and prevent its possible violations . O-RAN’s open interfaces and AI/ML based 13 
architecture will enable such challenging mechanisms to be implemented and help pave the way for operators to realize 14 
the opportunities of network slicing in an efficient manner. 15

3.10 Use case 10: Multi-vendor Slices 1 
This use case “Multi-vendor slices” is a case that vO -DU and vO-CU functions composing each slice is provided from 2 
different vendor. In this sub section, concept, motivation and benefits of introducing “Multi-vendor slices” are explained 3 
and candidate solutions are studied. 4 
3.10.1 Background and goal of the use case 5 
Proposed use case enables multiple slices with functions provided from multi- vendors, such as slice #1 is composed with 6 
DU and CU provided from vendor A and slice #2 is composed with DU and CU provided from vendor B . 7 
 8 
Figure 3.10.1-1: Multi-vendor Slices 9 
 10 
When providing multiple slices, it is assumed that suitable vO-DU/scheduler and vO-CU treat each slice respectively. A 11 
vendor who providing vO-DU and vO-CU function may have a strength of a customized scheduler for a certain service. 12 
With accomplishment of multi-vendor circumstances, following benefits can be expected. 13 
1) More flexible and time to market deployment 14 
Operator can maximize options to choose suitable vO-DU/scheduler and vO-CU to offer various slice. For example, 15 
some vendor has a strength of a scheduler for eMBB service and the other has a strength of scheduler for URLLC 16 
service. Or, vendor A can provide vO-DU/scheduler and vO -CU suitable for URLLC earlier than vendor B, 17 
therefore operator can choose vO-DU and vO-CU functions from vendor A to meet their service requirement. 18 
 19 
Also, when operator will add a new service/slice, new functions from a new vendor can be introduced with less 20 
consideration for existing vendor if multi -vendor circumstance was rea lized. This may lead to expand vendor’s 21 
business opportunities rapidly. 22 
 23 
2) Flexible deployment when sharing RAN equipment among operators 24 
When operators want to share RAN equipment and resources, RAN vendors and their placement of each RAN 25 
functions may be different. If multi -vendor circumstance was introduced, then it can relax restrictions among 26 
operators to share RAN equipment and resources. This may lead to expand opportunity reaching agreement of 27 
RAN sharing among operators. With expansion of RAN sharing, CAPEX and OPEX by operator will be optimized 28 
and additional investment can be done more. 29 
 30 
3) Reducing supply chain risk 31 
If existing vendor providing a certain pair of vO-DU and vO-CU functions would withdraw of their market due to 32 
business reason, operator can deploy new vO-DU and vO-CU functions alternatively from other vendor under this 33 
multi-vendor circumstance. This can reduce a risk for operators’ business continuity. 34

3.11.1 Background and goal of the use case 1 
As we transition from 4G to 5G, the spectral resources used for 5G deployment is a key consideration and this situation 2 
varies from one operator to another. Though, new C -band resources between 3 -6 GHz and mmWave bands have been 3 
acquired by operators, these bands suffer from great propagation and penetration loss, limiting their coverage to those 4 
users close to the cell , this situation worsens in the UL as the UE device is power cons trained. A cost-effective way to 5 
address this is the 5G deployment on lower bands (i.e., below 2GHz), which are also used in 4G LTE deployments today. 6 
Operating on lower bands along with non -standalone mode of 5G deployment helps to cover large geography, enables 7 
seamless mobility between 4G and 5G while being sensitive to overall cost of deployment. In addition, DSS offers the 8 
advantage of dynamically sharing the available spectrum adapting to the varying workloads of the 4G and 5G network. 9 
DSS is compelling considering the need for operators to dynamically share already deployed spectral resources between 10 
LTE and NR devices without degrading the QoE of the current 4G subscribers while offering the same level of coverage 11 
and necessary QoS to NR devices, under the assumption that the two networks will co-exist in the near term. The objective 12 
of this use case is to propose DSS in the context of the ORAN architecture, specifically to realize it as an application in 13 
the RIC framework.  14 
This would particularly benefit vRAN implementations when the 4G/5G CU/DU are from different vendors and one could 15 
leverage RAN data over O -RAN’s framework for traffic prediction, DSS related resource management and conduct 16 
control functions. Towards this, the intelligent control functions are identified, which can be realized as a DSS application 17 
to augment the L3/L2/L1 control functions defined as part of LTE-NR coexistence in Rel-15/16 [3][8]. 18 
In Figure 3.11.1-1, the architectural context is set for this discussion. DSS enables 4G and 5G U Es to operate over the 19 
same spectrum identified as X (typically low band), while 5G itself could operate on new bands Y (typically high band) 20 
not used by current 4G deployment. In a typical setting, Y would offer higher capacity, low latency and smaller coverage, 21 
while X would be used to offer reasonable capacity along with larger coverage. 3GPP specifications offers DSS support 22 
over X2/Xn interface to enable dynamic sharing of the spectrum resource in addition to the L2/L1 adaptation for 5G-NR 23 
to co-exist with LTE. 24 
Considering the scenario of incremental deployment - in the  5G NSA mode, the 5G UE is required to have dual 25 
connectivity capability and be able to connect to eNBs on LTE bands for control plane requirements and user plane 26 
connectivity towards the LTE and/or 5G depending on deployment requirements. In the scenario where gNB only operates 27

eNB MAC scheduler, as the UE is expected to be dual  stacked. While, if the gNB is required to operate on lower LTE 29 
bands as well, then spectral sharing needs to be coordinated between the LTE and 5G schedulers.  30 
When DSS is enabled in the SA mode, 5G UE would be capable of operating on lower LTE bands (below 2GHz), C and 31 
mmWave bands and connects only to the  gNBs. The sharing of the LTE bands between LTE and 5G data channels are 32 
achieved by both 4G scheduler and 5G scheduler using resource management and interference mitigation functions in the 33 
RIC between them.  34

3.15 Use case 15: O-RAN Signalling Storm Protection 16 
This use case provides the background, motivation, and requirements for the O-RAN Signaling Storm Protection use case, 17 
allowing protecting the mobility network against signaling storms initiated by devices. 18 
3.15.1 Background and goal of the use case 19 
Society is increasingly dependent on network connectivity at any time and in any place and increasing diversity of device 20 
types ranging from complex devices such as smart phone to very simple and low -cost IoT devices are connecting to the 21 
network. The sheer number of connected devices, as well as the wide range of device types, makes the mobility network 22 
subject to accidental or intentional attacks that may disrupt the regular usage of the network. Given that life -critical 23 
applications are moving to wireles s networks, such network disruptions are not only an inconvenience but may have 24 
impact on life and health of individuals. The O-RAN architecture offers an opportunity to address such security challenges 25 
in customizable and creative ways by utilizing the near-RT RIC xApps and non-RT RIC rApps. 26 
Currently, the main defense mechanism standardized in 3GPP against attacks coming from the devices toward the network 27 
is based on configuration of the devices themselves and trust that the devices will indeed comply with restrictions defined 28 
by mobility standards. One such defense mechanism is the back -off timer that restricts the number of repeated device 29 
registrations, thus preventing devices from overloading the network with attaches. If this trust is breached there are no 30 
other options for defending the network rather than rejecting (denying service) randomly to both benign and malicious 31 
devices, a state which is equivalent to DDoS. Unfortunately, even today the network has few hundreds of device types 32 
that under certain conditions accidently breach this trust and allow devices to aggressively attach to the network in a rate 33 
of few thousand times per hour (the maximum allowed number by standard is less than 20 attaches per hour). An attacker 34 
that finds a way to manipulate vulnerabilities in a large set of these devices remotely can cause an attach storm that could 35 

 
________________________________________________________________________________________________ 
Copyright © 2021 by the O-RAN Alliance e.V. Your use is subject to the terms of the O-RAN Adopter License Agreement in Annex ZZZ  66 
O-RAN.WG1.Use-Cases-Detailed-Specification-v05.00 
lead to a long outage of large parts of the network. Furthermore, this attacker can continue this attack over many hours, 1 
each time picking few thousand of d evices from a large pool of millions of vulnerable devices connected to the same 2 
carrier network; the network carrier will not be able to stop this attack without intelligent and fine-grained controls to act 3 
against a certain patterns of behavior.  4 
Fortunately detecting these aggressive devices is possible as their behavior is very different from the other devices in the 5 
network. What the network really needs is to apply dynamic restriction over these devices to prevent them from 6 
overloading the control pla ne of the network. This restriction should be smart enough to still allow benign devices to 7 
register to the network without interruption. Having smart security control at the RAN can stop such attack and without 8 
overloading deeper parts of the network in the core. 9 
The goal of this use case is utilize O-RAN to detect and mitigate signaling storms DDoS quick and as close to the network 10 
edge, thus minimizing affected network nodes. The near -RT RIC would detect these signaling storms by analyzing 11 
signaling events from RAN nodes it controls. When such a storm is detected the near-RT RIC creates fine grained filters, 12 
which cover the aggressive UEs that cause the storm. These UEs registration requests will then be blocked/throttled while 13 
the behaving UEs will continue to get service as usual. In some cases the attack may be spread across many locations. It 14 
could be that the volume of signaling per location has not crossed a critical threshold but the moderate increase in many 15 
locations do cause an overload of centra l nodes such as the network core elements. In this case a network -wide view is 16 
required; thus the non -RT performs the network -wide analysis and in the case of a network signaling storm, it pushes 17 
policies to the local near -RT RIC to adjust detection parame ters to reduce the moderate increase of signaling from a set 18 
of one or more E2 Nodes. This combined view of both non -RT RIC and near -RT RIC ensures quick reaction to local 19 
signaling storms as well as response to widely distributed attacks.   20 
While flows in  this use case focus on the signaling storm scenario, they could be easily extend to include other attack 21 
scenarios both in terms of detection and mitigation. For example, the scenario where rogue devices report false CQI 22 
measurements that indicate high va lues while the real channel quality is poor. When exploited by attackers and applied 23 
to large set of devices this attack can cause to waste of radio resources and eventually to DoS. Detection of the attack may 24 
be achieved by analyzing anomalous CQI reports or abnormal volume of NACK messages based on signaling messages. 25 
For mitigation actions either rejecting the rogue devices or limiting radio resources can be applied . 26
