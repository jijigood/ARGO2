1.3 Definitions and Abbreviations 12 
1.3.1 Definitions 13 
For the purposes of the present document, the terms and definitions given in the following apply 14 
Badging: The process by which interoperability for a pair of products or E2E system integration for a group of products 15 
are assessed, and the involved products are authorized to carry a specific set of credentials that indicate successful results. 16 
Badging information, which is common to all products involved, will include information on all products (including 17 
hardware version and software version) which were involved in interoperability testing or E2E system testing.  18 
Blueprint: is the set of inputs which is used to describe a specific O-RAN deployment from several aspects. These aspects 19 
include the specification of the O -RAN deployment at the system level (e.g. architecture, performance metrics) as well 20 
as subsystem level and the int erfaces between the specified subsystems. This would then allow definition and 21 
documentation of the testing methodology and the test cases using the blueprint specification. 22 
Certification: The process by which compliance with O -RAN interface or reference design specifications for a product 23 
is verified, and the product is authorized to carry a specific set of credentials that indicate that it is conformant. 24 
Compliance folder: The set of evidence demonstrating an O-RAN ALLIANCE device’s compliance to the Badging and 25 
Certification Criteria, which includes the ICS, test reports, technical documentation, change management documentation 26 
and certification information. 27 
Compliant portion : Only those specific portions of an O -RAN ALLIANCE device (hardware, software or  the 28 
combination of the two) that are certified within the bounds of the compliant scope. 29 
Host: is a founder and main sponsor of Open Testing and Integration Centre (OTIC). Only O -RAN member (including 30 
the affiliates) or non-vendor O-RAN contributor may become a host. Host is mandatory entity in OTIC. 31 
Implementation Conformance Statement (ICS) : Statement of conformance provided by vendors who bring their 32 
DUTs/SUT for badging and/or certification service, which is the key element in determining which test cases will be 33 
executed on a particular product. 34 
IOT profile: specifies a specific selection of parameters, optional features, default values and ranges of configu rable 35 
attributes and mechanisms from O-RAN subsystem interface specification(s) that must be supported and used in order to 36 
guarantee interoperability between implementations from different vendors. 37 
MVP: describes the minimum viable plan to be tested for Certification 38

Chapter 2. Certification and Badging Overview 1 
2.1 Objectives 2 
To promote the O-RAN ecosystem, certifying and badging network components can be a very important way to encourage 3 
implementation and testing of O-RAN solutions and their deployment in operator networks. 4 
Certification and badging are helpful for operators as it provides some level of confidence that vendor O-RAN solutions 5 
work. With certification and badging in place, operator awareness of a vendor O -RAN solution is expected to increase, 6 
and the operator lab and/or field testing is expected to reduce. 7 
Certification and badging may also be helpful for vendors as it provides some level of evidence that their O-RAN solutions 8 
work, which could be used to promote their O-RAN solutions to operators. Further, for vendors looking for other vendors 9 
to interoperate with, certification and badging may also be beneficial to increase acceptance from other vendors. 10 
 11 
2.2 Scope 12 
The certification and badging described in this document defines the processes and associated technical procedures in the 13 
following areas: 14 
- Compliance verification of DUTs with O-RAN interfaces or reference design specifications based on conformance 15 
test specifications (conformance certification); 16 
- Interoperability assessment of DUT pairs with O -RAN interfaces based on IOT specifications ( interoperability 17 
badging); 18 
- E2E system integration assessment of SUT with O-RAN interfaces based on E2E system testing specifications 19 
(E2E system integration badging). 20 
The reason to use the term “ certification” for conformance testing and “badging” for interoperability and E2E system 21 
testing is as follows: 22 
- Conformance tests are controllable as it only involves a single DUT and verifies whether that DUT behaves in 23 
accordance to a technical specification (e.g. open fronthaul interface specification); 24 
- IOT and E2E system tests have some variability as it involves multiple DUTs /SUT (from different vendors) and 25 
the results may depend on the DUT/SUT combinations, deployment blueprints and test profiles. 26

Chapter 3. Certification and Badging Process  1 
3.1 General 2 
3.1.1 Objectives of defining certification/badging process 3 
The objective is to have in place some level of uniformity on the certification/badging processes in order to: 4 
- Ensure quality of the certification/badging service provided by different certification/badging entities; 5 
- Align procedures that users of the certification/badging service (both vendors bringing in DUTs /SUT for 6 
certification/badging, and operators requesting access to test results of certified/badged DUTs/SUT) experience at 7 
different certification/badging entities. 8 
3.1.2 Principles 9 
The certification/badging process sub-chapter adheres to the following principles: 10 
- A distributed approach is adopted in which (a) roles pertaining to ce rtification/badging are allocated to 11 
certification/badging entities and (b) related details are left to certification/badging entities’ discretion and 12 
responsibility. Rather than having rigorous centralized O-RAN control and management, this approach helps avoid 13 
imposing heavy requirements and allows freedom of operation to the certification/badging entities. However, 14 
centralized approach will also be adopted where required, e.g. to ensure quality of certification/badging, sharing 15 
of test results, etc, 16 
- Defining certification/badging process elements to address near -term needs of the community, while also 17 
establishing a framework that can evolve and scale with the ecosystem growth , i.e. this sub -chapter can evolve 18 
over time. 19 
- O-RAN will, as necessary, issue new versions of its functional specifications. As a result, O-RAN may determine 20 
that it will launch an updated version of its certification/badging program. 21 
- O-RAN will, as necessary, determine that an existing version of its functional specification is no longer viable and 22 
should be taken out of service for product development and certification/badging. As a result, O -RAN may 23 
determine when the specification is to be taken out of service. 24

O-RAN has defined and published many documents that describe various specifications. Within those specifications there 1

account market needs. In case certification/badging entities do not find certification defined for their intended scenarios, 3

3.2.2 Test platforms 5 
In order to reliably and successfully execute the tests for conformance certification , IOT badging, and E2E b adging, 6 
specialized test equipment is needed. Such test equipment needs to meet specific criteria and be designed in such a way 7 
as to execute the test cases identified. 8 
It is the responsibility of certification /badging entities together with test equipment  vendors to prepare appropriate test 9 
equipment for certification. O-RAN will neither provide qualify process nor acknowledge processes for test equipment , 10 
i.e. the choice of test equipment for certification will be left up to the testing market and their a ppropriateness will rely 11 
on self-declaration by certification entities and test equipment vendors. 12 
Certification/badging test setup including information of test equipment used by the test case needs to be document within 13 
the catalogue of test results crea ted and stored by the certification/badging entity. This will allow for vendors of the 14 
DUTs/SUT to challenge possible failure of tests which may be due to the test equipment. A standardized template to 15 
capture the test setup is provided in the certification and badging report template.   16

certification/badging service. 21 
Step 1: Identify which type of certification/badging: Conformance certification (Chapter 4), Interoperability badging 22 
(Chapter 5) and/or E2E system integration badging (Chapter 6). 23 
Step 2: Based on logical architecture in Figure 3.2-1 identify relevant sub-system. After selecting the subsystem, then 24 
identify relevant interfaces for the device to be tested. [8] 25

3.2.4 Executing Tests 4 
The certification/badging entity should use commercially available or open source test solutions. The certification/ 5 
badging entity should also ensure test equipment calibrations where specified by the test equipment vendor. (Pre-) 6 
Commercial UEs can also be used as long as the information on the UE is documented as part of the test setup and test 7 
results are reproducible. 8 
3.2.5 Verifying Test Results 9 
Certification/badging entities must capture the test result for each test undertaken. A value/measurement, screenshot, or 10 
packet capture must be recorded and entered into the certification/badging entity’s database for test result catalogue and/or 11 
the DUTs/SUT vendor’s compliance folder. Each test result must be compared to the range in the test specification to 12 
determine pass or fail. The test results should be reproducible, i.e. consistent results should be observed under the same 13
