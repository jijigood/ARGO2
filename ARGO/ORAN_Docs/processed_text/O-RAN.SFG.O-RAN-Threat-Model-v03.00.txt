The present document specifies the O-RAN Security Threat Modeling and Remediation Analysis. It identifies assets to 15 
be protected, analyzes the O-RAN components for vulnerabilities, examines potential threats associated with those 16 
vulnerabilities and provides security principles which stakeholders should address when building a secure end-to-end 17 
O-RAN system. 18 
The purpose of this version of the document is to present a list of system-level security principles as a basic guideline to 19 
be considered in the design, development, and operation of the O-RAN system. The principles are short and concise and 20 
can be used by organizations to improve the security of their O-RAN components. 21

1.3.1 Definitions 33 
For the purposes of the present document, the terms and definitions given in 3GPP TR 21.905 and the following apply: 34 
A1: Interface between non-RT RIC and Near-RT RIC to enable policy-driven guidance of Near-RT RIC 35 
applications/functions, and support AI/ML workflow.  36 
A1 policy: Type of declarative policies expressed using formal statements that enable the non-RT RIC function in the 37 
SMO to guide the near-RT RIC function, and hence the RAN, towards better fulfilment of the RAN intent. 38 
A1 Enrichment information: Information utilized by near-RT RIC that is collected or derived at SMO/non-RT RIC 39 
either from non-network data sources or from network functions themselves. 40

Chapter 2 Overview 1 
2.1 Objective and structure 2 
O-RAN architecture [13] is quite different from the architecture of 3GPP RAN, where new components, interfaces and 3 
technologies are proposed, new actors (stakeholders) arise and novel business models are made possible. The attack 4 
surface is bigger and we foresee that O-RAN design and deployment will raise numerous security challenges and 5 
resulting risks because of the new O-RAN specific interfaces and components, the virtualization/containerization 6 
techniques, the support of open source code, the capability to support an AI/ML model, etc. 7 
Therefore, the security analysis and the threat model for O-RAN must be carefully studied and relevant 8 
assets/stakeholders/vulnerabilities/threats/requirements/countermeasures/recommendations must be identified to reduce 9 
risk exposure and mitigate any harmful effects expected. 10 
The material presented in this analysis aims at supporting various O-RAN stakeholders understanding the relevant 11 
threats resulting to an exposure of O-RAN assets by exploiting the vulnerabilities. 12 
The objective is to give a comprehensive and high-level view on how security is organized in O-RAN system. In this 13 
document, we assume that 3GPP security requirements are met. Unless explicitly stated, features relate to O-RAN 14 
specifications. 15 
This analysis is consolidated from various relevant sources, including main 5G standardization documents and 16 
telecommunication best practices (e.g. 3GPP, ETSI, NIST, ENISA and GSMA). 17 
The first main part outlines the main stockholder roles involved in managing and using O-RAN system. Further, it 18 
addresses the prerequisites and assumptions needed to securely implement and run O-RAN systems. It also identifies 19 
the list of critical assets to be protected in integrity, availability, confidentiality, replay and authenticity. 20 
The second main part addresses the threat model. It identifies the threat agents, determines the threat surface, identifies 21 
vulnerabilities, and lists the threats for each O-RAN component or interface. For each threat, the description, threatened 22 
asset(s), vulnerabilities, threat agents and affected components are given. 23 
The third main part describes the security principles to be achieved to counter the identified threats. In addition, it 24 
illustrates the coverage between threats and security principles. 25 
The fourth main part is the risk assessment of the identified threats in terms of severity and likelihood. 26 
Note that this document focuses only on the components, interfaces and protocols specified by O-RAN alliance. 27 
Components, interfaces and protocols specified by 3GPP are only referenced where needed but are not within the scope 28 
of this specific O-RAN security analysis. 29 
The current document is a working document with the need to update the content on a regular basis following the risk 30 
evaluation. 31 
Note: In this document, the term NF (Network Function) is used to designate either VNF (Virtual Network Function), 32 
CNF (Containerized or cloud-native Network Function) or PNF (Physical Network Function). 33 
Note: Terms “Containers” and “Virtual Machines” are used interchangeably in this document as the implementation of 34 
O-RAN SW components could either be container-based, VM-based or hybrid (Containers and VMs together). 35 
2.2 Methodology 36 
The methodology adopted in this document is based on the standard ISO 27005 [14] which provide a detailed and 37 
flexible structure to release a risk assessment.  38 
Refer to NIST SP 800-154 [15] for the definition of Attack, Attack Surface, Attack Vector, Controls, Risk, Risk 39 
Mitigation, Threat, and Vulnerability. 40 
The methodology followed in this document comprises three stages: 41 
1) Identification (scope of this version of the document) 42

a. Identify stakeholders: First, we need to identify the stakeholders involved in the implementation, 1 
management, operation and maintenance of the O-RAN system. Roles and responsibilities of each 2 
stakeholder are given. 3 
b. Define assumptions: The list of minimum prerequisites and assumptions need to be defined for the 4 
operational environment (not under the control of the O-RAN system) required to successfully 5 
operate the O-RAN system. 6 
c. Identify assets: First, we need to locate relevant assets the O-RAN system hold and give details about 7 
the type (Data, component, etc.), the security properties (CIA) at rest and in transit and location. 8 
d. Identify threats: We need to identify the relevant threats associated with the new O-RAN 9 
components, interfaces and technologies. In addition, the threat surface and agents are given. 10 
e. Identify vulnerabilities: O-RAN system may have weaknesses in its new O-RAN components, 11 
interfaces and technologies which need to be identified. 12 
f. Define security principles: We need to define security principles to be achieved in order to reduce 13 
risk exposure. 14 
g. Elaborate and refine security principles: Each security principle needs to be detailed and refined 15 
into requirements, recommendations and countermeasures. 16 
h. Identify existing/ongoing countermeasures: We need to identify all of O-RAN existing/ongoing 17 
controls and to take into account the protection provided by these controls before applying any new 18 
ones. 19 
2) Risk assessment 20 
a. After the identification stage, we need to focus efforts on the biggest threats, so we should use the 21 
information you’ve gathered during the identification stage to prioritize the biggest risks. There are 22 
many ways to do this, but the most common approach involves the following equation: 23 
Risk = (the probability of a threat exploiting a vulnerability) x (total impact of the vulnerability being exploited) 24 
3) Risk treatment (will be provided in a future version) 25 
a. Now that we know the level of risk that each threat poses, we need to decide how we’ll treat them. 26 
There are four options: 27 
i. Modify the risk by implementing a control to reduce the likelihood of it occurring. 28 
ii. Avoid the risk by ceasing any activity that creates it. This response is appropriate if the risk 29 
is too big to manage with a security control. 30 
iii. Share the risk with a third party. There are two ways we can do this: by outsourcing the 31 
security efforts to another organization or by purchasing cyber insurance to ensure we have 32 
the funds to respond appropriately in the event of a disaster. Neither option is ideal, because 33 
we are ultimately responsible for our organization’s security, but they might be the best 34 
solutions if we lack the resources to tackle the risk. 35 
iv. Retain the risk. This means that our organization accepts the risk and believes that the cost 36 
of treating it is greater than the damage that it would cause. 37

Chapter 5 Threat model 1 
As the industry evolves towards RAN virtualization, it is important that a risk-based approach is taken to adequately 2 
address O-RAN security risks. Building upon the foundation set forth by 3GPP, O-RAN is standardized by the O-RAN 3 
Alliance with new functions and open, interoperable interfaces. With any nascent technology, including O-RAN, 4 
security should be built upon security-by-design using a risk-based approach, with consideration of potential zero-day 5 
attacks.  The O-RAN architecture [13] has security risks beyond those in a 3GPP architecture due to its expanded threat 6 
surface from new functions, additional interfaces, and the Lower Layer Split (LLS).  The disaggregation of hardware 7 
and software, virtualization, and use of open source components also present security risks that must be addressed.  8 
5.1 Threat surface 9 
The O-RAN architecture [13] introduces new functions and interfaces. The introduction of additional interfaces and 10 
nodes, and the decoupling of hardware and software, expands the threat and attack surface of the network. Threat 11 
surfaces may be divided into 6 main groups: 12 
• Additional functions: SMO, Non-Real-Time RIC, Near-Real-Time RIC 13 
• Additional open interfaces: A1, E2, O1, O2, Open Fronthaul 14 
• Modified architecture: Lower Layer Split (LLS) 7-2x 15 
• Decoupling increases threat to Trust Chain 16 
• Containerization and Virtualization: Disaggregation of software and hardware 17 
• Exposure to public exploits may be increased due to use of Open Source Code 18 
 19 
The following entry points are considered:  20 
• API between planes which facilitate the propagation of threats 21 
• Threats coming from inside the O-RAN system 22 
• Threats coming from outside the O-RAN system 23 
5.2 Threat agent 24 
Threat agents can be categorized as follows: 25 
• Cyber-criminals: Represents individuals who commits cybercrimes, where he/she makes use of the computer 26 
either as a tool or as a target or as both. 27 
• Insiders: Represents malicious attacks perpetrated on a network or computer system by a person with 28 
authorized system access.  29 
• Hacktivists: Represents actors that perform cyber-attacks to achieve political or social gains.  30 
• Cyber-terrorists: Represents actors that their sole aim of violence against clandestine agents and subnational 31 
groups through the compromise of O-RAN infrastructures.  32 
• Script kiddies: Represents actors that do not poses deep technical expertise or resources to perform 33 
sophisticated attacks. 34 
• Nation-State: actors aggressively target and gain persistent access to public and private sector networks to 35 
compromise, steal, change, or destroy information. They may be part of a state apparatus or receive direction, 36 
funding, or technical assistance from a nation-state. 37

user data messages (i.e. DNS) and redirect a user to a malicious website.

5.4.1.3 Threats against O-RU 3 
The O-RU introduces the following threats: 4 
Threat ID T-ORU-01 
Threat title An attacker stands up a false base station attack by attacking an O-RU  
Threat 
description 
A false base station attack occurs when an attacker masquerades as a legitimate mobile network to facilitate a 
Man-in-The-Middle (MiTM) attack between a subscriber’s user equipment (UE) and the mobile network.  
There are three attack scenarios on an O-RU that enable an attacker to realize a false base station attack:  
1. Hijack fronthaul to realize a false base station attack: Attacker disables an operational O-RU’s access 
to the open fronthaul, plugs a false base station system into the operational O-RU’s fronthaul interface, 
and launches a false base station attack with the O-RU providing the air interface. 
2. Recruit a standalone O-RU to realize a false base station attack: The stand-alone O-RU is an O-RU 
that is not operational but is available to an attacker to incorporate into a false base station system. The 
attacker plugs a false base station system into the standalone O-RU’s fronthaul interface and launches 
a false base station attack with the O-RU providing the air interface. 
3. Gain unauthorized physical access to O-RU to realize a false base station attack: An attacker gains 
access to external and internal components of an O-RU (other than the open fronthaul interface), 
connects the O-RU under attack to a false base station system, and launches a false base station attack 
with the O-RU providing the air interface. 
Successful attacks may cause: 
a) For a subscriber’s UE in attack scenarios 1, 2, and 3: the false base stations, also known as SUPI/5G-
GUTI catchers, retrieves a subscriber identity by forcing a UE to attach to the false base station 
systems. This opens the door to subscriber identity interception/disclosure and unauthorized 
subscriber tracking attacks. These attacks include stealing subscriber information, tampering with 
transmitted information, tracking subscribers, and compromising subscriber privacy. 
b) For the operator network: in attack scenario 1, the attacker removes the operational O-RU from 
providing service to UEs in the coverage area served by the operational O-RU. 
c) For operators and vendors in attack scenarios 1, 2, and 3, the attacker recruits legitimate 
operator/vendor equipment for the purpose of creating a false base station attack on subscribers, 
possibly harming the reputation of the operator and vendor whose O-RU was used in the attack.

Compromised credentials may also grant an adversary increased privilege to specific O-Cloud services or 
access to restricted areas of the O-Cloud network.  
Access may be also gained through an exposed service that doesn’t require authentication. In containerized

Threat ID T-GEN-03 
Threat title Untrust binding between the different O-Cloud layers 
Threat description 
One major challenge in virtualized architectures and especially in O-Cloud is to prove that a particular 
VM/Container runs on top of a specific Hypervisor/Container Engine. More specifically, it is necessary to 
assure that a trusted VM/Container is executed on a particular trusted Hypervisor/Container Engine, 
whereas the Hypervisor/Container Engine's trust state relies on an attestation that considers the entire 
corresponding hard and software stack. More precisely, this includes all hardware chips, firmware, OS and 
Hypervisor/Container Engine components that are relevant for the Hypervisor/Container Engine's trust 
state determination. 
If it is not possible to establish a correlation between VM/Container and Hypervisor/Container Engine, an 
attacker is able to make use of a trusted VM/Container that runs on top of an untrusted 
Hypervisor/Container Engine and it would be impossible to detect any interference made by the malicious 
Hypervisor/Container Engine, e.g. intercepting communication, replacing strong or using weak 
cryptographic keys, etc. Similarly, trustworthiness in the service-layer might only be established if there is 
a mechanism to determine that only trusted VNFs/CNFs, w.r.t trusted VM/Container's, are running on 
specific trusted Hypervisors/Container Engines that are part of the service-provisioning-chain.

Threat ID T-RADIO-01 
Threat title Disruption through radio Jamming2, Sniffing3 and Spoofing4 
Threat 
description 
Like for any wireless technology, disruption through radio jamming is possible by analyzing the physical 
downlink and uplink control channels and signals. 5G radio network is vulnerable to: 
• Jamming Vulnerability of Reference Signals 
• Jamming Vulnerability of Synchronization Signal 
• Jamming Vulnerability of the PBCH 
• Sniffing and Spoofing Vulnerability of the PBCH 
• Jamming Vulnerability of PDCCH 
• Jamming Vulnerability of Physical Uplink Control Channel

Note: The O-RAN OEMs need to develop new intelligence that can proactively alert the operator when this

can proactively alert the operator when this attack is initiated so that the operator can take appropriate actions to 
mitigate. 
 4 
Threat ID T-RADIO-02 
Threat title DoS attacks on cognitive radio networks [22] 
Threat 
description 
Cognitive radio (CR) technology, which is designed to enhance spectrum utilization, depends on the success of 
opportunistic access, where unlicensed secondary users (SUs) exploit spectrum void unoccupied by primary users 
(PUs) for transmissions. To realize DoS attacks, malicious users (MUs) target the critical functionalities for CR 
ecosystems, including spectrum sensing, agile radio, and light-handed regulation since once these functionalities 
fail, SUs are not able to communicate effectively. For example, MUs can directly jam the victim by injecting 
interference or deceive SUs into believing that there is a PU by emulating the signal characteristics of the PU, 
thereby evacuating the occupied spectrum. Moreover, the liability rule is vulnerable to the selfish and greedy 
users aiming to maximize their own private benefits. Since complying with the rule results in less transmission 
opportunities, such SUs may not want to invest efforts to follow the rule and thus will transmit simultaneously 
with Pus. 
 
2 Radio jamming is the deliberate jamming, blocking or creating interference with authorized wireless network. A radio jammer i s 
a transmitter that tunes to the same frequency as the opponents’ receiving equipment and with the same type of modulation, 
with enough power to override any signal at the receiver.  
3 Radio Sniffing technique helps to decode all sorts of essential network configuration details easily with low -cost software 
radios. Sniffing information can aid attackers in optimizing and crafting attacks.  
4 RF spoofing refers to transmitting a fake signal meant to pretense as an actual signal.

5.4.6 Threats against ML system 2 
This section provides the relevant threats against the ML system implemented in O-RAN architecture. The threats listed 3 
here below are generic to cover the ML model and not refined at ML components (training and inference hosts) due to 4 
the various deployment scenarios that are considered for ML architecture/framework in O-RAN. The deployment 5 
scenarios are: 6 
1. Scenario 1.1: SMO/Non-RT RIC acts as both the ML training and inference host.  7 
2. Scenario 1.2: Non-RT RIC acts as the ML training host and the Near-RT RIC as the ML inference host 8 
3. Scenario 1.3: Non-RT RIC acts as the ML training host and the O-CU/O-DU as the ML inference host 9 
The involved components and interfaces within each scenario are:  10 
• Scenario 1.1: SMO/Non-RT RIC, Near-RT RIC, O-CU, O-DU, O-RU, SMO internal/O1/A1 interfaces 11 
• Scenario 1.2: SMO/Non-RT RIC, Near-RT RIC, O-CU, O-DU, O-RU, O1/O2/A1/E2 interfaces 12 
• Scenario 1.3: SMO/Non-RT RIC, O-CU, O-DU, O-RU, O1/O2 interfaces 13 
Threat ID T-ML-01 
Threat title Poisoning the ML training data (Data poisoning attacks) 
Threat 
description 
An attacker gains access to the training set of a machine learning model and alters the data (e.g. datasets that are 
assembled to train, test, and validate an ML system) before the training begins without the knowledge of the 
machine learning engineers. The training data will already be tampered with and has lost its original quality 
which will result in modeling on wrong data. Hence, the ML model will no longer be a reliable one since it was 
trained on bad data and therefore modelling, decisions, predictions, model classifications, detections, etc. will 
surely not be appropriate.  
Also, another scenario can be in a situation where a model is online and continues to learn during operational use, 
modifying its behavior over time. In this case, an attacker can feed the model with bad data and the model can 
learn from this bad data, and as a result, negatively impact its performance and retrain the ML system to do the 
wrong thing. 
 14

Threat title Altering a machine learning model (System manipulation and compromise of ML data confidentiality and 
privacy) 
Threat 
description 
An attacker can illegally access a machine learning model and alter its parameters and thereby influence how it 
produces results. This can lead to wrong prediction and might result in catastrophic decisions if the results of the 
predictions were being used to make key business decisions. 
Also, an attacker can extract sensitive or confidential data that, through training, are built right into the ML 
model.

 Threat

available. An attacker could use the public model as a cover for their malicious ML behavior.

Chapter 6 Security principles 1 
This chapter consists of a set of security principles that the O-RAN system should achieve. They provide high level and 2 
abstract statement of the intended solution to countering potential Threats.  In the future, this chapter is expected to be 3 
normative, therefore these security principles will be developed and refined into security requirements, 4 
recommendations and potential countermeasures in order to clearly identify what is required (SHALL), recommended 5 
(SHOULD) or optional (MAY). In the current version, only ‘SHOULD’ is used within the security principles to make 6 
recommendations.  7 
6.1 Principles (SP) 8 
6.1.1 SP- AUTH Mutual Authentication 9 
• Mutual authentication SHOULD be established to allow the O-RAN system verifying who performs what, thus 10 
possible to detect fake base stations, unauthorized or malicious components, malicious applications and 11 
malicious users/administrators. To authenticate each component, a unique identifier and one or more 12 
credentials that SHOULD be kept secret are needed. 13 
6.1.2 SP- ACC Access Control 14 
• The O-RAN system SHOULD forbid unauthorized administrators or components to access O-RAN resources 15 
or services anytime and anywhere. Access controls are required for: 16 
o Network Access Controls for filtering unauthorized/unexpected traffic in the O-RAN components 17 
over their interfaces. 18 
o Access controls to restrict access to component configurations. 19 
o Access controls for hardware to maintain the trust chain. 20 
6.1.3 SP- CRYPTO Secure cryptographic, key management and PKI  21 
• Well-known, standardized, secure and unbroken cryptographic schemes and protocols SHOULD be used. 22 
Proprietary schemes and protocols SHOULD be avoided.  23 
• A secure key management of O-RAN keys (KgNB, KRRC-enc, KRRC-int, KUP-int, and KUP-enc, ksn) 24 
SHOULD be implemented to manage all the steps of key lifecycle: key generation using an appropriate level 25 
of entropy from a reliable source, secure key storage, key rotation and revocation, secure key destruction, etc. 26 
• Reliable PKI for authentication and data encryption SHOULD be used. Public CAs SHOULD be supported. 27 
The certificates SHOULD be issued by a trusted or rooted Certificate Authority (CA). The CA implements the 28 
Certificate Policy which specifies the rules and policies about who may or may not receive a Certificate. 29 
Relying parties can access the Certificate Policy to determine what validation/verification checks were 30 
performed prior to certificate issuance.  31 
• Each O-RU SHOULD be configured with lists of algorithms which are allowed for usage. There SHOULD be 32 
one list for integrity algorithms, and one for ciphering algorithms. These lists SHOULD be ordered according 33 
to a priority decided by the operator. 34 
6.1.4 SP- TCOMM Trusted Communication 35 
• Integrity, confidentiality, availability, authenticity and replay protection of resources SHOULD be ensured in 36 
transit (see ‘Critical Assets) over O-RAN interfaces according to industry best practices. 37

6.1.5 SP-SS Secure storage 1 
• Integrity, confidentiality, availability protection of resources SHOULD be ensured at rest (see ‘Critical Assets) 2 
according to industry best practices. Special attention SHOULD be paid to the protection of private keys, 3 
certificates and hash-values at rest. 4 
6.1.6 SP-SB Secure boot and self-configuration 5 
• O-RAN components SHOULD secure their underlying firmware and configuration to provide the opportunity 6 
for trust to be extended higher in the software stack. Verified platform firmware can, in turn, verify the 7 
operating system (OS) boot loader, which can then verify other software components all the way up to the OS 8 
itself, the hypervisor or container runtime layers and O-RAN components. The transitive trust SHOULD be 9 
consistent with the concept of the chain of trust (CoT)-a method where each piece of code in the boot process 10 
measures and checks the signature of the next stage of the boot process before the software boots. 11 
• The secure boot process, signature verification and self-configuration SHOULD be securely and properly 12 
implemented for all O-RAN components to authenticate them before loading. 13 
6.1.7 SP-UPDT Secure Update 14 
• A secure update management process SHOULD be implemented for introducing a new component or software 15 
change into the O-RAN system. The process SHOULD consider the ability to update the cryptographic 16 
algorithms and to adapt to upcoming O-RAN security challenges. O-RAN vendors SHOULD constantly 17 
provide ongoing security monitoring and patches for all of O-RAN components. A timely update cycles 18 
if vulnerabilities are discovered SHOULD be in place. 19 
6.1.8 SP-RECO Recoverability & Backup 20 
• Recoverability process in terms of the capacity of recovery in case of denial of service SHOULD be 21 
implemented. An approach for detecting and mitigating DoS attacks SHOULD be in place. 22 
• O-RAN vendors SHOULD define a recovery plan that resets the O-RAN components to a trustworthy state in 23 
case of a malfunction or an attack (e.g. DoS). 24 
• Backup systems SHOULD be in place to allow data or component on the O-RAN to be secured. Backup 25 
systems SHOULD ensure a suitable level of data or component availability and reliability. 26 
6.1.9 SP-OPNS Security management of risks in open source components  27 
• Vendors using open source code SHOULD enhance its security by applying industry coding best practices. It 28 
is recommended that vendors practice a higher level of due diligence for exposure to public exploits when 29 
using Open Source code.  30 
• A Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) SHOULD be maintained to understand which open source components 31 
are in use and where. 32 
• Security Analysis (Audit, vulnerability scan, etc.) SHOULD be performed to ensure all identified components 33 
are free of security vulnerabilities. 34 
• Because of the pull operation mode of the open source software, a proper policy and process SHOULD be in 35 
place for identifying and patching known issues with the open source components. Open source software 36 
components SHOULD be kept up to date and patched. 37 
6.1.10 SP-ASSU Security Assurance 38 
• Mobile networks are classified as critical infrastructure making security assurance especially more than 39 
relevant: 40

6.1.11 SP-PRV Privacy 9 
• In O-RAN, the privacy of end users SHOULD be considered. The privacy of end users can be divided into data 10 
privacy, identity privacy and personal information privacy. Most Communication services are to gather data 11 
and personal information around end users themselves, which may reveal information sensitive to their 12 
privacy. Adversaries would further extract more personal information about end users, such as UE priority, 13 
location information, trajectory, and preference. 14 
6.1.12 SP-SLC Continuous security development, testing, logging, monitoring 15 
and vulnerability handling 16 
• Continuous development and continuous integration (CD/CI) with continuous regression testing and software 17 
security auditing SHOULD be implemented. 18 
• Relevant activities events SHOULD be logged and logs collected SHOULD be analyzed in real time for the 19 
identification of potential security attacks and for security auditing.  20 
• Continuous monitoring SHOULD be implemented to verify that the wanted security state is maintained 21 
throughout the lifecycle of deployed O-RAN components. 22 
• Vulnerability management SHOULD be in place with intelligence to continuously track, identify and 23 
remediate vulnerable applications. Vendors SHOULD keep track of any new vulnerabilities discovered and is 24 
ready to act on customer product security incidents and reported security issues affecting O-RAN components. 25 
6.1.13 SP-ISO Robust Isolation 26 
• In a multi-vendor environment, intra-domain host isolation SHOULD be enforced. In the same host, VMs, 27 
CNs, virtualization/container layer, CPU, storage, and network security isolation of resources SHOULD be 28 
ensured by implementing system security orchestration, segmentation, lifecycle management, time scheduling, 29 
monitoring and audit on the management, signaling, control and data planes, and the execution of virtualized 30 
O-RAN components. 31 
6.1.14 SP-PHY Physical security 32 
• The O-RAN system SHOULD be located at physically secure environment in a way that minimizes the risk of 33 
resource theft and destruction. It SHOULD support secure storage of sensitive data (cryptographic keys and 34 
configuration data), execution of sensitive functions (encryption/decryption, authentication), and execution of 35 
boot and update processes. 36 
• Special attention SHOULD be paid to the site intrusion and physical access threats against O-RU sites. 37 
Consequently O-RU equipment SHOULD disable all unnecessary physical and logical ports, protocols and 38 
interfaces. In addition, secure physical connections to O-RU for O&M operations SHOULD be implemented 39 
(e.g. secure laptop with secure credentials). 40

6.1.15 SP-CLD Secure cloud computing and virtualization 1 
• Defense methods SHOULD be implemented: virtual machine-based intrusion detection, virtual machine-based 2 
isolation, virtual machine-based kernel protection, virtual machine-based access control, and virtual machine-3 
based trusted computing. 4 
• To get a fully trusted virtualized application, all the layers in the stack from hardware to firmware to 5 
virtualized software SHOULD be trust, as it is impossible to protect a virtual machine or containers from the 6 
host system. 7 
6.1.17 SP-ROB Robustness 8 
• The O-RAN system SHOULD not only ensure the robustness of software or hardware resources, but also 9 
guarantee the robustness of the cognitive radio channel for meeting the QoS of communication services 10 
required by users. In some scenarios, the robustness of spectrum sensing SHOULD be enhanced when some 11 
sensing nodes (e.g. O-RU) are easily malfunctioned. Robustness is an essential consideration for overcoming 12 
the security threats caused by jamming, DoS or DDoS attacks.  13
