- for NR access: O-CU-CP, O-CU-UP, O-DU or any combination;  16 
- for E-UTRA access: O-eNB. 17

optimal result to achieve stated objective. 20

optimization of RAN elements and resources via fine-grained data collection and actions over E2 interface. 22

SDAP protocol. 30 
O-DU: O-RAN Distributed Unit: a logical node hosting RLC/MAC/High-PHY layers based on a lower layer functional 31 
split. 32 
O-RU: O-RAN Radio Unit: a logical node hosting Low-PHY layer and RF processing based on a lower layer functional 33

extraction). 35

O-RAN architecture, including O-CU/O-DU/O-RU. 39

Chapter 3 Use Cases 1 
3.1 Use case 1: Context-Based Dynamic HO Management for V2X 2 
3.1.1 Background Information 3 
V2X communication allows for numerous potential  benefits such as increasing the overall road safety, reducing 4 
emissions, and saving time. Part of the V2X architecture is the V2X UE (SIM + device attached to vehicle) which 5 
communicates with the V2X Application Server (V2X AS). The exchanged information comprises Cooperative 6 
Awareness Messages (CAMs) from UE to V2X AS  [21], radio cell IDs, connection IDs, and basic radio measurements 7 
(RSRP, RSPQ etc.). 8 
3.1.2 Motivation 9 
As vehicles traverse along a highway, due to their high speed and the heterogeneous natural environment V2X UE -s are 10 
handed over frequently, at times in a suboptimal way, which may cause handover (HO) anomalies: e.g., short stay, ping-11 
pong, and remote cell.  Such suboptimal HO sequences substantially impair the functionality of V2X applications. Since 12 
HO sequences are mainly determined by the Neighbour Relation Tables (NRTs), maintained by the xNBs, there is hardly 13 
room for UE-level customization. 14 
This UC aims to present a method to avoid and/or resolve problematic HO scenarios by using past navigation and radio 15 
statistics in order to customize HO sequences on a UE level.  To this end, the AI/ML functionality that is enabled by the 16 
Near-RT RIC is employed. 17 
3.1.3 Proposed Solution 18 
 19 
Figure 3.1.3- 1: Dynamic Handover Management for V2X use case 20 
In order to prevent anomalous HO sequences causing performance degradation for the V2Xs, an xApp can be built with 21 
two main functionalities. Applying long-term analytics by using AI/ML algorithms is the first function expected from the 22 
xApp. As it is suggested by O-RAN framework, non-real-time intelligent management of RAN functions are deployed in 23 
the Non-RT RIC. V2X AS maintains the UE-based HO events and mobility data which are shared with Non-RT RIC over 24 
O1 interface. Hence, Non-RT RIC can identify causes  of HO anomalies and discover optimal HO sequences. Finally, a 25 
data base is maintained to keep track of resolutions to anomalies based on these feedbacks. The other function of the 26

xApp is performing real-time optimization which is offered by trained ML model on the Near-RT RIC. Near-RT RIC will 1 
monitor UE specific real -time mobility context based on V2X data.  Deployed ML model is used to detect/predict 2 
unexpected HO events and generate desired HO sequence which can eliminate/avoid HO anomaly.  Finally, Near-RT 3 
RIC is expected to create and update UE-specific NRTs to improve performance of the V2X users.  4 
The xNB is assumed to host, besides the default NRT, also UE-specific NRTs (UE-NRTs) for V2X (and potentially other 5 
types of) users. If the UE-NRT for a specific V2X user exists, it is used. If not, the default NRT remains valid. 6 
The input samples for handover profiling can come from the V2X AS and the xNB:  7 
 CAMs, navigation data, geo data (position, velocity, direction), 8 
 Radio cell ID, connection ID, basic radio measurements (RSRP, RSPQ etc.), 9 
 Handover event (source and target cell IDs), 10 
 Handover success / error codes.  11 
 12 
The long -term analytics function and the real -time optimization function can be thought of as ML training and ML 13 
inference, respectively. 14 
3.1.4 Benefits of O-RAN Architecture 15

3.6 Use case 6: Massive MIMO Beamforming Optimization 14 
3.6.1 Background Information 15 
Massive MIMO is seen as one of the key technologies for 5G. Due to the multi -antenna transmission and reception, this 16 
technology can inherently to provide diversity and improve capacity by targeting high gain antenna beams towards on e 17 
or multiple subscribers, thus improving the receive power levels and spatially filtering the interference from neighboring 18 
subscribers and transmission points. In addition, a spatial multiplexing operations regime can improve the network 19 
capacity by transferring multiple data streams towards/from one or different subscribers utilizing a spatial re -use of the 20 
scarce time/frequency resource blocks. Further advantages include the controlling of electromagnetic (EM) emissions or 21 
advanced network management te chnologies like beam shaping, beam-based load balancing, optimized beam mobility, 22 
adaptive cell coverage areas, especially in highly dense urban 3D environments and mobile subscribers. In order to further 23 
optimize networks, e.g., maximize spectral efficien cy, optimize coverage, or maximize cell capacity, fully digital 24 
beamforming (BF) methods are to be employed for below 6 GHz frequency wireless telecommunications. Grid of Beams 25 
(GoB) is a BF method which aims at selectively covering regions of interest with a suitable subset of radio beams (chosen 26 
from a dictionary of possible beams).  Beam-based Mobility Robustness Optimization is a BF method enhancing beam 27 
specific mobility performance e.g. by adding beam specific individual offsets. Mobility Robustness Op timization is a 28

3.7 Use case 7: RAN Sharing 4 
3.7.1 Background Information 5 
One of the main challenges for network operators is to d eploy a massive number of services, while providing different 6 
Quality of Service (QoS) requirements and keeping reasonable the level of investment in the network. Network sharing 7 
is envisioned as an efficient and sustainable way to accelerate the deployment of 5G, while taking advantage of a common 8 
pool of physical infrastructures and resources, made available for two or more partner operators.  9 
Besides, regulatory requirements often force operators to provide coverage in not business attractive areas, caus ing 10 
profitability issues. To this end, RAN sharing is seen as a promising solution that should reduce network costs, increase 11 
network capacity and coverage, while enhancing customer satisfaction. Accordingly, the open and multivendor nature of 12 
the O-RAN architecture can accelerate the introduction and development of RAN sharing solutions, by enhancing the 13 
deployment of virtual network functions (VNF) on commodity shared hardware, while taking into account diverse QoS 14 
requirements. 15 
Among the different RAN-sharing models that have been experimented so far, a special focus is put here on the evaluation 16 
of the compatibility of the “Geographical Split” RAN sharing model with the O -RAN architecture. In such a model, a 17 
coverage area is split between two or more ope rators; each operator manages the RAN in a specific area, while offering 18 
access to its RAN resources to its operator partners. Two main configurations  have been used worldwide  [23] Multi 19 
Operator Core Network (MOCN) and Multi Operator RAN (MORAN). In MOCN, both the RAN infrastructure and 20 
carriers are pooled. Even though this model enables further cost saving, especially in rural areas due to a lower number 21 
of carriers, it requires t he presence of a regulatory entity that takes care of the allocation of different parts of the shared 22 
spectrum between operators. Conversely, in MORAN, each operator utilizes a separate carrier, while getting more 23 
freedom and independency on the control of the radio resources. MORAN is the most widely used sharing configuration 24 
as it can provide appropriate independency to each sharing partner operator, while maximizing the benefits of sharing in 25 
terms of CAPEX and OPEX. 26 
Besides, the O -RAN architecture can provide new opportunities for implementing this RAN sharing model in a more 27 
efficient way, thanks to its multi -vendor interfaces and abstraction control provided by the RIC (RAN Intelligent  28 
Controller). Accordingly, O-RAN can accelerate the deployment of 3 GPP-based solutions by providing more flexibility 29 
in the management of the shared resources. 30 
3.7.2 Motivation 31 
Currently, in 3GPP there are ongoing discussions for identifying which RAN functions should be included in the RAN 32 
sharing procedures in 5G [4][17]. Specifically, it has been analysed the feasibility to share the whole or a part of the DU 33 
or CU functional blocks while assuming the sharing of a common physical layer (PHY). In  [20], a first agreement has 34 
been achieved stating that multiple logical CU -CP/ DU can control the same PHY radio resources but coordination 35 
between logical CU-CP needs to be ensured by an appropriate implementation (not standardized).  36 
In this context, O -RAN can be seen as the ideally enabler of the 3GPP RAN sharing model by providing the required 37 
coordination between the shared network nodes. To this end, the RIC can enable the coordination of multiple CU -CP/ 38 
DU via the E2 interface, while opening the road for  diverse RAN sharing scenarios. Moreover, O -RAN can accelerate 39 
the deployment of compliant 5G RAN sharing solutions by taking advantage of the multi -vendor nature of the F1, X2 40 
interfaces. 41

3.11 Use case 11: Dynamic Spectrum Sharing (DSS) 6 
3.11.1 Background Information 7 
As we transition from 4G to 5G, the spectral resour ces used for 5G deployment is a key consideration and this situation 8 
varies from one operator to another. Though, new C -band resources between 3 -6 GHz and mmWave bands have been 9 
acquired by operators, these bands suffer from great propagation and penetration loss, limiting their coverage to users in 10 
proximity of the cell site, this is compelling particularly on the  UL where the UE device  is power constrained . This 11 
mandates the requirement of 5G deployment on lower bands (i.e., below 2GHz), which are widely used in existing 4G 12 
LTE deployments. Operating on lower bands  along with non -standalone mode of 5G deployment  helps to cover large 13 
geography, enables seamless mobility between 4G and 5G while being sensitive to overall cost of deployment. In addition, 14 
DSS offers the advantage of dynamically sharing the available spectrum adapting to the changing work loads of 4G and 15 
5G network.  16 
3.11.2 Motivation 17 
DSS is compelling considering the need for operators to dynamically share already deployed spectral resources be tween 18 
LTE and NR devices without degrading the QoE of the current 4G subscribers while offering the same level of coverage 19 
to support NR devices, with the consideration that they will be rolled out in an incremental manner. The objective of this 20 
use case is to propose DSS in the context of the O-RAN architecture, specifically to realize it as an application in the RIC 21 
framework. This would particularly benefit vRAN implementations when the 4G/5G CU/DU are from different vendors 22 
and one could leverage RAN data over ORAN’s framework for traffic prediction, resource management and control 23 
functions. Towards this, we identify the intelligent control functions which can be realized as a DSS application to 24 
augment the L2/L1 control functions defined as part of LTE-NR coexistence in Rel-15/16.   25

3.13 Use case 13: Local Indoor Positioning in RAN 5 
3.13.1 Background Information 6 
Cellular network based positioning is an important technology for 5G vertical industries, individuals and operators , 7 
especially in local indoor scenarios. For instance, Mall can provide value-added services, such as local indoor navigation 8 
and shop recommendation by leverage real -time indoor positioning . Industrial manufacturing field shall send real -time 9 
safe warning to remind operators keep away from the dangerous area which would also need real-time indoor positioning. 10 
NR positioning is introduced by 3GPP Rel.16. The location management function (LMF) resides in core network acts as 11 
a location server. LTE positioning protocol (LPP) is reused for the UE measurements  and NR positioning Protocol “a” 12 
(NRPPa) based on LPPa is used for the gNB measurements [11]. The NR Positioning Protocol A (NRPPa)  PDUs carry 13 
E-CID, OTDOA are routed between eNB/gNB and the LMF via AMF. The AMF routes the NRPPa PDUs transparently 14 
based on a Routing ID corresponding t o the involved LMF over NG -C interface without knowledge of the involved 15 
NRPPa transaction. Th is long route messages between eNB/gNB and centralized LMF may  suffer network jitters and 16 
leads to un-real-time UE location results. For some local indoor scenario, e.g., mall and industrial manufacturing, it would 17 
be better to directly deploy the positioning function in the RAN side and expose UE location to local applications.  18 
3.13.2 Motivation 19 
For local indoor scenarios, the positioning function inside RAN is envisioned to be a promising solution. It not only 20 
reduces the latency of positioning but also can reuse the edge cloud infrastructure  in RAN. In the context of O -RAN 21 
architecture, the positioning function can be deployed as a positioning xApp in t he Near-RT RIC. The positioning xApp 22 
computes the UE location and optional velocity based on the positioning measurement obtained via the E2 interface.  23 
One example is that distributed indoor small base station in the operator’s domain can provide UE positioning service by 24 
adding Near-RT RIC with positioning xApp. The positioning related information report from small base station  to the 25 
positioning xApp inside Near-RT RIC can be measured based on the uplink SRS information.  By leveraging multi-point 26 
positioning, field strength positioning and other positioning algorithms , positioning xApp inside Near-RT RIC  can 27 
conduct a real-time position of UE. 28

3.14 Use case 14: Massive MIMO SU/MU-MIMO Grouping 16 
Optimization 17 
3.14.1 Background Information 18 
The Massive MIMO is one of the key technologies for 4G and 5G. Due to the multi-antenna transmission and reception, 19 
this technology can inherently provide diversity and improve capacity by targeting high gain antenna beams towards one 20 
or multiple subscribers, thus improving the receive power levels and spatially filtering the interference from neighboring 21 
subscribers and transmission points.  The case that only one subscriber is served by a given physical resource block of a 22 
massive MIMO cell in a transmission interval is called single -user MIMO (SU -MIMO). While the case that multiple 23 
subscribers are served by a given physical resource block of a massive MIMO cell in a transmission interval is called 24 
multi-user MIMO (MU-MIMO). 25 
In the commercial deployment, the massive MIMO eNB/gNB support both SU-MIMO and MU-MIMO transmission. The 26 
SU-MIMO and MU -MIMO are targeting for different scenarios, and can achieve different spectral efficiency. Some 27 
subscribers can be stationary, some subscribers can be pedestrian, and some subscribers can be moving at a high speed. 28 
The performance of MU-MIMO is very susceptible to subscriber’ s moving speed, while the performance of SU -MIMO 29 
is relatively robust to subscriber’s moving speed. Additionally, some subscribers may have VoLTE or VoNR traffic, some 30 
subscribers may have download/FTP traffic, and some subscribers may have YouTube -like traffic. The performance of 31 
MU-MIMO considerably reduces in the low traffic volume scenario, while the performance of SU -MIMO is relatively 32 
robust to low traffic scenarios. 33 
The spectral efficiency of SU-MIMO and MU-MIMO differ from each other. The spectral efficiency of MU -MIMO can 34 
be several times of spectral efficiency of SU-MIMO. Furthermore, SU-MIMO and MU-MIMO have different requirement 35 
for C -DRX, SRS, and other RRC configurations. These RRC configurations can be optimized based on scenario 36 
information.  37

3.15 Use case 15: O-RAN Signalling Storm Protection 10 
3.15.1 Background Information 11 
Society is increasingly dependent on network connectivity at any time and in any place and increasing diversity of device 12 
types ranging from complex devices such as smart phone to very simple and low -cost IoT devices are connecting to the 13 
network. The sheer number of connected devices, as well as the wide range of device types, makes the mobility network 14 
subject to accidental or intentional atta cks that may disrupt the regular usage of the network. Given that life -critical 15 
applications are moving to wireless networks, such network disruptions are not only an inconvenience but may have 16 
impact on life and health of individuals. The O-RAN architecture offers an opportunity to address such security challenges 17 
in customizable and creative ways by utilizing the near-real time RIC xApps and non-real time RIC rApps.  18 
3.15.2 Motivation 19 
The main defense mechanism against attacks coming from the devices towa rd the network is based on configuration of 20 
the devices themselves and trust that the devices will indeed comply to restrictions defined by mobility standards. One 21 
such defense mechanism is the back -off timer that restricts the number of repeated device re gistrations, thus preventing 22 
devices from overloading the network with attaches. If this trust is breached there are no other options for defending the 23 
network rather than rejecting (denying service) randomly to both benign and malicious devices, a state which is equivalent 24 
to DDoS. Unfortunately, even today the network has few hundreds of device types that accidently breach this trust and 25 
allow devices to aggressively attach to the network in a rate of few thousand times per hour (the maximum allowed 26 
number by standard is less than 20 attaches per hour). An attacker that finds a way to manipulate a large set of these 27

vulnerable devices remotely can cause an attach storm that would lead to a long outage of large parts of the network. 1 
Furthermore, this attacker can continue this attack over many hours, each time picking few thousand of devices from a 2 
large pool of millions of devices available; the network carrier will not be able to stop this attack without intelligent and  3

different prioritized transmission multiplexing and etc. 20 
PDCP duplication and EHC is applied to the corresponding DRB. Improper configuration of PDCP duplication and EHC 21

configured to listen to the Pre-emption Indication (PI) or Cancellation Indication (CI) depends on the service 28 
characteristics and network conditions. Improper configuration of PI or CI related configurations will cause invalid 29 
monitoring and power consumptions. 30 
Therefore, the the enhancement mechanisms for IIoT shall be semi-static or dynamic configured for some specific users 31 
and some specific service s based on the service prediction and performance prediction, considering the variant network 32 
load and channel quality/interference environment. AI/ML can be used for service prediction and KPI prediction to infer 33 
and derive the strategies. 34

3.18 Use case 18: BBU Pooling to achieve RAN Elasticity 23 
3.18.1 Background Information 24 
Driving cost efficiency by improving the elasticity of workloads is one of the principal goals of cloudification. This use 25 
case presents BBU pooling as an opportunity to achieve RAN elasticity. O -RAN CADS describes multiple deployment 26 
options for the O-RAN cloudified NFs, which allows for different variants of centralization of these resources in the edge 27 
and regional clouds. In particular, cloudified BBUs can be deployed on a common hardware pool, centralized at the same 28 
cloud location. This centralization opens up the opportunity for O-RUs to be flexibly mapped to BBUs, thereby enabling 29 
RAN elasticity, while potentially lowering the costs in the long term  30 
3.18.2 Motivation 31 
Pooling of BBU resources provides many benefits like having to maintain only a single large, controlled environment for 32 
many cell sites, better resiliency and agility to deal with unexpected failures and increases in demands, and allowing 33 
capacity to be added incrementally and assigned to cell sites as needed resulting in better overall utilization. More 34 
importantly, BBU Pooling provides significant statistical multiplexing benefits due to better resource consolidation. The 35 
total resources required from the shared pool will be less than total resources required across distributed locations, because 36 
the peak of the sum of the traffic will generally be lower than the sum of the individual cell site traffic peaks. BBU Pooling 37 
enables more aggressive capacity dimensioning based on different percentiles of resource consumption rather than for the 38 
peak consumption. This proposal addresses the scenarios where both O -CUs and O-DUs can pooled, but for simplicity 39 
the following sections focuses on the description of O-DU pooling.   40

3.18.3 Proposed Solution 1 
Figure 3.18.3-1 shows a high-level depiction of approaches to O-DU Pooling. The O-DU resources for multiple cell sites 2 
are pooled at a single centralized location or the O-DU Pool . The Cloud Access Switch (CAS) at the O -DU Pool 3 
aggregates traffic from the multiple Front Haul Gateways and routes it to the appropriate O -DU blade based on the load 4 
balancing requirement. For the O-DU pooling use case, it is assumed that an open Front Haul exists with O-RAN defined 5 
7-2x split. Based on the association between O-RUs and O-DUs, and the granularity at which the traffic is assigned from 6 
the O-RUs to the O-DUs, we define the following classes of O-DU pooling.  7 
1) Class 0 pooling: A scenario where an O -RU is assigned to a single specific O -DU statically, and the traffic from 8 
the O -RU is not split into subsets that could be assigned to different O -DUs. This is the same as Simple 9 
Centralization as defined in CADS V2.0. With Class 0 pooling, re -assigning an O-RU to connect to a different O -10 
DU would require significant “hands on” activity, and is performed very infrequently during specific maintenance 11 
windows.   12 
2) Class 1 pooling: A scenario where n O-RUs are initially assigned to a single O-DU during a specific period of time, 13 
but the O-RUs can be re-assigned to different O-DU at any point of time via orchestration procedures with the help 14 
of SMO. This automated re -assignment can be triggered outside maintenance windows, based on various 15 
performance criterion or for load balancing. Like with Class 0 pooling, the traffic from the O -RU is not split into 16 
subsets that could be assigned to different O-DUs. 17 
3) Class 2 pooling: A scenario where n O -RUs are assigned to m O -DUs, and subsets of traffic from one O -RU are 18 
dynamically distributed (and load-balanced) across the O-DU resources within the O-DU pool using a cluster aware 19 
scheduler.   20

With all classes of O -DU pooling, the total resources required from the shared pool will be less than total resources 24 
required across distributed locations, because the peak of the sum of the traffic will generally be lower than the sum of 25 
the individual cell site traffic peaks. In particular, Class 1 and Class 2 O -DU pooling enables more aggressive capacity 26 
dimensioning based on different percentiles of resource consumption rather than for the peak consumption. However, the 27 
key distinction between the classes of pooling is the granularity at which traffic can be load balanced across the O -DUs, 28 
which provides different statistical multiplexing benefits. While Class 0 pooling provides a one -time statistical 29 
multiplexing gains at the time of initial deployment due to the centralization, Class 1 pooling allows further load balancing 30 
benefits due to the automated and flexible re-mapping of O-RUs to the O-DUs periodically. However, the re-assignment 31 
of O-RUs needs to be performed during maintenance windows (or via hitless migration) to minimize service interruptions. 32 
Finally, Class 1 Pooling enables better handling of hot spots, and more efficient management of traffic loads and 33 
maintenance/upgrades. 34

3.21 Use case 21: Energy Saving 7 
3.21.1 Background Information 8 
Energy Consumption (EC) of the RAN is an important topic for network operators, especially for 5G network. RAN 9 
Energy Saving (ES) depends on rigorous planning and configuration. Due to the varying nature of traffic load and to user 10 
mobility, the optimization of EC of the RAN is complex and can be applied to different network layers and in different 11 
time scales. There is a risk that RAN equipment consume much energy while serving low traffic, or even no traffic at all. 12 
Different ES features are investigated in industry, some of which are deployed in operational networks. Examples are 13 
deep sleep mode (e.g., shut down of a base station of a given technology); carrier shut down; and RF channels’ switch 14 
off/on). More recently, short time scales ES mechanisms have been proposed, at symbol -, subframe- and frame-levels, 15 
known as Advanced Sleep Modes (ASM).        16 
O-RU is responsible for the major part of the mobile network EC. EC of VNFs have not yet been standardized in Release 17 
17 of 3GPP and can be only estimated, e.g. based on mean vCPU usage of the underlying VMs. EC measurements will 18 
be further addressed in release 18. In O-RAN O-Cloud, the only feature with relation to ES is scale in/out that is mentioned 19 
in the use case document [26]. Sub -use cases involving scale-in / scale -out processes, workload placement or HW 20 
processors’ sleep modes are FFS. 21 
3.21.2 Motivation 22 
ES for legacy and 5G networks can be carried out using manual configuration or SON functions. 3GPP defines both 23 
centralized and distributed ES features  [6], which are mainly targeting intra - or inter-RAT cell on/off switching. The 24 
motivation of the ES use case is to leverage on ORAN AI/ML services and open interfaces in order to introduce optimized 25 
ES and EE solutions involving switching off/on of different network components at different time scale. Will be 26 
considered off/on switching solutions supported by 3GPP configurations or supported by propriety implementations that 27 
require additional standardization. 28
