Chapter 3 Use Cases 1 
3.1 Use case 1: Context-Based Dynamic HO Management for V2X 2 
3.1.1 Background Information 3 
V2X communication allows for numerous potential benefits such as increasing the overall road safety, reducing 4 
emissions, and saving time. Part of the V2X architecture is the V2X UE (SIM + device attached to vehicle) which 5 
communicates with the V2X Application Server (V2X AS). The exchanged information comprises Cooperative 6 
Awareness Messages (CAMs) from UE to V2X AS  [18], radio cell IDs, connection IDs, and basic radio measurements 7 
(RSRP, RSPQ etc.). 8 
3.1.2 Motivation 9 
As vehicles traverse along a highway, due to their high speed and the heterogeneous natural environment V2X UE -s are 10 
handed over frequently, at times in a suboptimal way, which may cause handover (HO) anomalies: e.g., short stay, ping-11 
pong, and remote cell.  Such suboptimal HO sequences substantially impair the functionality of V2X applications. Since 12 
HO sequences are mainly determined by the Neighbour Relation Tables (NRTs), maintained by the xNBs, there is hardly 13 
room for UE-level customization. 14 
This UC aims to present a method to avoid and/or resolve problematic HO scenarios by using past navigation and radio 15 
statistics in order to customize HO sequences on a UE level.  To this end, the AI/ML functionality that is enabled by the 16 
Near-RT RIC is employed. 17 
3.1.3 Proposed Solution 18 
 19 
Figure 3.1.3- 1: Dynamic Handover Management for V2X use case 20 
In order to prevent anomalous HO sequences causing performance degradation for the V2Xs, an xApp can be built with 21 
two main functionalities. Applying long-term analytics by using AI/ML algorithms is the first function expected from the 22 
xApp. As it is suggested by O-RAN framework, non-real-time intelligent management of RAN functions are deployed in 23 
the Non-RT RIC. V2X AS maintains the UE-based HO events and mobility data which are shared with Non-RT RIC over 24 
O1 interface. Hence, Non-RT RIC can identify causes  of HO anomalies and discover optimal HO sequences. Finally, a 25 
data base is maintained to keep track of resolutions to anoma lies based on these feedbacks. The other function of the 26

The long-term optimization function can jointly process UE level radio and navigation dat a. Both the analysis and the 16

3.1.5 Notes / FFS 18 
An option for associating the data coming from the gNB and that coming from the V2X AS can  be achieved by the 19 
ECGI+C-RNTI [3][8]. The ECGI uniquely identifies the cell, while the C -RNTI uniquely identifies a UE within a gNB. 20 
The ECGI+C-RNTI pair constitutes a globally unique UE identifier. 21 
3.2 Use case 2: Flight Path Based Dynamic UAV Radio Resource 22 
Allocation 23 
3.2.1 Background Information 24 
This use case provides the background, motivation, and requirements for the support the use case of flight path based 25 
dynamic UAV Radio Resource Allocation, allowing operators to adjust radio resource allocation policies through the O -26 
RAN architecture, reducing unnecessary handover and improving radio resource utilizati on. 27 
3.2.2 Motivation 28 
The field trials’ results show that the coverage for low altitude is good and can provide various services for terrestrial UEs 29 
with good performance. However, since the site along the flight is mainly for terrestrial UEs, the altitude of the UAV is 30 
always not within the main lobe of the ground station antenna. And the side lobes give rise to the phenomenon of scattered 31 
cell associations particularly noticeable in the sky. The cell association pattern on the ground is ideally contiguous area 32 
where the best cell is most often the one closest to the UE. As the UE move up in he ight, the antenna side lobes start to 33 
be visible, and the best cell may no longer be the closest one. The cell association pattern in this particular scenario 34

 6

3.2.3 Proposed Solution 10 
To manage resource allocation of UAV s based on Flight P ath, Non-RT RIC retrieves necessary Aerial Vehicles related 11 
metrics from network level measurement reports. Moreover , flight path information o f Aerial V ehicle, climate 12 
information, flight forbidden/limitation area information  and Space Load information ar e received from an application, 13 
e.g. UTM (Unmanned Traffic Management) for constructing/training relevant AI/ML model. For example, this could be 14 
UL/DL interference from/to Aerial vehicles, the detection of Aerial Vehicle UEs, and available radio resource (e.g. 15 
frequency, cell, beam, BWP, numerology) prediction. The Near-RT RIC supports deployment and execution of AI/ML 16 
model from Non-RT RIC. Based on this, Near-RT RIC can perform the radio resource allocation for UAV s considering 17 
the radio channel conditio n, flight path information and other application information  with on -demand coverage . 18 
Moreover, Aerial Vehicles performance reports should be sent to Non-RT RIC for evaluation and optimization of the ML 19 
model. 20

3.6 Use case 6: Massive MIMO Beamforming Optimization 24 
3.6.1 Background Information 25 
Massive MIMO is seen as one of the key technologies for 5G. Due to the multi -antenna transmission and reception, this 26 
technology can inherently to provide divers ity and improve capacity by targeting high gain antenna beams towards one 27 
or multiple subscribers, thus improving the receive power levels and spatially filtering the interference from neighboring 28 
subscribers and transmission points. In addition, a spatial  multiplexing operations regime can improve the network 29 
capacity by transferring multiple data streams towards/from one or different subscribers utilizing a spatial re -use of the 30 
scarce time/frequency resource blocks. Further advantages include the control ling of electromagnetic (EM) emissions or 31 
advanced network management technologies like beam shaping, beam-based load balancing, optimized beam mobility, 32 
adaptive cell coverage areas, especially in highly dense urban 3D environments and mobile subscribers. In order to further 33

from a dictionary of possible beams).  Beam-based Mobility Robustness Optimization is a BF method enhancing beam 37 
specific mobility performance  e.g. by adding beam specific individual offsets. Mobility Robustness Optimization is a 38 
well-known SON concept [9][13]. First supporting measurements have been specified in LTE in Release 9 [8] 39

3.7 Use case 7: RAN Sharing 18 
3.7.1 Background Information 19 
One of the main challenges for network operators is to deploy a massive numbe r of services, while providing different 20 
Quality of Service (QoS) requirements and keeping reasonable the level of investment in the network. Network sharing 21 
is envisioned as an efficient and sustainable way to accelerate the deployment of 5G, while taking advantage of a common 22 
pool of physical infrastructures and resources, made available for two or more partner operators.  23 
Besides, regulatory requirements often force operators to provide coverage in not business attractive areas, causing 24 
profitability issues. To this end, RAN sharing is seen as a promising solution that should reduce network costs, increase 25 
network capacity and coverage, while enhancing customer satisfaction. Accordingly, the open and multivendor nature of 26 
the O-RAN architecture can accele rate the introduction and development of RAN sharing solutions, by enhancing the 27 
deployment of virtual network functions (VNF) on commodity shared hardware, while taking into account diverse QoS 28 
requirements. 29 
Among the different RAN-sharing models that have been experimented so far, a special focus is put here on the evaluation 30 
of the compatibility of the “Geographical Split” RAN sharing model with the O -RAN architecture. In such a model, a 31 
coverage area is split between two or more operators; each operator  manages the RAN in a specific area, while offering 32 
access to its RAN resources to its operator partners. Two main configurations  have been used worldwide  [20] Multi 33 
Operator Core Network (MOCN) and Multi Operator RAN (MORAN). I n MOCN, both the RAN infrastructure and 34 
carriers are pooled. Even though this model enables further cost saving, especially in rural areas due to a lower number 35 
of carriers, it requires the presence of a regulatory entity that takes care of the allocation of different parts of the shared 36 
spectrum between operators. Conversely, in MORAN, each operator utilizes a separate carrier, while getting more 37 
freedom and independency on the control of the radio resources. MORAN is the most widely used sharing configura tion 38 
as it can provide appropriate independency to each sharing partner operator, while maximizing the benefits of sharing in 39 
terms of CAPEX and OPEX. 40

3.8 Use case 8: QoS Based Resource Optimization 5 
3.8.1 Background Information 6 
QoS based resource optimization can used when the network has been configured to provide some kind of preferential 7

this case, the network has functionality that ensures resource isolation between slices as well as functionality to monitor 9 
that slice Service Level Specifications (SLS) are fulfilled.  10 
In RAN, it is the scheduler that ensures that Physical Resource Block (PRB) resources are isolated between slices in the 11 
best possible way and also that the PRB resources are used in an optimal way to best fulfill the SLS for different slices. 12 
The desired default RAN behavior for slices is configured over O1. For example, the ratio of physical resources (PRBs) 13 
reserved for a slice is configured at slice creation (instantiation) over O1. Also, QoS can be configured to guide the RAN 14 
scheduler how to (in real-time) allocate PRB resources to different users to best fulfill the SLS of a specific slice. In the 15 
NR NRM this is described by the resource partition attribute. 16 
Instantiation of a RAN sub -slice will be prepared by rigorou s planning to understand to what extent deployed RAN 17 
resources will be able to support RAN sub-slice SLS. Part of this procedure is to configure RAN functionality according 18 
to above. With this, a default behavior of RAN is obtained that will be able to ful fill slice SLSs for most situations. 19 
However, even through rigorous planning, there will be times and places where the RAN resources are not enough to 20 
fulfill SLS given the default configuration. To understand how often (and where) this happens, the performance of a RAN 21 
slice will continuously be monitored by SMO. When SMO detects a situation when RAN SLS cannot be fulfilled, Non-22 
RT RIC can use A1 policies to improve the situation. To understand how to utilize A1 policies and how to resolve the 23 
situation, the non RT-RIC will use additional information available in SMO.  24

3.15 Use case 15: O-RAN Signalling Storm Protection 10 
3.15.1 Background Information 11 
Society is increasingly dependent on network connectivity at any time and in any place and increasing diversity of device 12 
types ranging from complex devices such as smart phone to very simple and low -cost IoT devices are connecting to the 13 
network. The sheer number of connected devices, as well as the wide range of device types, makes the mobility network 14 
subject to accidental or intentional attacks that may disrupt the regular usage of the network. Given that life -critical 15 
applications are moving to wireless networks, such network disruptions are not only an inconve nience but may have 16 
impact on life and health of individuals. The O-RAN architecture offers an opportunity to address such security challenges 17 
in customizable and creative ways by utilizing the near-real time RIC xApps and non-real time RIC rApps.  18 
3.15.2 Motivation 19 
The main defense mechanism against attacks coming from the devices toward the network is based on configuration of 20 
the devices themselves and trust that the devices will indeed comply to restrictions defined by mobility standards. One 21 
such defense mechanism is the back -off timer that restricts the number of repeated device registrations, thus preventing 22 
devices from overloading the network with attaches. If this trust is breached there are no other options for defending the 23 
network rather than rejecting (denying service) randomly to both benign and malicious devices, a state which is equivalent 24 
to DDoS. Unfortunately, even today the network has few hundreds of device types that accidently breach this trust and 25 
allow devices to aggressively attach to the network in a rate of few thousand times per hour (the maximum allowed 26 
number by standard is less than 20 attaches per hour). An attacker that finds a way to manipulate a large set of these 27

vulnerable devices remotely can cause an attach storm that w ould lead to a long outage of large parts of the network. 1 
Furthermore, this attacker can continue this attack over many hours, each time picking few thousand of devices from a 2 
large pool of millions of devices available; the network carrier will not be abl e to stop this attack without intelligent and 3 
fine-grained controls to act against a certain patterns of behaviour. The good news is that detecting these aggressive 4

This restriction should be smart enough to still allow benign devices to register to the network withou t interruption. 7 
Having smart security control at the RAN can stop such attack and without overloading deeper parts of the network in the 8

3.15.3 Proposed Solution 10 
In order to protect the network from such UE originated signalling storms, an xApp can b e built with two main 11 
functionalities: a DDoS detection capability and a DDoS mitigation capability. The DDoS detection capability has two 12 
parts: the near real time detection, which takes place in a RIC xApp and a non -real time detection, which takes place  at 13 
the SMO and relies on enrichment data originated in external system (e.g., 5G Core or OAM system) (see Figure 1). The 14 
reason for this functionality split is that some detection logic relies on information that is available only at the core (su ch 15 
as IMSI, IMEI, device types, PLMN etc.), while for other detection logic, the information available at the RAN will 16 
suffice. It is assumed that the Near Real-Time RIC DDoS Detection xApp provides a faster response with coarse grained 17 
aggressive device detection while the 5G Core detection results computed by the SMO rApp provide slower response 18 
with finer grained aggressive device detection. It is noted that for some attack scenarios the xApp can perform the 19 
detection without the help of external information coming from the Non Real-Time RIC. The DDoS Mitigation xApp can 20 
implement an E2 INSERT -CONTROL control loop where the xApp can decide for each attach request if it should be 21

including factory automation, transport industry. To satisfy these scenarios requirements, several key features as below 20 
have been supported for IIoT in 5G system, such as data duplication and multi -connectivity enhancement (PDCP 21 
duplication, PDU session duplication, QoS flow duplication), Time Sensitive Networking (Ethernet Header Compression 22 
(EHC), Accurate reference timing provisioning, QoS and scheduling enhancements) and different prioritized transmission 23 
multiplexing. 24

Network deployment of IIoT use case includes both public network and non -public network (NPN). For public network, 9 
network slicing may be used to implement the resource isolation to guarantee the performance of IIoT use case. For NPN 10 
or private network, it can have different ways to implement the network deployment. For example, one way is to deploy 11 
private network equipment completely, such as gNB, UPF and 5GC. The network equipment can only provide service s 12 
for the private use. The other way is to use network slicing to implement a virtual NPN , which means only some of the 13 
network equipment are deployed for private use, such as gNB. Meanwhile, the others, such as UPF, 5GC, are shared with 14 
the public network. The networ k resource isolation can be implemented by network slicing. If network slicing is used , 15 
IIoT use case only focuses on optimization for the specific piece of slicing bearing IIoT traffic.   16 
3.17.2 Motivation 17 
The objective of the IIoT optimization use case is to guarantee reliability, and resource utilization efficiency in the above 18 
scenarios by  optimizing the enhancement mechanisms of related technique features, e.g. PDCP duplication, EHC, 19 
different prioritized transmission multiplexing and etc. 20 
PDCP duplication and EHC is applied to the corresponding DRB. Improper configuration of PDCP duplication and EHC 21 
will leads to the waste of HW/SW and transmission resources. What service or data radio bearer ( DRB) needs to use 22 
PDCP duplication is not only depends on the reliability requirement, but also related to the network environment, such as 23 
network load and channel quality/interference condition.  Besides, what service or DRB needs to use EHC is depends on 24 
the service characteristics, such as service type, traffic period, duration, and packet size. EHC is especially beneficial 25 
when payload sizes are relatively small compared to the overall size of the frame, which is typically the case in  IIoT 26 
networks based on Ethernet. Additionally, for different prioritized transmission multiplexing, which and where users are 27 
configured to listen to the Pre-emption Indication (PI) or Cancellation Indication (CI) depends on the service 28 
characteristics and network conditions. Improper configuration of PI or CI related configurations  will cause invalid 29 
monitoring and power consumptions. 30 
Therefore, the the enhancement mechanisms for IIoT shall be semi-static or dynamic configured for some specific users 31 
and some specific service s based on the service prediction and performance predicti on, considering the variant network 32 
load and channel quality/interference environment. AI/ML can be used for service prediction and KPI prediction to infer 33 
and derive the strategies. 34

3.18 Use case 18: BBU Pooling to achieve RAN Elasticity 23 
3.18.1 Background Information 24 
Driving cost efficiency by improving the elasticity of workloads is one of th e principal goals of cloudification. This use 25 
case presents BBU pooling as an opportunity to achieve RAN elasticity. O -RAN CADS describes multiple deployment 26 
options for the O-RAN cloudified NFs, which allows for different variants of centralization of these resources in the edge 27 
and regional clouds. In particular, cloudified BBUs can be deployed on a common hardware pool, centralized at the same 28 
cloud location. This centralization opens up the opportunity for O-RUs to be flexibly mapped to BBUs, thereby enabling 29 
RAN elasticity, while potentially lowering the costs in the long term  30 
3.18.2 Motivation 31 
Pooling of BBU resources provides many benefits like having to maintain only a single large, controlled environment for 32 
many cell sites, better resiliency and a gility to deal with unexpected failures and increases in demands, and allowing 33 
capacity to be added incrementally and assigned to cell sites as needed resulting in better overall utilization. More 34 
importantly, BBU Pooling provides significant statistical multiplexing benefits due to better resource consolidation. The 35 
total resources required from the shared pool will be less than total resources required across distributed locations, because 36 
the peak of the sum of the traffic will generally be lower than the sum of the individual cell site traffic peaks. BBU Pooling 37 
enables more aggressive capacity dimensioning based on different percentiles of resource consumption rather than for the 38 
peak consumption. This proposal addresses the scenarios where both O -CUs and O-DUs can pooled, but for simplicity 39 
the following sections focuses on the description of O-DU pooling.   40

3.18.3 Proposed Solution 1 
Figure 3.18.3-1 shows a high-level depiction of approaches to O-DU Pooling. The O-DU resources for multiple cell sites 2 
are pooled at a single centralized location or the O-DU Pool . The Cloud Access Switch (CAS) at the O -DU Pool 3 
aggregates traffic from the multiple Front Haul Gateways and routes it to the appropriate O -DU blade based on the load 4 
balancing requirement. For the O-DU pooling use case, it is assumed that an open Front Haul exists with O-RAN defined 5 
7-2x split. Based on the association between O-RUs and O-DUs, and the granularity at which the traffic is assigned from 6 
the O-RUs to the O-DUs, we define the following classes of O-DU pooling.  7 
1) Class 0 pooling: A scenario where an O -RU is assigned to a single specific O -DU statically, and the traffic from 8 
the O -RU is not split into subsets that could be assigned to different O -DUs. This is the same as Simple 9 
Centralization as defined in CADS V2.0. With Class 0 pooling, re -assigning an O-RU to connect to a different O -10 
DU would require significant “hands on” activity, and is performed very infrequently during specific maintenance 11 
windows.   12 
2) Class 1 pooling: A scenario where n O-RUs are initially assigned to a single O-DU during a specific period of time, 13 
but the O-RUs can be re-assigned to different O-DU at any point of time via orchestration procedures with the help 14 
of SMO. This automated re -assignment can be triggered outsi de maintenance windows, based on various 15 
performance criterion or for load balancing. Like with Class 0 pooling, the traffic from the O -RU is not split into 16 
subsets that could be assigned to different O-DUs. 17 
3) Class 2 pooling: A scenario where n O -RUs are assigned to m O -DUs, and subsets of traffic from one O -RU are 18 
dynamically distributed (and load-balanced) across the O-DU resources within the O-DU pool using a cluster aware 19 
scheduler.   20

With all classes of O -DU pooling, the total resources required from the shared pool will be less than total resources 24 
required across distributed locations, because the peak of the sum of the traffic will generally be lower than the sum of 25 
the individual cell site traffic peaks. In particular,  Class 1 and Class 2 O -DU pooling enables more aggressive capacity 26
