3 Scope 1 
This document defines the requirements for an Open Xhaul transport infrastructure. As far as possible 2 
it tries to make no assumptions about the underlying transport technology, rather define a set of 3 
requirements about the overall capabilities of an Open Xhaul transport infrastructure that can support 4 
different 5G services, different radio architectures and is multi-service in nature. This document refers 5 
to 5G services, but the transport requirements also apply to O-RAN networks deploying 4G services. 6 
 7 
Following introductory sections 1 through 5, section 6 concentrates on a general description of Open 8 
Xhaul transport and high -level requirements. The next three sections concentrate on the transport 9 
networking requirements needed to support the different radio architectures identified in 5G, namely, 10 
Fronthaul, Midhaul and Backhaul. The aim is to provide the reader specific information about the 11 
transport infrastructure in order to support different radio splits. Section 11 provides requirement for 12 
ease of operability of transport networks. The last two main sections revolve around requirements for 13 
synchronization and legacy services.  14 
 15 
This document uses information published by O -RAN, 3GPP, IEEE, ITU -T, IETF, CableLabs, 16 
NGMN and several other relevant standard bodies and industry associations. It contains educational, 17 
informative, and normative content. 18

6 Open Xhaul Transport infrastructure  1 
This section provides a definition of Open Xhaul transport, some of the key characteristics and 2 
high-level requirements associated with Open Xhaul transport. 3 
6.1 Xhaul transport network details  4 
At the highest -level the Open Xhaul transport network provides the connectivity from the Radio 5 
Access Network (RAN) to the mobile packet core, which subsequently makes the connection to the  6 
application function. These could reside in a public network infrastructure, such as the Interne t, a 7 
walled garden or a completely private network. For details of future developments envisaged in 8 
services / spectrum and timelines please see the â€œInternational Mobile Telecommunications (IMT) 9 
2020 and beyondâ€ [73]. 10 
 11 
Below are some of the key considerations   12 
6.1.1 5G services and vertical markets 13 
 5G networks are expected to enable a new range of services that can be categorized in three 14 
classes: 15 
- enhanced Mobile Broadband (eMBB) 16 
- Ultra Reliable Low Latency Communications (URLLC) 17 
- massive Machine Type Communications (mMTC) 18 
 19 
These services will drive the need for new or enhanced classes of RAN and transport networks. 20 
 21 
6.1.2 Network Scale and capacity  22 
Whereas eMBB services demand more capacity in transport networks, URLLC applications will pose 23 
new challenges for the design of Xhaul networks in terms of low latency and increased reliability . 24 
Finally, mMTC necessitate a network that can efficiently manage huge number of devices such as  25 
intelligent devices in utility meters or house appliances.  26 
6.1.3 5G RAN Architectures 27 
As illustrated in Figure 1, in a traditional D-RAN (distributed RAN) architecture,   the O-DU and O-28 
CU are integrated as a BBU,  collocated with the O-RU (Radio) at cell site.  When evolving to the C-29 
RAN (centralized RA N) archit ecture, the O -CU, which contains the higher layer of the RAN 30 
processing stacks, is centralized in a Hub. Depending on Operatorâ€™s deployment needs, the O -DU 31

6.2 Xhaul Functional Split 27 
The introduction of 5G services necessitates a new look at the distribution of functions between 4G 28 
BBU and RRH (Figure 3). 4G BBU hosts the majority of functional elements with the exception of 29

10.2.3 Estimating cell site loads 12 
Backhaul and midhaul cell site throughput is the sum of all the traffic generated by all UEs using that 13 
site. UE throughput varies depending on the quality of the radio link to the gNB and the amount of 14 
spectrum assigned to it. 5G radios use adaptive modulation to adjust their data rates based on the 15 
radio conditions. In good conditions, where the UE has good signal strength from the radio and there 16 
is little interference, more information c an be carried without errors for each unit of spectrum. This 17 
is in contrast with scenarios where the UE has low signal strength from the radio and interference 18 
levels are high. This is called spectrum efficiency and measured in bits per second, per Hz.  19 
  20 
The highest radio throughput occurs when there is high spectral efficiency and largest spectrum 21 
allocation for UEs. Typically, this occurs at quiet times when there are a small number of active UEs 22 
that is in close proximity to the radio. This is known as the Quiet Time Peak rate.  As the number of 23 
UEs and distribution of UEâ€™s around the cell site increases, so the busy time mean rate is seen. This 24 
is normally significantly lower than the quiet time peak rate and is impacted by interference between 25 
radios, carriers and UEs, along with environmental factors that impact signal strength and interference 26 
such as physical location of the cell site, its surroundings and other sources of interference.    27 
 28 
In the provisioning examples shown in Table 13, busy time mean has been calculated as 30% of quiet 29 
time peak. This is applicable to 4G implementations, however at this time it is not clear whether this 30 
figure is appropriate to 5G  NR. Transport engineers will need to liaise with the radio engineers to 31 
ascertain this figure for 5G environments.       32

11.1.3 Supervision 22 
It is important to ta ke care of operational expenditure dedicated to the medium of the transport and 23 
related equipment. The goal of supervision is to reduce the operational expenditure of the transport 24 
systems, without significantly increasing the capital expenditure by includ ing as much test and 25 
diagnostic capability as possible. Naturally, this should be achieved without compromising the 26 
available bandwidth for services, i.e., test and diagnostics must be non-service affecting. 27 
The ability to reliably differentiate between optical medium and electrical faults at the equipment and 28 
establish if the faults are in the optical medium or in the electronics is a key operator requirement. 29 
Error inference can usually be made from the presence (i.e., power or equipment failure), or absence 30 
(i.e., fibre failure), of the Dying Gasp alarm. Several key points for the supervision can be summarized 31 
as follows: 32 
â€¢ optical medium monitoring/checking: Monitoring and on -demand checking the condition of 33 
optical medium independently from a transport sy stem is important to differentiate optical 34 
medium failures from transport system failures. It is desirable that such monitoring and 35 
checking be available regardless of whether the transport equipment antenna site is in service 36 
or even connected. Several implementations could be proposed.  Optical monitoring solution 37 
is proposed by ITU-T G.697 to detect anomalies, defects, degradation and fault affecting the 38 
quality of the optical transport. Another solution is to use an optical time domain reflectometer 39 
(OTDR) which is a powerful tool for diagnosing such faults in the optical medium . Power 40 
meter and light source can also be used to aid in this monitoring process. Several demarcation 41 
devices are under research for further improving the optical medium monitoring and checking. 42

Annex C: Delay Asymmetry 1 
 2 
This section is not intended to describe a requirement, but rather specify a problem that is important 3
