1.2 Scope 14 
1.2.1  General  15 
The present document specifies the interoperability testing (IOT) for O-DU and O-RU from different vendors connected 16 
using the O-RAN fronthaul (FH) interface. It is noted however that the same content can be utilized for the IOT for O-17 
DU and O-RU from the same vendor connected using the O-RAN FH interface. 18 
A guiding principle defining the tests in this document shall be that any test must exercise the fronthaul interface to a 19 
greater extent than the test prerequisite so that the test is a proper test of some fronthaul functionality or performance . 20 
All tests focus on testing the fronthaul interface, but due to the non-intrusive nature of the tests, system-level aspects of 21 
the O-DU and O-RU are inevitably part of the interoperability tests too.  Because of this, it is recognized that positive 22 
test outcomes indicate successful interoperability, but negative results may not be attributed solely to problems in the 23 
FH interface and additional investigation may be required. 24 
It is also noted that additional test scenarios are required for comprehensive testing of the  FH interface functionality and 25 
performance, which shall be considered in future WG4 test specification versions. 26 
In general, unless otherwise stated, the tests cover LTE (Stand-Alone), NR Non-Stand-Alone (NSA) and NR Stand-27 
Alone (SA). The tests shall be executed using whichever IOT profile(s) are appropriate for the DUTs. See Annex A for 28 
details on the IOT profiles. 29 
1.2.2  Summary of Test Scenarios 30 
The following set of interoperability test cases are defined for the current version of WG4 FH IOT specification. 31

1.5 Introduction 28 
1.5.1 General 29 
O-RAN WG4 has specified and published the O-RAN FH interface specifications (CUS-Plane [2] and M-Plane [3]) 30 
with the objective to enable interoperability of O-DU and O-RU from different vendors. The aim of this document is to 31 
further facilitate such multi-vendor IOT using the O-RAN FH interface by defining standard test configurations, IOT 32 
profiles and interoperability test cases. 33 
It shall be possible to perform interoperability testing in a non-intrusive manner; that is, in a manner in which the 34 
network elements under test are not required to support any functionality or mode of operation beyond that required for 35 
normal operation in a telecommunication network. However, making the endpoints of the FH interface between the O-36 
DU and O-RU visible would require definition of new interfaces which may entail caching and transport of data. 37 
Furthermore, and more importantly, operators require that the total radio system function and perform adequately when 38 
integrating O-DU and O-RU from different vendors. Consequentially, this specification approaches interoperability 39 
testing by means of system level testing. 40

FH interoperability testing by way of system test involves creation of a stimulus in the O-DU to O-RU direction using 1 
an actual or emulated O-CU, potentially with CN support/emulation, and measurement of the result at the output of the 2 
O-RU in the RF domain by an actual or emulated UE together with an RF signal/spectrum analyzer as required. 3 
Likewise, in the reverse direction, the stimulus to probe the FH in the O-RU to O-DU direction is provided by an actual 4 
or emulated UE and is measured at the output of the O-DU by an actual or emulated O-CU with CN support/emulation 5 
as required. 6 
Inasmuch as the interoperability testing by way of system test involves configuring and collecting results from the O -7 
RU it shall be effected by means of the M-Plane FH interface. For elements that fall outside of this scope, such as the 8 
O-DU, the configuration and collection of data and status information for testing purposes may be accomplished by 9 
NMS. 10 
Interoperability involves testing the FH interface in terms of M-Plane, S-Plane, C-Plane and U-Plane. Some aspects of 11 
these planes may be tested independently. However, some tests, such as those that require the devices to be brought into 12 
service and a call established entail simultaneous activity across multiple planes. 13 
1.5.2 Fronthaul M-Plane Architectural options 14 
As described in the “O-RAN WG4 Management Plane Specification” [3], two architectural models are supported, 15 
namely a “hierarchical” and a “hybrid” model. As a general guideline the following apply, 16 
1. Hierarchical model. The O-RU is entirely managed by one O-DU (sudo) using a NETCONF based M-Plane 17 
interface. 18 
2. Hybrid model:  The architecture allows one or more direct logical interface(s) between management system(s) 19 
(performing different roles, nms, fm-pm, swm, etc) and O-RU in addition to a logical interface between O-DU 20 
(sudo role) and the O-RU. 21 
1.5.3 Fronthaul Synchronization options  22 
Various synchronization options have been defined in the O-RAN WG4 CUS-Plane Specification [2] (LLS-C1, LLS-23 
C2, LLS-C3 and LLS-C4). Depending on the specific O-RAN deployment being considered, not all of them might be 24 
relevant. When testing the S-Plane, the System Integrator shall identify which of the test cases are relevant depending 25 
on the specific deployment scenarios addressed. As a general guideline the foll owing applies: 26 
1. Direct connection between O-DU and O-RU:  27 
LLS-C1 is generally the main sync option to be validated  28 
LLS-C4 may be considered as an alternative or as a complement to LLS-C1 29 
2. Bridged network between O-DU and O-RU 30 
LLS-C2 for cases where the synchronization is delivered to the O-RU via the O-DU and over the bridged 31 
network. In this case the PRTC/PRC may be embedded in the O-DU or may be located anywhere in the 32 
network (connected via backhaul or FH transport). 33 
LLS-C3 for cases when the synchronization is distributed to the O-RU without involving the O-DU In this case 34 
the PRTC/PRC may be located anywhere in the network (connected via backhaul or FH transport) and may 35 
also be co-located with the O-DU. 36 
LLS-C4 may be considered as alternative or as a complement to LLS-C2/LLS-C3. LLS-C4 is considered for 37

2.1.1  Standard Test Configurations 3 
Interoperability testing is performed to prove that the end-to-end functionality between the O-DU and O-RU is as 4 
required by the O-RAN FH CUS-Plane [2] and M-Plane [3] specifications on which these components are based. This 5 
requires system level testing with O-DU and O-RU as an integrated system. 6 
2.1.1.1  Device Under Test (DUT) 7 
The case where the O-DU and O-RU are provided by different vendors is the focus of this document, but the case where 8 
both are from the same vendor is also valid. 9 
The O-DU and O-RU, with their interconnecting FH, is considered as the DUT. This is the same whether the O-CU and 10 
O-DU are implemented as a combined node, or as stand-alone nodes.  11 
For simplicity and without prejudice the tests in the following sections are defined with reference to a stand-alone O-12 
DU. However, the tests apply equally when the O-DU is replaced by the O-DU functionality of a combined O-CU/O-13 
DU and where the Layer 2 and Layer 3 radio processing on the network side is provided by the O-CU functionality of 14 
the combined-node. Any differences to the test due to replacement of the stand-alone O-DU with a combined O-CU/O-15 
DU are detailed as they arise. 16 
The simplest test configuration involves a single O-DU and a single O-RU. In this configuration, the O-DU and O-RU 17 
are labelled as DUT1(O-DU) and DUT1(O-RU) respectively.  18 
More advanced test configurations will involve defining the cardinality between the 1…M O-DU(s) and 1…N O-RU(s) 19 
as part of the test scenario which will determine the configuration required.  20 
An example of such test configuration is to have a single O-DU connected to multiple N O-RUs. In this example, the O-21 
DU is labelled as DUT1(O-DU) and the O-RU(s) are labelled as DUT1(O-RU) ... DUTN(O-RU) accordingly.  22 
A second example is to have multiple M O-DUs connected to multiple N O-RUs. In this second example, the O-DUs 23 
are labelled as DUT1(O-DU) … DUTM(O-DU) and the O-RU(s) are labelled as DUT1(O-RU) ... DUTN(O-RU) 24 
accordingly. However, deployment scenarios where a single O-RU is managed and used by more than one O-DU is not 25 
addressed in this version of the WG4 FH IOT Specification. 26 
External physical and or logical connection between the O-DUs and O-RUs are to be specified in the IOT profiles (eg. 27 
number of 10/25/40GE, physical medium eg. fiber), see Annex A for details. 28 
IOT test cases defined in the following sections of this specification shall be executed using whichever IOT profile(s) 29 
that are appropriate for the DUTs. See Annex A for more details on the IOT profiles.  30
