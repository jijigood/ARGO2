1.3.1 Definitions 7
NF Deployment: An O-Cloud NF Deployment is a deployment of a cloud native Network Function (all or 8
partial), resources shared within a NF Function, or resources shared across network 9
functions. The NF Deployment configures and assembles user -plane resources required 10
for the cloud native construct used to establish the NF Deployment and manage its life 11
cycle from creation to deletion. 12
NF Deployment Descriptor: A completed data model which provides an O-Cloud the necessary information to create 13
a deployment.  14
Deployment ID: Correlation Identity created by the O -Cloud for the SMO to relate to its inventory and 15
manage. 16
Deployment Management Services (DMS):  17
 The DMS are the logical services provided by the O -Cloud providing for managing the 18
life cycle of deployments which use cloud resources.  19
Federated O-Cloud Orchestration and Management (FOCOM): 20
 The SMO treats the collection of O-Clouds as a single federated cloud. The FOCOM are 21
the logical services provided by the SMO to manage the distribution of O-Cloud software 22
and provides orchestration for O-Cloud life cycle processes. 23
Infrastructure Management Services (IMS): 24
 The IMS are the logical services provided by the O -Cloud which provides the interface 25
to orchestrate O-Cloud life cycle processes with the network functions it may be hosting 26
and other operational procedures. 27
Network Function Orchestration (NFO): 28
 The NFO are the logical services of the S MO which coordinates between the O -Cloud 29
for managing deployment life cycle events, open loop, and closed loop operational 30
procedures. 31

Chapter 2. General Aspects 1
2.1 Introduction to O2 2
The O2 interface is an open logical interface within O -RAN architecture providing secured communication between the 3
SMO and O-Cloud. It enables the management of O-Cloud infrastructures and the deployment life cycle management of 4
O-RAN cloudified NFs that run on O -Cloud. The O2 interface is defined in an extensible way that enables new 5
information or functions to be added without necessarily needing to change the protocol or the procedures. This interface 6
enables a multi-vendor environment and is independent of specific implementations of the SMO and O -Cloud. 7
2.2 Alignment with O-RAN Architecture 8
This section provides any added detail not found in the high- level O-RAN architecture documents which define and 9
describe the O2 interface. The intent is to extend knowledge and not to redefine existing information. Therefore, if a 10
conflict exists between this document and the O-RAN Architecture Description [2] or the O-RAN OAM Architecture [3] 11
the information presented will not supersede those documents. 12
2.2.1 Alignment with Overall Architecture 13
This document adopts the architecture as described in the O -RAN Architecture Description [2]. The O2 is described as 14
the interface between the SMO and the O-Cloud to provide platform resources and workload management. 15
The O-Cloud needs to be created before the SMO can interface with it.  16
O-Cloud can consist of multiple Deployment Management Services. Each DMS can manage leased resources from 17
multiple resource pools and span multiple locations. There is a single IMS for O -Cloud that manages all resources of  18
DMSes as well as resources that are not allocated to any DMS in the O -Cloud. Diagram below depicts relationship 19
between SMO and components of O-Cloud. 20

resource to a DMS.  24

• SOL005 standard is used for NS/PNF/VNF Package Management, LCM and Monitoring  8
• SOL006 standard is used to describe VNF, PNF, and NS in YANG 9
• SOL002 standard is used for VNF/VNFC -level EM triggered scenarios (LCM, Fault,  Performance, 10

• ETSI Package and communication security are supported / API security are supported (SEC022 GS)  12
• IFA 029 Enhancements of the NFV architecture towards "Cloud-native" and "PaaS” 13
• IFA040 CISM OS container compute management service interface requirements (ongoing) 14
• ETSI IFA 005/006 covers topics Software Image Management, Virtual Resource Management (Information, 15

Resource Fault Management and NFP 17
The O2 interfaces will generally follow ETSI GS NFV-SOL 013 [11] section 8 for interface security. Further refinements 18
will be based on recommendations from the STG and are FFS. 19
Other sections of SOL 13 [11] are still under review and are therefore FFS. 20
NFV SOL-015 [12] provides RESTful Patterns. Section 5.9 defines the Subscribe -Notify pattern. Generally, this pattern 21
is adopted by the O2, with the following caveats. 22
• Notifications should indicate success or failure of the receipt of the notification to the notifying function.  23
• The subscribe and POST may not be in the same service API. The typical pattern for the O2 is that the subscribe, 24
or its equivalent, is in the O -Cloud services and the post for the notification is an SMO service. There are no 25
existing use cases of notifications from the SMO to the O-Cloud. 26
• The event types may be extended over time. Therefore, the O -Cloud services should provide the ability to 27
discover what event types the cloud instance supports. The minimal list of supported event types SHALL be:  28
o Inventory Change 29
o Configuration Change 30
o Fault Events 31
o Performance Reporting 32
o Heartbeat 33
Another form of notification is the callback mechanism where the notify function is identified in the request. This 34
mechanism is used in platform the software update procedure, as the cloud may not yet be configured for notifications 35
when the update occurs. It is expected that all subscriptions and identified callbacks are per sisted through a restart or a 36
software update. 37

This section describes the general approach to solving common problems with managing a cloud and the deployments 2
which execute on it. 3

3.3 O-Cloud Monitoring Service 10
When the O -Cloud Infrastructure or the ORAN cloudified NFs fails, it needs to be fixed immediately, and preferably 11
automatically, to prevent end users from experiencing service disruptions. To avoid this service disruption Network 12
Operations must consider the telemetry information of O -Cloud deployments in the network. The telemetry informat ion 13
serves as a vital resource for analysing the O-Cloud’s state and health, and for delivering on service monitoring goals. 14
The O-Cloud Monitoring Service uses telemetry data to provide monitoring of O -Cloud infrastructures. The telemetry 15
data is provided by Deployments and Infrastructure and is correlated to reduce duplication of effort in determining the 16
root cause in the network.  17
There are different types of telemetry, Managed Element Telemetry, Deployment Telemetry, and Infrastructure 18
Telemetry. 19
1. For Managed Element Telemetry, the objective is to monitor the application behaviour. This will be addressed 20
in the O1 Specification [4]. 21
2. For Deployment Telemetry, the objective is to monitor the number of deployment instances an O -Cloud has at 22
that moment and how many were expected, how the on- progress deployment is going, and health checks. 23
Additional Deployment Telemetry metrics like CPU, network, and memory usage can also be collected.  This 24
will be addressed in the O2 Specification. 25
3. For Infrastructure Telemetry, the objective is to monitor the health of the O -Cloud Infrastructure components. 26
Network Operations are interested in discovering if all the components in the O-Cloud Infrastructure are working 27
properly and at what capacity, how many deployments are running on each node, and the resource utilization of 28
the O-Cloud Infrastructure. This will be addressed in the O2 Specification. 29

3.4.1 Affinity, Anti-Affinity, Quorum Diversity Rules 22
An Affinity rule specifies that deployments with the same rule applied must be collocated withi n the same scope. This 23
can be applied to a single server or a server group.  Anti-Affinity is the opposite of the Affinity Rule in that deployments 24
with the same rule applied cannot be collocated within the same scope. Affinity and Anti-Affinity rules can be applicable 25
to entities outside of the current deployment such as Tenant or Namespace. 26
A Quorum diversity rule stipulates that deployments with this rule can be collocated if less than 50% of the deployments 27
exist within the same scope. This rule can only be enforced when there is a minimum of 3 deployments. In which it will 28
be the same deployment assignment as Anti-Affinity. In scenarios with more than 3 deployments a minimum of 3 different 29
resource assignments at the specified scope (Server or Server Gro up) will be used. More can be used at the discretion of 30
the O-Cloud Resource Scheduler. 31

3.4.3.3 Resource Views 15
A Resource View is defined as the visible and/or accessible part of the Resource that an actor sees based on their 16
authority. Resources are seen by different actors that need to manage or act upon resources. Different perspectives from 17
these actors are “views” of the Resources. O-Clouds can have multiple Resource Views to enable separation of 18
authority and operations of the resources, into logical groups of actors (generally other SW entities). The four kinds of 19
O-Cloud resource views are: Cloud Infrastructure Resource, Abstracted Resources, Assigned Resource, and Consumed 20
Resource. This is done to provide maximum separation of concern between different actors. These views accommodate 21
public, external, and shared private cloud deployments.  22
For example, Consider a hotel room as a resource. The front desk staff who assigns a room to a tenant has a relationship 23
to the room and job requirements of how to manage the room. That is, they have a “view” of the room. The tenant that 24
checks into the room has a different “resource view” of that same room. The cleaning staff has yet another relationship, 25
or “resource view”, to the same hotel room. 26

3.5.2.1 Fault Information Model 1
A fault information model is expected to be developed in the O2ims information model. As a general concept, an 2
information model provides a common lexicon, terminology, and model to serve as a basis for coordination between a 3
group of actors. It facilitates communications between groups and aligns their contributions. The fault information 4
model is applicable for the resources & resource pools as defined in the infrastructure information model.  5
3.5.2.2 Subscription  6
It is expected that there will be a subscription mechanism between a client an d an agent. The client would pass an 7
endpoint where notifications should be sent to. It is expected that multiple clients can be supported.  Procedures are 8
expected to be defined in the Fault O2 specifications for IMS and DMS. Subscriptions originate from t he client (e.g. 9
SMO).  10
For example, the concept of a subscription allows a client, such as the SMO, to register for call -backs to the O-Cloud to 11
receive fault notifications. 12
3.5.2.3 Notifications  13
Fault notifications originate from the O-cloud infrastructure. There is a relationship between the subscription and 14
notification. When a condition occurs within the IMS that warrants a fault notification an event is issued towards clients 15
that have subscribed to receive such a type of notification or event.  16
For example, a user could receive alerts on a fault dashboard GUI based on cloud IMS fault notifications. 17
3.5.2.4 Parallel Reporting 18
A fault occurrence on an O-Cloud resource should be reported through IMS. DMS resource faults are associated with a 19
workload. It is possible that multiple resources can associate with a workload, whereby one fault might trigger a fault 20
notification on IMS, DMS and O1. Due to the nature of the subscribe/notify mechanism for fault reporting, it is possible 21
to: (1) have multiple publishers sending to a single consumer and (2) conversely a single publisher that can send to 22
multiple consumers. The interfaces for subscription & notification of fault reporting are: O2ims , O2dms, O1. 23
For example, it is conceivable that a vDU application might report a fault on O1, and a related physical server allocated 24
to a workload of a vDU would raise a DMS fault which might also have an infrastructure fault raised on IMS.  25
3.5.2.5 Simple Devices  26
Collectively, in the O-Cloud it is conceivable that many devices are simple devices that do not have a full suite of OAM 27
S/W.  28
For example, suppose a network interface card (NIC) is a resource that is a simple device, and if that NIC encounters a 29

3.5.2.6 Source of Truth  33
In an O-Cloud IMS context, it is expected that the “source of truth” will be the resources of the O-Cloud itself. When a 34
fault which has been reported to a client, such as the SMO, the fault data is replicated within the fault notification to 35
provide details and information of the fault. Thus, the fault information now exists in multiple places both in the IMS 36
and the entity receiving the fault notification. Which element then has the “true” information should the network or 37
elements be disconnected or faulty? After connectivity returns, how would the SMO reconcile its view of the existing 38
fault against the current faults on the O-Cloud resource? The answer is to query the source of truth (O-Cloud resource) 39
for the most current faults.  40
For example, if a resource in the O-Cloud goes out of service, and it had previously sent a fault notification, and then 41
later returns to service, potentially the information at the client and the O-Cloud resource may have different fault 42
information. But the O-Cloud resource is the source of truth and it is expected that client (IMS) would query the O -43
Cloud resource for the current information and reconcile that with client data. 44

3.5.2.10 Fault Logging  23
Fault logging may be part of the general event logging and is a concept where faults are stored at the O- Cloud resource 24
(the source of truth) for a configured amount of time. IMS provisioning should be able to configure the amount of time 25
that logs stored for. The concept of fault logging and storing faults allows a client, algorithm, or user to query for the 26
fault log and analyze those faults. The faults should be timestamped with a common time format and clock source. 27
Faults that are intermittent, repetitive, or transient can be identified this way. For example, Deb ug traces used by some 28
O-cloud providers might be a way to facilitate fault logging meaning that certain condition can trigger writing to the 29
fault log. 30
For example, fault from an O-Cloud resource could be stored in a log for 1 week.  31
3.5.2.11 Alarm Synchronization 32
There are many potential situations where a client and source of truth might become disconnected or disrupted. In this 33
case, the client will need to synchronize with the source of truth after it is able to reconnect.  34
For example, network fault might prevent communication between the SMO (client) and IMS resources in the O -Cloud. 35
By the time connectivity has been restored, many new alarm conditions might have arisen and logged. This would 36
motivate the SMO to resynchronize to get the current alarms again. A variety of mechanisms might be used to trigger 37
alarm resynchronization. It might also trigger an upload of the most recent fault logs.  38

3.5.2.14 Fault Domains  12
A fault domain is a “unit of failure”. A fault domain associates a collection of IMS O-Cloud resources together. We 13
expect a fault domain to be within a Data Center Zone (see the provisioning chapter basic concepts for more detail). 14
Faults could be associated with a fault domain. In the context of IMS fault a rea, fault domains are an attribute that could 15
be correlative but do not have intrinsic fault operations, they are not a manageable entity.  16
This would allow operators to design systems that are fault tolerant across fault domains. This might also guide 17
placement strategies, and update cycles to improve reliability and availability.  18
For example, a network cloud can host some number of servers per rack (e.g. 40 servers) with a number of racks of 19
equipment (e.g. 10 racks) in a single deployment (totaling 400 servers). A fault domain might be defined to be rack. If a 20
fault occurred, this concept would allow an operator trying to isolate the problem down to a rack (the fault domain). It 21
would also allow a user to correlate a problem if multiple racks served by a common infrastructure resource. 22

operations to be performed on the collected data and statistics to be built over time. This can tell an operator or system 26

3.5.3.1 O- Cloud Performance Measurements 7
Measurements are defined as the act of quantitatively checking the O-Cloud resources for defined gauges of operation. 8

3.5.3.2 Performance Indicators 12
Performance indicators take execution measurements and use them in a way that can give a meaningful notion of how a 13
system is utilizing its resources and/or how well a system is operating. Indicators might be local in the sense of a 14
function or more broader in scope for a group of functions. Performance indicators are typically a formula -driven 15
