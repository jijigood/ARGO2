1.2 Scope 14 
1.2.1  General  15 
The present document specifies the interoperability testing (IOT) for O-DU and O-RU from different vendors connected 16 
using the O-RAN fronthaul (FH) interface. It is noted however that the same content can be utilized for the IOT for O-17 
DU and O-RU from the same vendor connected using the O-RAN FH interface. 18 
A guiding principle defining the tests in this document shall be that any test must exercise the fronthaul interface to a 19 
greater extent than the test prerequisite so that the test is a proper test of some fronthaul functionality or performance. 20 
All tests focus on testing the fronthaul interface, but due to the non-intrusive nature of the tests, system-level aspects of 21 
the O-DU and O-RU are inevitably part of the interoperability tests too. Because of this, it is recognized that positive 22 
test outcomes indicate successful interoperability, but negative results may not be attributed solely to problems in the 23 
FH interface and additional investigation may be required. 24 
It is also noted that additional test scenarios are required for comprehensive testing of the FH interface functionality and 25 
performance, which shall be considered in future WG4 test specification versions. 26 
In general, unless otherwise stated, the tests cover LTE (Stand-Alone), NR Non-Stand-Alone (NSA) and NR Stand-27 
Alone (SA). The tests shall be executed using whichever IOT profile(s) are appropriate for the DUTs. See Annex A for 28 
details on the IOT profiles. 29

1.5 Introduction 27 
1.5.1 General 28 
O-RAN WG4 has specified and published the O-RAN FH interface specifications (CUS-plane [2] and M-plane [3]) 29 
with the objective to enable interoperability of O-DU and O-RU from different vendors. The aim of this document is to 30 
further facilitate such multi-vendor IOT using the O-RAN FH interface by defining standard test configurations, IOT 31 
profiles and interoperability test cases. 32 
It shall be possible to perform interoperability testing in a non-intrusive manner; that is, in a manner in which the 33 
network elements under test are not required to support any functionality or mode of operation beyond that required for 34 
normal operation in a telecommunication network. However, making the endpoints of the FH interface between the O-35 
DU and O-RU visible would require definition of new interfaces which may entail caching and transport of data. 36 
Furthermore, and more importantly, operators require that the total radio system function and perform adequately when 37 
integrating O-DU and O-RU from different vendors. Consequentially, this specification approaches interoperability 38 
testing by means of system level testing. 39 
FH interoperability testing by way of system test involves creation of a stimulus in the O-DU to O-RU direction using 40 
an actual or emulated O-CU, potentially with CN support/emulation, and measurement of the result at the output of the 41 
O-RU in the RF domain by an actual or emulated UE together with an RF signal/spectrum analyzer as required. 42

O-DU, the configuration and collection of data and status information for testing purposes may be accomplished by 6 
NMS. 7 
Interoperability involves testing the FH interface in terms of M-Plane, S-Plane, C-Plane and U-Plane. Some aspects of 8 
these planes may be tested independently. However, some tests, such as those that require the devices to be brought into 9 
service and a call established entail simultaneous activity across multiple planes. 10 
1.5.2 Fronthaul M-plane Architectural options 11 
As described in the “O-RAN WG4 Management Plane Specification” [3], two architectural models are supported, 12 
namely a “hierarchical” and a “hybrid” model. As a general guideline the following apply, 13 
1. Hierarchical model. The O-RU is entirely managed by one O-DU (sudo) using a NETCONF based M-Plane 14 
interface. 15 
2. Hybrid model:  The architecture allows one or more direct logical interface(s) between management system(s) 16 
(performing different roles, nms, fm-pm, swm, etc) and O-RU in addition to a logical interface between O-DU 17 
(sudo role) and the O-RU. 18 
In the current version of WG4 FH IOT Specification, only the Hierarchical model is addressed. The hybrid model is 19 
considered for future versions of the WG4 FH IOT Specification. 20 
1.5.3 Fronthaul Synchronization options  21 
Various synchronization options have been defined in the O-RAN WG4 CUS-Plane Specification [2] (LLS-C1, LLS-22 
C2, LLS-C3 and LLS-C4). Depending on the specific O-RAN deployment being considered, not all of them might be 23 
relevant. When testing the S-Plane, the System Integrator shall identify which of the test cases are relevant depending 24 
on the specific deployment scenarios addressed. As a general guideline the following applies: 25 
1. Direct connection between O-DU and O-RU:  26 
LLS-C1 is generally the main sync option to be validated  27 
LLS-C4 may be considered as an alternative or as a complement to LLS-C1 28 
2. Bridged network between O-DU and O-RU 29 
LLS-C2 for cases where the synchronization is delivered to the O-RU via the O-DU and over the bridged 30 
network. In this case the PRTC/PRC may be embedded in the O-DU or may be located anywhere in the 31 
network (connected via backhaul or FH transport). 32 
LLS-C3 for cases when the synchronization is distributed to the O-RU without involving the O-DU In this case 33 
the PRTC/PRC may be located anywhere in the network (connected via backhaul or FH transport) and may 34 
also be co-located with the O-DU. 35 
LLS-C4 may be considered as alternative or as a complement to LLS-C2/LLS-C3. LLS-C4 is considered for 36 
future versions of the specification. 37 
The FH focused tests for S-plane for the current version of this specification covers LLS-C1, LLS-C2 and LLS-C3 38 
using the ITU-T G.8275.1 [5] profile (Full Timing Support). FH focused tests for S-Plane for LLS-C1, LLS-C2 and 39 
LLS-C3 using the ITU-T G.8275.2 [6] profile (Partial Timing Support), and LLS-C4 are for future study. 40

Chapter 2 Interoperability Measurements 1 
2.1  Interoperability Standard Test Definitions 2 
2.1.1  Standard Test Configurations 3 
Interoperability testing is performed to prove that the end-to-end functionality between the O-DU and O-RU is as 4 
required by the O-RAN FH CUS-Plane [2] and M-Plane [3] specifications on which these components are based. This 5 
requires system level testing with O-DU and O-RU as an integrated system. 6 
2.1.1.1  Device Under Test (DUT) 7 
The case where the O-DU and O-RU are provided by different vendors is the focus of this document, but the case where 8 
both are from the same vendor is also valid. 9 
The O-DU and O-RU, with their interconnecting FH, is considered as the DUT. This is the same whether the O-CU and 10 
O-DU are implemented as a combined node, or as stand-alone nodes.  11 
For simplicity and without prejudice the tests in the following sections are defined with reference to a stand-alone O-12
